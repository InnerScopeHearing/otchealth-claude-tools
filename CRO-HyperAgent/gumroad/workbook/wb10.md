## Chapter 10 Workbook — When the Family Is in the Room

**The takeaway:** The family member in the room is not an obstacle — they are often the most powerful voice in the decision. When you bring a loved one into the demonstration rather than managing them from the sideline, you give the whole family a shared experience that no clinical chart can replicate. The moment a spouse or adult child witnesses the shift firsthand, the room changes — and so does the outcome.

### 3 — The Drill: Build Your Loved-One Demo Script

Kim's method: seat the patient, have them close their eyes and turn their back to the door. Take the loved one just outside — close enough for their voice to carry through — and ask them to speak naturally. Normal questions. The kind a family asks every day. Then watch.

Write your own version of the setup instructions you will say to the family member before the demo begins. Use the blanks below.

**My setup line to the family member:**

"Before we get into the paperwork, I'd like to show you something. I'm going to ask __________ [patient's name] to close their eyes and turn their back. I'd like you to step just outside this door and ask them a few normal questions — things like __________

__________ and __________. Just a regular conversation, the kind you'd have at home. Ready?"

**My transition line after the demo — when the family member has witnessed it:**

"What you just saw — __________ answering you without straining, without asking you to repeat — that's __________

__________."

**Model line from the manuscript (Kim, Ch. 10):**

> "When that patient answered back and kept on talking to their loved one, they had a smile on their face. I knew right then that I had that loved one understanding what a hearing aid can do."

Practice this setup with a colleague before your next appointment where a family member is expected. The script should feel natural — not read.
