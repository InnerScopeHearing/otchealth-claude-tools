# Chapter 5: You Are the Business

*(Kim)*

> "I learned that I am the business — I am representing the company that I'm working for, and you need to understand that that is important."

---

Before I ever worked a single day in a hearing aid office, I worked at a chiropractor's office. The detail that stayed with me from that job is so simple it almost sounds trivial: there was a mirror mounted above the phone. You were supposed to look at yourself in that mirror before you answered a call. Not to fix your hair. Not to check your lipstick. You looked at yourself to make sure you were smiling — because when you smile, the person on the other end of the line can hear it.

I never forgot that mirror. When I moved into the hearing aid world, there was no mirror above the front desk phone. But I put one there in my mind, and I have never answered a phone without it.

That is the whole philosophy of the front desk in five seconds: the person calling cannot see you. They can only hear you. And what they hear — your energy, your warmth, your genuine pleasure that they called — is the first thing that will determine whether they make an appointment or hang up and try somewhere else. No one thinks they need a hearing aid. Everyone who calls our office is calling from a place of some hesitation, some embarrassment, maybe a little fear. The voice they hear on the other end of the line is either going to ease that fear or confirm it.

I chose, every single time, to ease it.

---

I grew up watching the front desk done right. My father's receptionist — a woman named Marilyn — was the person who shaped everything I became in this business. I was thirteen years old, spending my first summer working with my dad, and I was just supposed to watch and help where I could. But what I actually did was study Marilyn. The way she said hello to every person who walked through the door. The way she remembered names, not just the patient's name but the names of their spouse, their dog, their grandchildren who had just graduated. The way she made everyone feel, from the moment they stepped inside, that this was a place they were welcomed and not just processed.

The customers just loved her. And I found myself loving her approach.

What I understood, even at thirteen, was that Marilyn was not just a receptionist. Marilyn was the first impression of every single transaction that happened in that office. If someone walked out having bought a hearing aid and feeling good about it, some part of that decision was made the moment Marilyn said hello. And if someone walked out without buying, and they came back three months later — some part of that return visit was because Marilyn had made them feel safe enough to try again.

I took those lessons with me into every office I ever worked in.

---

Here is what I believe with every fiber of my professional being: the selling process begins the moment the front desk person answers the phone. Not when the dispenser starts the audiogram. Not when the patient sits down in the testing booth. The moment the phone rings and someone picks it up — that is when it starts.

And that means the front desk person is not a support role. The front desk person is a selling role, whether they think of themselves that way or not. Every nervous person who calls, every spouse who is trying to get their husband in the door, every adult child who is worried about their aging parent — they are all making a judgment in the first thirty seconds of that phone call about whether this is a place they can trust. You are either building that trust or you are not. There is no neutral.

I tried to teach this to every receptionist we ever hired, across all the offices we ran over the years. Some understood it immediately. Some never quite got there. But the ones who got it — you could measure the difference in patient volume. You could see it in who came back. You could hear it in the way patients talked about the office when they were in the chair.

When I worked the front desk, I was not sitting there waiting for things to happen. I was making things happen. I was listening for the hesitation in a caller's voice and adjusting my tone. I was noticing that a patient in the waiting room had been sitting quietly for too long and going over to ask if they needed anything. I was making sure the coffee was hot, the cookies were fresh, the office was warm and tidy and felt like a place where someone who was a little afraid might feel comfortable enough to stay.

Clean bathrooms. I cannot tell you how many times I talked to staff about clean bathrooms. It sounds like the least glamorous part of running a practice, but it matters. When a patient walks into your restroom and it looks like a gas station, they have learned something about how much you value them. When they walk in and it is clean and neat and there are things that say we thought about you, they have learned something else. Everything communicates. Everything.

---

One thing I learned from years at that front desk is that the people who come into a hearing aid office are often dealing with something much bigger than a hearing aid. Many of them are lonely. Many of them live alone, or live with a spouse who is also aging and struggling. Many of them have not had a real conversation with someone outside their household in days. For some of our patients, especially in our earlier offices in California, we were the only people they saw that week. Not the only healthcare providers — the only people.

That changes how you answer the phone. It changes how you say hello when they come through the door. It changes everything, if you let it.

I always felt empowered to help them — not just to check them in or hand them a clipboard, but to actually be present with them. To look them in the eye. To ask a question and listen to the answer. To make them feel, in the truest sense, important. Because they were.

My dad used to say: "Everyone puts their pants on the same way as everyone else. No matter what walk of life they come from, everyone deserves kindness and respect." That lesson is the foundation of every good front desk interaction I have ever had or witnessed. The professor and the postal worker both sit in the same waiting room chair. They both deserve the same warmth. They both came in with some version of the same fear. Your job is to meet them where they are, every single time.

---

The last thing I will say about the front desk is this: I could step back into it today and still do it. Not because I have a script or a system — though I do have those — but because I genuinely love it. I love the first moment of contact. I love the challenge of making a nervous person feel at ease in sixty seconds. I love knowing that what I did in those first few minutes shaped everything that happened in the room after.

The front desk is not where you wait for patients to arrive. It is where the practice either wins or loses them, one phone call and one hello at a time. If you are working that desk and you think of yourself as support staff, I want you to rethink that. You are not support. You are the front line. You are the reason they stay.

You are the business.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

Kim has always said the front desk controls the environment and the outcome. I believe that. The first impression sets the trust, and trust is everything in this business — because nobody in that waiting room wants to be there. They came in worried, they came in embarrassed, and they came in because something made them finally pick up the phone. The job of the front desk is to make sure they are glad they did. The job of the dispenser begins from a position of advantage if the front desk has done its work. Kim's warmth in that room was not a soft skill. It was a business strategy, and it worked every single time.

---

**The principle**

The sale begins at first contact — on the phone, at the door, in the waiting room. The front desk does not support the selling process; it is the first act of it. Every patient who feels welcomed, valued, and at ease before they ever see a dispenser is a patient who is already one step closer to saying yes to better hearing.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Kim's mirror-above-the-phone philosophy translates directly into how AI voice and messaging tools should be configured for a hearing care practice. An AI front desk or automated intake line must be scripted and tested to pass a simple question: does it sound warm, unhurried, and genuinely glad the caller called? The tone, pacing, and opening phrase of any AI touchpoint — text message, phone bot, appointment reminder — can carry the smile you can hear. That is not cosmetic. It is the spec.

---

**For the Closer**

The front desk is the first close. Train your receptionist the way you would train a dispenser — because every patient who walks through the door already warmed up is a patient your dispenser does not have to win from zero.

**For the Professional**

Your front desk role is not administrative — it is relational. You hold the environment. You set the tone. The patient's trust begins with you, and the entire journey to better hearing starts with whether you made them glad they called.
