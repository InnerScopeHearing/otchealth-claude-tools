# Chapter 16: Hiring for Heart

*(Kim)*

---

> "What was your relationship with your parents and grandparents? The answer would tell me a lot about how they would work in the hearing aid world."
> — Kim Moore

---

Running a practice across multiple offices means you are almost never the person standing at the front desk when a patient walks in. You are in the back, in the testing booth, on the phone with a supplier, or driving between locations. The person a patient sees first — the one who hands them a clipboard, brings them coffee, and says "We're so glad you came in today" — that is whoever you hired. And if you hired wrong, no amount of skill on your part fixes what happens before you ever enter the room.

I learned this lesson the slow way, through years of watching staff members who were perfectly competent on paper treat patients in ways that made me wince. They answered the phone with a flat voice. They pointed at the sign-in sheet without looking up. They were polite enough, I suppose, but there was no warmth there, no genuine interest in the person who had just walked through the door. Patients felt it. You can always feel it when someone is just doing a job versus when they actually care.

So over the years, I started asking a question in interviews that I am certain surprised a lot of people. It had nothing to do with whether they had worked in healthcare before, whether they knew how to operate a scheduling system, or whether they could handle a busy phone. All of that can be taught. Instead, I would ask: **What was your relationship with your parents and grandparents? Or maybe you had older friends who played that kind of role in your life?**

Then I would sit and listen to the answer.

If they lit up — if they told me how they used to drive their grandmother to appointments, or how Sunday dinners at their grandparents' house were the best part of the week, or how they still called their mom every day just to check in — I knew something important. I knew they had been shaped around older people. They understood that this age group moves a little differently, tells longer stories, and needs a little extra patience. They would be comfortable sitting with a 78-year-old man who wanted to tell them about his time in Korea before he let you put him in the testing booth. They would not rush him.

If, on the other hand, the answer was cold — if they mentioned estrangement, indifference, or simply had no real memories of time spent with older family members — I knew they were not suited for this work. Not because they were bad people. But because kindness toward the elderly is something you have to have absorbed somewhere along the way. You cannot train it from scratch in a three-day orientation.

I know it sounds simple. But that one question told me more than a full page of references ever could.

---

The other thing I cared about — and I will not apologize for how particular I was about this — was the physical environment of every office. I wanted clean bathrooms. Not just tidy, but genuinely clean. I wanted the lobby vacuumed. I wanted fresh coffee every morning, cookies on the counter, and water available for anyone who wanted it. We had tea. We had donuts on the good mornings. In our first office in Woodland, people would come in just to sit in the lobby and have a cup of coffee, even on days they had no appointment. That is what I wanted our offices to feel like: a place people wanted to be.

Now, you might wonder what bathroom cleanliness has to do with hearing aids. Everything. When a patient comes into your office for the first time, they are already nervous. They have spent months, maybe years, quietly struggling — asking people to repeat themselves, missing punchlines, turning the TV up until their family complained. Finally coming in to do something about it takes courage. The last thing that patient should encounter is a waiting room that feels like nobody cares, or a restroom that looks like it belongs to a gas station on a highway. That environment says something about how you run everything else in your business. Patients read it immediately, even if they never consciously articulate it.

I would never ask any staff member to do something I was not willing to do myself. If the bathroom needed cleaning before the first patient arrived, I cleaned it. If the coffee had run out, I made a new pot. If the carpet needed vacuuming between appointments, I vacuumed it. I felt it was important for the team to see that no job in the office was beneath the person who owned it. That attitude sets the standard. People rise to what you model for them.

What I found, over and over again, is that the staff members who thrived in our offices were the ones who had the same instinct — who felt genuinely uncomfortable when something was out of order, who straightened the magazines without being asked and greeted patients by name when they came back for a follow-up. They owned the environment the same way I did. That ownership did not come from a policy manual. It came from who they were as people.

---

Patients are your lifeline. I said that to every new hire, and I meant it with every word. You do not get many chances to earn a patient's trust, and you can lose it in a single interaction. It takes months of marketing, mailers, phone calls, and follow-ups to get one person through the door. It takes one dismissive moment at the front desk to make sure they never come back and never recommend you to anyone they know.

If you can treat people with kindness, they will come back. They will tell their friends. They will bring their spouse in six months later, and their neighbor the following year, and their brother who lives two hours away but makes the drive because they insisted. That is how a practice grows — not through gimmicks, but through patients who feel genuinely cared for and want to pass that experience on.

If you cannot treat people with kindness, no amount of clinical skill saves you.

So when I was building a team, what I cared about most was heart. Not credentials. Not experience. Heart. Everything else, I could teach. The warmth, the genuine interest in the person sitting across from you, the ability to be fully present with a nervous 72-year-old who needed someone to slow down and listen — that had to already be there. The question about parents and grandparents was my way of finding out whether it was.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

What Kim figured out about hiring for heart, I applied to managing staff across our offices — and it works just as well with communication styles. When you understand whether a team member is a thinker, a knower, or a feeler, you stop wondering why your best instructions land differently depending on who hears them. A thinker on staff needs the reasoning behind the policy, not just the rule. A feeler needs to understand how the change affects the patients they care about. A knower wants the headline — give them the what, trust them on the how. Once I stopped trying to lead everyone the same way, the culture in our offices got a lot stronger. Kim built the heart of the team by hiring right. Understanding modalities was how I helped each person give their best once they were there.

---

**The principle**

Hire for what cannot be taught. Competence in systems, scheduling, and procedures can be developed through training. Genuine warmth toward elderly patients — the kind shaped by real relationships with parents, grandparents, and older friends — either exists in a person or it does not. One well-crafted interview question, asked honestly and listened to carefully, tells you more than a resume. Set a physical environment that matches the care you want to deliver, and never ask a team member to do something you would not do yourself.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Hiring philosophies are hard to encode, but the standard is not. Once you know what "hiring for heart" looks like in practice — the stories people tell, the warmth in their voice, the specific memories they carry — you can build that standard into a structured interview guide that every practice location uses consistently. AI-assisted candidate screening can flag culture-fit language in resumes and surface patterns in interview notes. Automated office-environment checklists, sent to managers each morning, ensure that clean bathrooms and fresh coffee are not left to memory. The human who greets the patient still has to mean it — but the system can make sure all the conditions for meaning it are in place before the first patient arrives.

---

**For the Closer**

Your team is your first impression, and first impressions either open the relationship or close it before you get the chance. Hiring for heart is not idealism — it is the most practical sales decision you make.

**For the Professional**

Every patient who walks through your door deserves to feel welcomed by someone who genuinely cares. Building that team starts before the first patient ever arrives — it starts with who you put at the front desk and what standard you hold for them, and for yourself.
