# Chapter 18: The Gift of Hearing

*(Kim, with Mark)*

---

> "Thank you so much for giving me that gift — that I could hear my granddaughter and her wedding."
> — A patient of Kim Moore

---

There is a woman I think about often. She came into our office about a month before her granddaughter's wedding. She had just started wearing hearing aids for the first time, and she was terrified. Not of the hearing aids themselves — but of missing the wedding. The whole thing. The vows, the music, the laughter at the reception, the toasts. She had watched her hearing slip away over the years, and now one of the most important days in her family's life was coming, and she was afraid she would be sitting in the front row hearing nothing but muffled sound and silence.

We started working together right away. I told her what I told every new hearing aid wearer: wear them six hours a day, then eight, then work up to a full day. Give your brain the time it needs to catch up. It takes about a month for the brain to start rewiring itself to process all that new sound. We had about a month before the wedding. It was going to be close.

She came in for every appointment. She practiced. She stuck with it even when everything sounded too loud at first, even when her own voice sounded strange to her. I gave her gold stickers when she made progress — the same ones I had used with my own children, the same ones I had been giving to patients for years when they hit a milestone on their journey. They would chuckle at the stickers, but they loved them. That small gesture of "you did it, I see you, you made it one more step" mattered. More than I can explain.

She earned a lot of gold stickers that month.

Two weeks after the wedding, she came back in. And the moment she walked through the door, I could see it on her face before she said a single word. She sat down and she said: "Kim, I heard the whole wedding. I heard the reception. I heard everything." Then she looked at me and she said, "I just want to tell you thank you very much for giving me that gift — that I could hear my granddaughter and her wedding."

I have been in this work for a long time. I have helped thousands of people. And still, in that moment, I had to take a breath.

That is what this is. That is the whole thing. Everything we do — the testing, the follow-ups, the patient journey, the honest conversations, the gold stickers, the clean waiting rooms and the warm coffee and the Hershey bars and the careful fitting and the long phone calls with nervous first-time wearers — all of it exists in service of moments like that one. A grandmother hearing her granddaughter say her vows. A woman sitting in a pew, eyes wet, hearing every word.

---

I have had more stories like that one than I can count. The 42-year-old woman who had lost her hearing in a car accident — who came back one day and told me she had no idea there were birds in her backyard, and that she had decided she needed to start being among the living again. The retired school teacher whose daughter stormed in demanding a refund, only to leave the office in tears when she heard her mother answer questions from across the hall with perfect clarity. The patients who came in just for coffee on days when they had no appointment, because they knew someone there would be happy to see them.

Every one of those stories is a gift. Not just to the patient. To me.

I want you to understand something about what it means to do this work well. It is not about the hearing aid. The hearing aid is a tool — a remarkable tool, one that can genuinely restore someone's engagement with the world around them — but it is only a tool. What actually changes a person's life is the relationship they have with the person helping them through the journey. The patience. The hand-holding. The honest conversation when they wanted to quit. The gold sticker when they made it through another week.

I always believed I had a gift of hearing to give my patients. I meant that literally. What I could give them was not just better sound — it was their place back in the world. Their independence at the doctor's office. Their ability to hear their spouse say I love you at the dinner table. Their voice in the conversation at the retirement community. Hearing is not a luxury. When you lose it, the world becomes a very quiet, very lonely place. I have watched that loneliness up close, and I know what it costs a person. Which is why giving it back — even partially, even with time and effort and patience — is one of the most meaningful things one human being can do for another.

---

**Mark:**

I want to tell you why I stayed.

Twenty-three years. Over ten thousand people fitted with hearing aids. More than thirty thousand tested. One of the largest privately owned practices in the country, built from California to Florida. A lot of people look at those numbers and assume the answer to "why did you stay?" must be something about business, about building, about the satisfaction of growing something.

And those things were real. I liked building. I liked the challenge of the craft — understanding hearing loss, reading people, finding the right device for the right person, developing the patient journey process, teaching the team to replicate what worked.

But none of that is why I stayed.

I stayed because of a woman who walked back through my office door three days after I fitted her with her first hearing aid and started calling my name down the hallway. I was new. I was twenty-something and consciously aware of everything I did not yet know. And this woman — probably seventy-five years old, and so gentle about it — found me and threw her arms around me and started crying.

"You have changed my life," she said. "The rain — it rained yesterday, and I just sat and bawled. I heard the rain on the roof, and I didn't even know I missed that."

Then she told me about the leaves blowing through the trees. The birds when the sun came out. The sounds she had not heard in years without even knowing they were gone.

From that day forward, what I was doing did not feel like a business anymore. It felt like something else entirely. It felt like the same feeling you get when you give a gift — a real gift, one you chose because you knew it was exactly what that person needed — and you watch their face when they open it. That joy of giving is, to me, the highest thing. I achieved what I can only call the ultimate gift of giving: hearing, which is one of the most important senses we have.

Hearing aids were never widgets to me. They were not a product line. They were the mechanism through which I could give that gift, over and over again, to thousands of people who had no idea what they were missing until it came back to them.

That is why I stayed for twenty-three years. Not the revenue, not the growth, not the challenge of building the practice. The gifts. One at a time, every single day.

**A letter to you**

You are finishing this book having heard two voices — two perspectives on the same work, in the same chairs, across the same table from the same patients over the same decades. Kim's and mine. We hoped that between us we covered enough of the experience to be useful to you, wherever you are in this work.

Maybe you are brand new. You answered a phone call about a job opening and found yourself standing at a front desk wondering whether you were cut out for this. You are. If you care about people — genuinely, not as a strategy — you already have the most important qualification. Everything else, you learn.

Maybe you have been doing this for years and you picked up this book looking for something to reignite you. We hope something in these pages reminded you of why you started. The woman who heard the rain. The grandfather who shared a Hershey bar with his late brother in memory. The grandmother at the wedding. These are not exceptional stories. They happen in practices like yours, every week, if you are paying attention.

Maybe you are building a team, or opening another location, or navigating the harder parts of running a small business — the staff turnover, the insurance headaches, the patients who push back, the returns that sting. We know all of it. We lived all of it. The unglamorous parts of this work are real, and we did not want to pretend otherwise.

But here is what we know, after all of it: the work is worth it. Not in the abstract, "meaningful career" sense — though that is true too — but in the specific, immediate, human sense. You will be in the room when someone hears their spouse's voice clearly for the first time in years. You will be at the front desk when the daughter who came in demanding a refund leaves instead in tears of relief. You will be in the testing booth when someone realizes they can hear birds again. You will hand out a gold sticker to someone who made it through another week of a hard journey, and they will laugh, and then they will mean it.

We did this work because it gave us the chance, every single day, to give people back something they had lost without even realizing it. We believe you can give that gift too.

Thank you for doing this work. Thank you for caring. The patients you will help in the years ahead — whether you know their names yet or not — are going to be better for having found you.

Here is to all of them.

*— Kim & Mark Moore, Licensed Hearing Aid Dispensers*

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

Kim gave the gift patiently, one gold sticker at a time. I gave it fast and loud and in the room, trying to get past denial before a patient walked back out the door. Different styles, same gift. That is the lesson I carry from twenty-three years at this: the gift of giving is available to every single person in a practice, regardless of whether they are at the front desk or in the dispensing chair. You do not have to be the one who does the fitting to change someone's life. Kim changed lives from the lobby. The woman who made the follow-up call changed lives from the phone. Everyone in this practice, at every station, is a potential moment of giving. Technology will keep getting better — hearing aids will keep getting smaller and smarter, AI will handle more of the scheduling and the follow-up and the outreach. But the gift itself, the human moment of one person genuinely helping another person hear better — that cannot be automated. That will always be yours to give.

---

**The principle**

The technical work of fitting hearing aids serves a deeper purpose: restoring a person's place in the world. When you understand that the hearing aid is a tool and the relationship is the gift, everything about how you practice changes. Patience is no longer optional — it is the job. Care is no longer a soft skill — it is the competency. And the work becomes not just a livelihood, but a vocation.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Technology should expand the gift of giving, never replace the human who gives it. AI-powered lifecycle management can ensure that no patient falls through the cracks between appointments — that the patient who hit a difficult stretch in week two gets a check-in call, that the woman preparing for her granddaughter's wedding has a touchpoint every few days, that the follow-up that used to depend on someone remembering to make the call now happens automatically. More time and energy freed from administrative work means more capacity for the moments that matter: the human, unhurried conversation in the room where someone is deciding whether to trust you with their hearing. Let the technology hold the schedule. You hold the patient.

---

**For the Closer**

Every patient in your chair represents a moment of giving — a chance to return something lost, not just make a transaction. The practices that last, the reputations that compound into forty thousand patients, are built on that understanding. Give the gift, again and again, and the business takes care of itself.

**For the Professional**

You may never know the full story of what you gave someone. The grandmother at the wedding did not tell anyone but Kim. The 42-year-old who heard birds for the first time did not make the news. These gifts happen quietly, in the middle of ordinary days, in offices that look like any other office. You are the reason they happen. Never underestimate what you are doing when you do this work with your whole heart.
