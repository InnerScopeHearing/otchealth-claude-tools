# Chapter 6: What Brings You In Today?

*(Mark)*

> "I don't wanna assume that they're here to buy a hearing aid — because that's where you're gonna get into problems. No one wants to buy. They don't wanna be sold, and they don't wanna buy. However, they wanna be helped."

---

Four words. That is all it took to change the direction of almost every patient conversation I ever had.

"What brings you in today?"

Not "Are you interested in hearing aids?" Not "Have you noticed problems with your hearing?" Not a clipboard full of questions and a form to fill out before I had even introduced myself. Just four words that told the person sitting across from me one thing: I am here to listen before I do anything else.

I learned early in my career that the biggest mistake you can make in this business is assuming. You assume someone is here to buy, and you start selling before you have earned the right to. The moment you do that, you have lost them — not because they are not interested in better hearing, but because you skipped the most important step in the entire process. You skipped getting to know who they are and why they actually walked through the door.

Nobody comes in to buy a hearing aid. I want you to sit with that for a moment, because I mean it literally. In twenty-three years of fitting over ten thousand people, I cannot think of a single patient who came in and said, "I've decided I want a hearing aid and I'm ready to purchase today." Not one. And the rare exception — the man with a checkbook in his front pocket and a pocket protector full of pens — practically announces himself the moment he sits down. Outside of that, every single person who comes in is there for a more complicated reason, and your job is to find out what that reason actually is.

---

"What brings you in today?" does several things at once.

First, it gives the patient permission to answer honestly, without any sales pressure attached. They can say, "My wife made me come." They can say, "I noticed I've been asking people to repeat themselves more often lately." They can say, "I'm not here to buy anything, I just want to get tested." All of those are real answers I heard hundreds of times, and every one of them is useful information — because it tells me where this person actually is in their own process.

Second, and this is the part that most people miss, it gives me my opening. Because whatever they say next, I repeat it back.

"I'm here because my wife thinks I have a hearing problem."

"Oh — your wife thinks you have a hearing problem."

That is not me being clever. That is me doing something very specific: I am reinforcing what they told me. I am saying it back to them in a way that signals I heard it and I take it seriously. And when I stop talking after that, they continue. They fill the silence. They tell me more. And everything they tell me is something I can use — not to manipulate them, but to understand where they are and how I can actually help.

"Well, I don't really think so, I think she's just... I mean, I do ask people to repeat sometimes, but everyone does that."

"Ah — so you're not sure you have a problem, but she's concerned."

Right. And now I know we have a denial situation on our hands. I know before we even get to the test booth that this person is going to need a different kind of conversation than someone who has already admitted there is an issue. I am already mapping out how I am going to approach the rest of the appointment, and I have not even picked up an audiogram form.

---

Here is something I have always believed and still believe: if a person tells you something, they believe it is important. And if they believe it is important, the least you can do is repeat it back so they know you heard it. It sounds almost too simple to be a technique, but it is one of the most powerful things I learned in all my years in this business. Pacing — really matching the pace and the content of someone's communication — builds rapport faster than any script ever could.

This is also how you draw out objections early, before they become walls.

I wanted to know as quickly as possible whether a patient had concerns about cost, or bad experiences with hearing aids in the past, or a spouse who was skeptical, or fear about looking old. I did not want to save those for the end of an hour-long appointment, right when I was ready to recommend a solution. I wanted them on the table at the beginning, because then I could address them as we went — naturally, through conversation — rather than trying to dismantle them at the finish line when both of us were tired.

So in the history-taking process, before testing, I would ask. "Have you ever tried hearing aids before?" If yes: "How did that go? What did you like, what did you not like?" "Do you know any friends or family members who have hearing aids? What have you heard about them from people you know?"

Every answer told me something. A patient who says his buddy got hearing aids and threw them in a drawer after two weeks is a patient who has a specific fear — the fear of wasted money and wasted effort. I can address that. A patient who says she has been wanting to do this for years but kept putting it off because she was not sure anyone could actually help her — that person needs something different. She needs to believe in me before she will believe in any device I could recommend.

None of that comes out if I walk in and start talking features and frequencies.

---

The other thing I always tried to understand before the appointment was this: who, if anyone, came with them, and what role that person is playing.

When a man comes in alone, without his wife, he is usually not there to make a decision. He is there to get confirmation that he does not need to make a decision. I learned that from years of experience: men coming in solo are more often looking for an exit than a purchase. Women who come in alone, on the other hand, have usually already made up their minds that they are going to deal with this. They came prepared. They are ready.

When the spouse is in the room — and the spouse is almost always in the room — I needed to figure out quickly what that dynamic looked like. Who brought who? Who is on my side? Who is resistant? Because sometimes the way to change a room is not to convince the patient — it is to win the spouse. If the wife is tired of repeating herself, tired of the television at full volume, tired of her husband nodding along to conversations he clearly cannot follow, then she is my ally. And two against one, when it is done with empathy and honesty, is a very effective conversation.

You empathize with the spouse. You make sure they know you understand what this has been like for them too. Because untreated hearing loss is not just the patient's problem — it is the whole household's problem. It affects marriages. It affects relationships with grandchildren. It affects the quality of dinner-table conversations and Sunday afternoons and everything in between. If I could make the spouse feel heard, she often did a better job of making my case than I could have done myself.

---

The other piece of that opening conversation that I always held onto was this: before the appointment was over — sometimes at the very beginning, while we were still just talking — I would ask a version of the same question: "If we find something today, and we can help you, would you be willing to consider doing something about it?" Not "would you buy," not "are you prepared to invest today." Just: would you be willing to consider it, if we find we can help you.

Most people said yes. Because that is a reasonable thing to agree to. And once they said yes, even to that — we had an implicit agreement. Not a commitment to purchase. Not a signature. Just a shared understanding that they came in to get help, and if help was available, they were open to it. That single question, asked early and without pressure, transformed more conversations than any closing technique I ever used.

People do not want to be sold. But they do want to be helped. The job of the opening question — "what brings you in today" — is to find the thread between those two things and follow it.

**From the other chair**

*(Kim)*

When a nervous patient came through that door, my job at the front desk was to start the settling-down process before they ever sat across from Mark. I would make sure they had coffee or water, offer to help with the paperwork, and just talk to them for a minute — not about hearing aids, just about them. By the time they got into the room, I wanted them a little more relaxed and a little less guarded. Mark's opening question only worked its full magic when the patient felt safe enough to answer honestly. That is what I was building in the waiting room. We were a team that way, from first contact to first question. The patient's real reason for coming in was usually revealed somewhere in that waiting room conversation — which is why I always made a point of quietly passing that along before the appointment started.

---

**The principle**

Every patient who walks in has a real reason for being there — and it is almost never "to buy a hearing aid." The dispenser's first obligation is to find that real reason through open questions, active listening, and the discipline of repeating back what the patient says. Objections surfaced early become conversations; objections saved for the close become walls.

---

**The AI-Layer**

The principle behind "what brings you in today" scales directly into digital intake. An AI-assisted intake form or pre-appointment questionnaire that asks open-ended questions — what is the situation that led you to call us, who will be coming with you today, have you tried hearing aids before — can arrive in the patient's hands before they sit down. The dispenser walks in already knowing the real reason, the likely objections, and whether the spouse is present. The opening question still matters; AI just gives you a head start on hearing the answer.

---

**For the Closer**

Surface the objection before the presentation, not after. A cost concern raised in the first five minutes is a conversation you can guide; a cost concern raised at the close is a rejection you have to fight. Draw them out early, handle them as you go, and you will close more rooms.

**For the Professional**

Your job is not to sell a hearing aid — it is to understand why this specific person finally came in. When you know that, you can meet them exactly where they are, speak to what they actually need, and help them say yes to something they already want, in their own time and their own words.
