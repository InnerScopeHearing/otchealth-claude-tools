# Chapter 7: Denial, Stigma, Vanity, Cost

*(Mark)*

---

> "They throw up cost as being the biggest thing — because they're in denial."

---

Let me tell you something that took me years to figure out, and once I did, everything changed about the way I worked with people.

Every time a person walked out of my office without a hearing aid — every single time — they gave me one of four reasons. Cost. Vanity. Stigma. Denial. And they almost always led with cost. "It's too expensive." "I can get it cheaper somewhere else." "I'll think about it and come back."

For a long time, I believed them. I spent energy working the price angle, trying to justify the investment, laying out financing options. And sometimes that worked. But more often than not, I could feel that something else was going on in that room, something they weren't saying out loud.

Then one day it clicked. I was in an appointment with a gentleman — professional, well-dressed, clearly comfortable financially. He came in wearing a good watch. He drove a nice car. He told me cost was the issue. And I sat there thinking: this man is not worried about four thousand dollars. So what is he really telling me?

He was telling me he wasn't ready. He was in denial.

Here is what I learned, and I want you to write it down: **flip the list**. The order patients give you is backwards. They say cost is number one. Vanity is number two. Stigma is number three. Denial is somewhere in there. But the truth — the real order of what is actually stopping them — is exactly inverted.

**Denial is number one.** Stigma is number two. Vanity is number three. Cost is number four.

Cost is the last real barrier. It is almost never the actual reason. It is the polite reason. It is the socially acceptable exit. Nobody wants to sit across from you and say, "I am terrified of looking old." Nobody wants to say, "I don't want people to think I'm damaged." So they say, "It costs too much," and they get up and walk out, and you let them — if you haven't understood what's actually happening.

---

Let me explain each one, because understanding them in the right order changes everything about how you respond.

**Denial** is the foundation of all of it. The person sitting in your chair genuinely does not believe they have a problem — or if they suspect they do, they have not yet admitted it to themselves. I have had patients come in whose spouses told me, privately, that they had been asking the person to repeat everything for three years. Three years of "What?" at the dinner table. Three years of the television turned up so loud the neighbors could hear. And that person will sit in my chair and say, "My hearing is fine. My wife just mumbles."

Denial runs deep because we don't experience our own hearing loss the way other people experience it. You can look in a mirror and see a gray hair. You cannot hear your own inability to hear. The brain quietly adjusts. It fills in gaps. It reads lips without you knowing it. So the person is genuinely, sincerely unaware of how much they are missing.

Your job is not to argue with denial. Your job is to gently show them the evidence — the audiogram, the speech test — and let reality do the talking, not you.

**Stigma** is what denial is protecting. Hearing aids have always been linked in people's minds to old, damaged, and handicapped. They think of their grandfather in the 1960s with those big banana-shaped aids squealing and screeching. They think of "Eh? Speak up!" They do not think of a small, virtually invisible device that fits in the canal and connects to a phone via Bluetooth. They are fighting an image that is thirty years out of date, but the image is powerful because it is tied to something everyone fears: mortality. A hearing aid, in that mental picture, means one foot in the grave.

When I mention a Duke University survey I read about years ago — on the most embarrassing products people associate with aging — people are always a little surprised. As I recall it, the number one most embarrassing product — above adult diapers, above canes, above walkers, above prosthetics — was a hearing aid. And the gap between number one and number two was significant. Not a close race. People would rather wear an adult diaper under their pants, where nobody can see it, than put something on their ear.

Think about that for a moment. Think about what we are up against.

And here is the irony I would always point out: asking "What?" every five minutes at a restaurant is far more visible than a discreet hearing aid. Interrupting a movie to ask what someone said is far more conspicuous. Missing the punchline of every joke and laughing three seconds too late — that draws more attention than any aid ever would. But logic does not move people who are in denial and stigma. Trust does.

**Vanity** is a close cousin of stigma, and I will tell you what the numbers showed me after fitting over 10,000 people: men are more vain about this than women. I know that might surprise some people, but think about it. Women generally have longer hair. They can conceal a behind-the-ear instrument more easily if that matters to them. But more than that, women take their health seriously. When a woman comes in by herself — without her husband — she has already made a decision. She has thought it through. She has given herself permission to address the problem. She is prepared to move forward if she believes you can help her.

When a man comes in by himself, without his wife? In my experience, he has come in to get confirmation that he does not need anything. He is there to be told he is fine. He is not prepared to buy. He is prepared to leave reassured.

Men do not want to feel old. They do not want the visible signal of a limitation. They look in the mirror and they see themselves ten or fifteen years younger than they are, because we all do — we live in our own reflection and we do not track our own aging the way people who haven't seen us in three years do. The vanity is real, and it deserves compassion, not dismissal.

**Cost** is last. And not because it is unimportant — for many people it genuinely is a consideration — but because you cannot solve a cost objection when denial, stigma, and vanity haven't been addressed first. If the person hasn't admitted they have a problem worth solving, no price point is going to feel right.

When cost does come up legitimately, I remind people of something simple: not acting is not free. The cost of untreated hearing loss is not zero. It is interpersonal friction at home. It is isolation at family dinners. It is pulling back from social situations because they are exhausting and embarrassing. And in my own reading and experience — and I want to be clear this is my understanding, not a clinical claim — I've come to believe untreated hearing loss is linked to cognitive decline over time: when the auditory nerve stops receiving signals, the brain's auditory processing has less to work with, and understanding gets harder even once the volume is back up. Today is a better day to address this than next year. We can do more with what you have now than we can with what you will have left if you wait.

I also tell people this: financing exists. We can bridge the gap. But more importantly, the price you pay includes me — my 23 years, my 10,000-plus fittings, my willingness to keep working with you until we get this right. You are not buying a device. You are buying the outcome.

---

The practical shift this understanding creates is simple: **draw out the real objections early**. Before the audiogram, during the intake conversation, ask what they know about hearing aids. Ask what friends have told them. Ask about previous experiences. Let them put their real hesitations on the table while the pressure is low, before you have ever shown them a device or quoted a number.

When the objection surfaces early, it is a conversation. When it surfaces at the moment of decision, it is a wall.

If they say flat out, "I'm not here to buy anything," repeat it back without defensiveness: "You're not here to buy anything. Okay. Tell me, what experience do you have that makes you feel that way?" Now you are in a real conversation. Now you can understand where they actually are.

Understand where they are in the four-part sequence. Have they admitted the problem? Are they stuck in stigma? Is vanity the remaining hurdle? Is cost a genuine constraint or a proxy for something else? The way you handle each is different, and applying the wrong approach to the wrong barrier will get you nowhere.

**From the other chair**

*Kim* — What I always saw, sitting at the front desk, is that people's real fear was failure. Not vanity, not cost — failure. They were afraid they would spend the money, go through all of this, and the hearing aids still wouldn't work. They'd tried them before, or a friend had, and it hadn't gone well. So when Mark talks about denial and stigma, I think underneath all of it is: "What if I do this and I still can't hear?" That's why I always told patients, "Be kind to yourself. There is no wrong way to go through this journey. We are going to walk it together, and we are not going to leave you alone in it." That reassurance matters as much as any technical explanation. They need to know that failure is not the destination, and that you will catch them if they stumble.

---

**The principle**

The four objections patients give you — cost, vanity, stigma, denial — are stated in the wrong order. The real sequence runs in reverse: denial is the root, stigma feeds it, vanity expresses it, and cost is the socially acceptable face they show the world. Understand what layer you are actually working with before you try to solve it, because the solution to denial is not a better price, and the solution to stigma is not a financing plan.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Every touchpoint before a patient sits in your chair — an ad, a website, a follow-up email, a social post — is an opportunity to meet denial and stigma with dignity rather than pressure. AI-driven communication tools can help you deliver that messaging consistently and at scale: a nurturing email sequence that normalizes hearing loss as a health issue, not an embarrassment; digital content that reframes hearing aids in the language of connection and independence rather than limitation; pre-visit intake questions that surface objections before the appointment so you walk in already knowing where the real conversation needs to go. The human work of trust still happens in your chair. AI helps you begin earning it days before they arrive.

---

**For the Closer**

The moment you identify which objection you are actually facing — denial, stigma, vanity, or cost — you can stop working the wrong angle and close on the real one. Handle denial with evidence, stigma with trust, vanity with discretion, and cost with the value of you.

**For the Professional**

When a patient says "it's too expensive," hear what they are not saying: "I'm not sure I'm ready, and I'm not sure this will work." Your response is not a price negotiation — it is an invitation to keep talking, to stay in the relationship, to make space for them to get to yes on their own terms.
