# Chapter 12: The Honest Truth

*(Kim)*

> "I know a secret. I know the hearing aids work. These people don't know that. They're skeptical about why they need to have this hearing aid. And sometimes it's not the cost that bothers them the most — it's the fact that they don't want to fail."

---

There were times — more than a few, over all the years — when being honest with someone meant accepting that I might lose the sale. I want to talk about those times, because I think they say something important about what kind of work this really is.

When you believe in something as completely as I believe in hearing aids, it can be hard to watch someone walk toward the door when you know what they are walking away from. I have seen too many lonely people come through our offices. I have watched them leave, years later, with their whole bearing changed — lighter, more present, more themselves — because they could finally hear the world around them again. I know what the gift of hearing does. I have seen it transform lives, one person at a time, for years. So yes, when someone is on the fence, there is a pull to push. To say one more thing. To find one more angle.

But honest is honest. And the times I chose honest, even when it cost me — I do not regret a single one.

---

Here is the truth about what hearing aids can and cannot do: they can give someone a great deal of their hearing back, but not all at once, and not without patience. The brain has to relearn. It has to rebuild pathways that have been quiet for years, sometimes decades. The first month is hard. Your own voice sounds strange. Environments that used to be manageable suddenly seem overwhelming. The kettle is too loud. The refrigerator hums in a way you never noticed. Everything is just a lot.

Most patients do not understand this going in, even when we tell them. We tell them, but they do not really absorb it until they are living it. And then they come back — sometimes with that white envelope, which we always recognized — ready to say it is not working, ready to return.

This is the moment where I had to be honest, even if it was uncomfortable. Telling someone that the hearing aids are not the problem, that the brain needs more time, that they have to be patient with themselves — that is not always what they want to hear. Sometimes it made them upset with me. Sometimes they got their back up. Sometimes I lost the sale anyway.

But I never faked it. I never told someone their devices were working perfectly when I could see they were struggling. I never let a patient leave without saying what I actually thought was true. Because here is the thing: I know the hearing aids work. I know it. I have seen it hundreds and hundreds of times. These patients don't know it yet. They have no reason to trust the process until someone they trust tells them clearly — this is normal, this is temporary, I have seen the other side of this, and I've got you.

That kind of honesty is not a risk to the sale. It is, in the long run, what saves it.

---

Being honest sometimes meant telling someone directly that they needed hearing aids when they were not ready to hear it. That is a strange kind of courage, when you think about it — telling someone something they do not want to know, not because it benefits you, but because it is true and because it matters for them.

Patients sometimes want to give up on their journey. They have been through the hard part and they want relief from it. I understood that. I was never dismissive of the struggle. But I would not pretend the struggle was a reason to stop, when I knew in my heart that the other side of it was worth it.

I would go back to listening. That was always my approach when a patient was frustrated — just listen. Find the one thing that was bothering them the most at that exact moment, because it was not always the same thing from visit to visit. One week they didn't care about the birds. The next week, hearing the birds was suddenly everything. You have to listen fresh each time, not assume you already know the concern.

And I would tell them the honest thing, which is that this is a journey, not a switch. There is not a moment when you put in the hearing aids and everything is fixed. There is a process, and the process takes time, and the process requires them to be patient with themselves. I would tell patients often: be kind to yourself. There is no right or wrong way through this journey. Each one is unique. And I am here with you through every step.

---

I'll tell you about the young woman who came in who had been in a car accident. She was forty-two years old — young for our typical patient — and she had lost her hearing pretty suddenly as a result of the crash. She was skeptical about whether she really needed to keep her hearing aids. She was managing, she said. She was getting along fine. On one of her visits — maybe the third time in — she sat down and told me she had made up her mind to keep them.

I asked her why.

She said she had not known there were birds in her backyard. She had lived in that house for years, and she had not heard them in so long that she had stopped knowing they were there. And she said that when she opened a can of soda, she had not realized it made a sound — that little popping, crackling noise when the seal breaks. She started to laugh when she told me about her husband's reaction, how funny he thought it was that she was suddenly noticing things he had never once thought about.

And then she said it: "I decided I need to start being among the living again."

That sentence stayed with me. She was forty-two years old, and she had been slowly withdrawing from a world she could not hear, without fully knowing that was what was happening. The honesty in that moment — hers, about what she had been missing — was the thing that sealed her decision. Not any pitch I made. Not any feature I could point to. Just the real experience of birds and soda cans, and a woman deciding she was not ready to stop living in the world.

That is what is on the other side of the hard first month. That is what I knew, every time I sat with someone who was frustrated and ready to quit. I knew, and I had to be honest enough to hold the line even when they were not there yet.

---

There is a version of this work where you do whatever it takes to close the sale and not worry about what happens after. I have seen that version. It does not build practices. It does not build patient bases of forty thousand people who come back, and bring their siblings, and bring their friends, and trust you with the hardest thing — their independence, their ability to stay connected to the people they love.

The version that builds that is the one where you are honest even when it costs you. Even when someone returns the hearing aids and you were not able to talk them out of it. Even when you tell a patient what they need to hear rather than what they want to hear. Even when you say, clearly, that the hearing aids are not the problem — that patience and time are the answer — and they look at you like you are asking for something unreasonable.

It is not unreasonable. It is the truth. And the truth is the only thing that earns the kind of trust that keeps people coming back.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

Kim is right that honesty is the foundation. From the sales side, I'll add one dimension: honest expectations are set at delivery, not corrected at the return visit. That is why the COSI — the three specific listening goals the patient names before they walk out the door — matters so much. You are creating an honest agreement about what success looks like, specific enough to hold up against. The audiogram and the device are easy to agree on in the moment. What is hard is agreeing on the reality that a damaged auditory system needs time to rewire, and that the first month is the hardest. Say that plainly, at delivery. Set it in writing. It is not pessimism — it is the most honest and helpful thing you can tell someone before they go out into the real world with new ears.

---

**The principle**

Honesty with patients is not a risk to the relationship — it is the foundation of it. Telling someone the truth about their hearing, about the adaptation process, about what to expect and when, builds the trust that keeps them in the journey through the hard parts. A patient who trusts you enough to hear the honest answer will stay. A patient who is told only what they want to hear will return the devices and walk away.

**The AI-Layer**

Honest claims are not only ethical — they are the brand. Automated messaging that overpromises the adaptation process will generate returns; messaging that prepares patients honestly for the hard first month reduces them. AI-assisted lifecycle communication can be scripted to set accurate expectations at every milestone: day three, week two, the one-month check-in. Honest, timed outreach that says "this is the hard part, and it is normal" is both good care and good business. Compliance starts with the first message you send.

---

**For the Closer:** Honest expectation-setting is the best retention strategy you have. The patient who knows the first month will be strange and stays anyway becomes a loyal patient who refers. The one who was oversold walks out the door — permanently. The truth is your best closing tool for long-term business.

**For the Professional:** Patients need someone in their corner who will tell them the truth even when it is hard to hear. Be that person. Not because it will always save the sale — it won't. But because the ones who needed to hear it and chose to trust you anyway will remember you for the rest of their lives.
