## Chapter 3 Workbook — Rain on the Roof

---

### 1. The Takeaway

The hearing aid is a tool. The relationship — the trust, the listening, the follow-through — is the product. Mark's first solo patient did not hug him in the hallway because of the device. She hugged him because he listened to her, explained what he saw, and treated her like a person who deserved help. When a patient trusts you enough to stay with the process, the device gets the chance to do what it can do. That trust, and the life-change that follows, is why this work matters — and writing it down is how you carry it with you on the days the work feels ordinary.

### 4. The Drill — Your Life-Change Script

**What it is:** The words you use at a follow-up appointment — specifically when a patient comes back in the early weeks and is frustrated, or uncertain, or half-ready to quit — to help them connect what they're going through to what is coming. This is not a sales script. It is a trust script.

**The shape (from the manuscript):**
> "I didn't even know what I was missing. The rain — it rained yesterday, and I just sat and bawled, because I heard the rain on the roof. And I didn't even know I missed that." — Mark's patient

The patient said those words because someone had listened well enough that she trusted the process. Your script is what you say *before* that moment arrives — at day three, at week one, at the appointment where they're telling you it still feels strange.

**Model line (from the manuscript):** Mark notes that the patient returns a hearing aid when she doesn't believe you can help her — and the reason she got there, almost always, is that someone tried to sell her a device instead of a relationship. Your script should earn the patient's trust first, before anything else.
