# Chapter 3: Rain on the Roof

*(Mark)*

---

> "From that day forward, I felt it was not a business anymore. It was something way more than a business. It was helping people. It was serving people. It was contributing to someone's life."
> — Mark Moore

---

I was consciously incompetent. That's the only way I can describe where I was when I sat down with the first patient I ever sold a hearing aid to on my own.

The uncle had gone on a hunting trip and left me in charge of the office for a week. This was about two and a half months into my training. I'd done the book learning, I'd sat in the room and observed, and I'd learned to build the devices in the back. But I hadn't done a full solo appointment — from the history all the way through to a decision — not by myself. Now the office was mine for a week, and there was a schedule.

I knew what I didn't know. I was new. I was brand new. But I also knew that I was there and the patients were coming in, so I had to do something. And what I defaulted to, that first week, was listening. I spent a long time just talking with the patient before I did anything else.

She was about 75 years old.

She told me that her friends felt like she was always asking them to repeat themselves, and they were getting frustrated. She said everybody told her she turned the television up way too loud. She was embarrassed by it. You could hear that in the way she talked about it — not dramatic, just tired of the problem and a little worn down by it.

I tested her. The audiogram showed what I expected after hearing her describe her situation: a sloping loss in the high frequencies, typical of what I'd been learning to read. I explained what I was seeing as best I could for someone a few months into the work, and I told her I could help her with a hearing aid. She said, "Great." Just like that. She was ready. She'd been ready for a while, probably — she just needed someone to listen to what she was actually telling them.

I sold her an in-the-ear hearing aid for $450. That was the device we had available at the time, the right fit for her loss. I took the impression, glued it into the box the way we'd been trained, and dropped it in the mailbox with the postage-paid label for the manufacturer. Two weeks out, two weeks back. I made her appointment.

The uncle came home from his trip. I'd sold eight or nine hearing aids while he was gone, which seemed to surprise him some. The lady came back a few days after I delivered her aid.

I saw her come through the front door. She saw me down the hallway and she started calling out — "Mark, Mark, Mark" — and walking toward me. I came up the hall toward her. She reached out with both arms and just started crying. She pulled me into a hug and held on.

I didn't know what was happening. "What's the matter? What happened?" I asked her.

She said, "You have changed my life."

She said, "I didn't even know what I was missing. The rain — it rained yesterday, and I just sat and bawled, because I heard the rain on the roof. And I didn't even know I missed that."

She kept going. She said she'd started listening to the leaves blow down the street, listening to the leaves move through the trees. She'd heard the birds that morning when the sun came out. She said, "I didn't know I'd lost all that. I didn't even know I wasn't hearing it. And then once I heard it, I remembered — oh my, I haven't heard that. I missed all of that."

She called it saving her life. She said she was experiencing things like they were all new again.

I think I was still standing there with my mouth open when she finished. I didn't know what to say. I was twenty-something, a few months into a job I barely understood, and this woman was hugging me and crying because she'd heard rain on her roof.

From that day forward, I felt it was not a business anymore.

That sounds simple. It was not simple. It was one of those moments that reorganizes how you understand your own work. Before that morning, the hearing aid was a product. A device. Something with a circuit and a mold and a trim pot on it, that I had learned to build and learned to sell. After that morning, it was something else. It was the conduit to something that mattered — really mattered — in the way that almost nothing in commerce actually does.

I've sold over 10,000 people hearing aids in a 23-year career. I've tested more than 30,000 people. And I will tell you honestly that the reason I stayed — the reason I worked five and six days a week, the reason I kept going back to the chair when I could have done something else — was that first patient. The rain on the roof. The understanding that what we do is not profit and loss, not widgets, not a transaction that closes and then disappears. What we do is restore something to people that they didn't even know they had lost.

That's a different reason to go to work.

It shaped everything about how I thought about our work in this business, too. Because here's what I learned from that visit: the hearing aid does nothing until the person believes in the process enough to wear it. It does nothing if it goes in a drawer. It does nothing if the patient gives up at week two because their own voice sounds funny and they don't understand why. The device is an inanimate object. It's a tool. What actually matters is the person in the chair across from you and whether they trust you enough to keep going.

When that woman came back after three days and hugged me in the hallway, she wasn't saying thank you for a well-built piece of acrylic and circuit board. She was saying thank you for taking the time to listen, for explaining what I was seeing in her test results, for treating her like a person who deserved help. Earning the patient's trust came first. The rain on the roof came after.

That's what most people get wrong. They think the job is to sell the hearing aid. It's not. The job is to make someone trust you enough to let you help them. The hearing aid is just the tool you use to do it. And if that particular tool isn't working — if the fit is off, if the device isn't right for their lifestyle, if they need a different form factor or a different brand — you change the tool. You don't return the relationship. Because if the patient returns the hearing aid, they're not returning a device. They're returning you. They're telling you they don't believe you can help them. And the reason they got there, almost always, is that someone tried to sell them a hearing aid instead of earning their trust and guiding them toward better hearing.

I stayed in this business for 23 years because of moments like the one in that hallway. The granddaughter's wedding where grandma heard the whole ceremony. The man who started sleeping through the night again because he wasn't straining to hear the television anymore. The retired teacher who came in alone, month after month, because we were the only people she talked to all week — and who left every time looking a little less lonely.

We were not selling hardware. We were giving people something back that they'd lost and hadn't yet figured out how to mourn.

It's the gift of giving. I mean that literally. You know the feeling when you give a gift to someone and you can see it land — you can see in their face that this is exactly right, that this is what they needed, that this gift means something to them. That feeling is way better than receiving a gift. It's one of the most specific human pleasures I know.

We get to do that every single day.

I've said to a lot of people over the years that hearing is one of the most important senses we have. But saying that doesn't capture it. What captured it for me was a 75-year-old woman, standing in a hallway, crying, because she heard rain on her roof for the first time in she didn't know how long.

That's why this work matters. That's why I stayed. That's the whole thing.

**From the other chair**

*(Kim)*

When patients couldn't hear, I watched what that silence actually did to people. It made the world smaller. It made them pull back from conversations they used to enjoy, stop going places they used to go, sit a little further back from the family at dinner so they wouldn't have to ask someone to repeat themselves again. Hearing loss creates a loneliness that most people don't name as loneliness — they just feel it closing in. When you put the right hearing aid on someone and walk them through those first weeks, you're not fixing a clinical problem. You're handing them their life back. I've seen patients come in looking worn down and leave, a few months later, looking like someone turned a light back on inside them. That's not an exaggeration. When you can hear again — really hear — the world stops being a quiet, lonely place and becomes the world again. That's what we were there for.

---

**The principle**

The hearing aid is a tool. The relationship is the product. When a patient trusts you — when they believe you will do whatever it takes to help them hear better — they will stay with the process long enough to succeed. When they don't trust you, no device is good enough to keep them. The first sale is never the device. The first sale is always you.

---

**The AI-Layer**

The emotional turning point Mark describes — the patient who came back three days after delivery, crying — is exactly the moment that rescues long-term retention. Today, AI-powered lifecycle tools make it possible to deliver that level of follow-up at scale: automated check-ins at day three, week one, and week two that ask the right questions early, flag patients who may be struggling, and prompt a personal outreach before the white envelope ever appears. The technology doesn't replace the human moment. It creates more of them, consistently, across every patient in the practice.

---

**For the Professional**

The patient who heard rain on the roof didn't just have a hearing aid fitted. She had someone sit with her, listen to her, and take her situation seriously. Write down what each patient tells you they've been missing. Bring it back up at every follow-up. The moment they can hear it again — that conversation, that grandchild, that baseball game on television — they need to know you were there for it.
