# Chapter 13 — The Patient Journey

*(Mark)*

> "We needed to make sure that the patient understood that they're going through a process called the patient journey process — and we wanted this process to be duplicatable through multiple offices."

---

I always say helping patients hear better is just the beginning. The moment a patient walks out with hearing devices in hand, the real work starts. And for years, across dozens of offices from California to Florida, that work had to happen the same way every time — whether I was in the room or not. That was the puzzle that drove me, and the solution we landed on was what we called the Patient Journey to Better Hearing.

Let me back up and explain why we needed a duplicatable process in the first place.

When you run one office and you are the clinician, you can hold everything in your head. You know exactly where each patient is in the adaptation curve. You know who's ready to be pushed a little further and who needs hand-holding. But the moment you open a second office, a third, and more, your head is not big enough to keep all that straight — and neither is anyone else's. If the process only lives inside the clinician's personality, it doesn't survive the first time you hire someone new. We had seen that failure mode enough times to be determined to build something different.

The journey process we developed starts even before we hand over the devices. At time of delivery, I would pull out what I called the modified COSI — and that word "modified" matters. COSI stands for Client Oriented Scale of Improvement, and it is a real tool used across the industry. The full COSI has a long list of listening situations. We trimmed it way down and asked each patient to name just three — the three specific listening situations they most wanted to improve.

Three. Not ten. Not everything. Three.

Here is why that matters. I had a lady, and she is someone I still think about, who came back after a few weeks and said the hearing aids were not working. She was returning them. I was confused because by every objective measure she was doing well — she could hear conversation, she was following television, her family said she seemed sharper. I asked her what she had bought them for.

She said, "I bought them to hear my PBS program. It's a British show I watch every night."

I sat with that for a second. A British accent. I thought, well, understanding that I cannot make hearing devices manufacture clarity where the speakers themselves are talking in a way that even normal-hearing people sometimes struggle with — that is a different conversation. What I learned from her is that if I had asked the right question at delivery, we could have had a real discussion about what she could reasonably expect, set her up differently, adjusted for her specific listening goal, and maybe — just maybe — she would have stayed in the journey rather than walking out the door disappointed.

That one patient changed how we did every delivery from that point forward.

So now, at delivery, after we go through the devices themselves — how to wear them, how to charge them, how to care for them — I sit down with the patient and say, "Tell me specifically. If you were going to grade these hearing aids, what three situations matter most to you? Not hearing in general. Specific. Where do you want to hear? What program? What voice? What room?"

I would write those three things down with the patient, right there in the Journey to Better Hearing booklet, and the patient would sign off on them. Then I'd ask them to rank the three by priority.

You would be amazed at what came out of that conversation. Nine times out of ten, the husband would start listing things like the television program he liked or the Elks Lodge on Thursday nights, and I would have to nudge him: "What about your wife? Is she in there somewhere?" He'd get a little sheepish and add her to the list, usually second or third. But at least now we knew.

We called this grading ourselves. Because when that patient came back for the first follow-up, I was not asking some vague question like "How are the hearing aids?" I was pulling out those three signed goals and saying, "Okay — your wife across the dinner table. Better, much better, slightly better, no different, or worse?" We were measuring patient outcomes against a contract we had made together.

That scoring system — better and much better on all three goals — is what we defined as a successful fitting. Not the audiogram alone, not the patient seeming happy, but documented improvement in the exact situations the patient themselves had named. When we got there, we asked the patient to formally acknowledge the completion. They signed it. That signed document became the foundation for everything that came after: the loyalty conversation, the referral conversation, the upgrade conversation down the road.

The booklet itself walked the patient through the whole adaptation process. We started by naming denial honestly — because the first step in the booklet is admitting you have a problem, and there are exercises in there to help work through what that means. Then comes education about their specific type of hearing loss. Then comes the section on realistic expectations, because one of the biggest enemies of a successful fitting is a patient who paid six thousand dollars and expects to hear as though they are twenty-five again. The cost and the expectation have to be in proportion, and we helped them think that through explicitly rather than letting them discover the mismatch on their own six weeks later.

The middle of the journey was about practice. Assignments, we called them. The first week: read out loud to yourself for twenty minutes a day. Why? Because the number one reason patients return a hearing device is their own voice. When you put a device in the ear, even an open fit, you change the balance between air conduction and bone conduction, and the patient's voice comes back louder and different to them — kind of hollow, like they are talking in a barrel, Kim always says. If we do not warn them about this, they feel something is wrong. If we do warn them and give them an exercise to adapt to it, most of them work right through it.

The second week's assignment was to get out of the quiet living room and into the real world — background noise, a store, a restaurant — not because that is comfortable but because the brain needs that full-range experience to begin creating new pathways. The brain that has been deprived of sound for years has actually rewired itself to get by without it. Think of it like a broken arm in a sling: take the sling off after six months and the arm needs physical therapy before it works again. Amplification is the sling coming off. The journey booklet is the physical therapy.

We also ran what we called the AWARE program — a five-session computerized listening program. It is auditory training, essentially. The brain learning to decode speech again, one small exercise at a time. And throughout all of this, we verified: we measured speech understanding at delivery and charted it, so three years down the road we could pull out that baseline and compare where the patient was now.

Every follow-up ran against the COSI goals. Every visit became a real touchpoint — not just the patient picking up batteries. We wanted patients coming in at least three to four times a year, partly for cleaning and maintenance, but really because each visit builds trust and deepens the relationship. We tracked it through an RFM system: recency, frequency, and monetary. One transaction means about twenty-five percent loyalty. Get them to five or six transactions and you are at seventy-five percent. Those are the patients who upgrade with you, who refer their friends, who bring their spouses in.

I will tell you something that might sound counterintuitive. The referrals were not from gimmicks. We never paid for referrals or ran point systems that felt transactional. The referrals came at the exact moment a patient signed their COSI completion form and we said, "You know, if you have friends wearing hearing aids who are not doing as well as you are right now, they may not have been through a program like this. You could help them." That is all it took. The satisfied patient became the most credible voice we never had to pay for.

We built over forty thousand patients that way — one journey at a time, documented and duplicatable.

**From the other chair**

*(Kim)*

Mark designed the framework, and I lived inside it every single day at the front desk and in the follow-up room. The journey booklet was something patients would actually hold, write in, sign — it made the process feel real to them. My favorite part was always giving out gold stickers when patients hit a milestone: first time they came in and told me they heard the birds, or they made it through a noisy family dinner. You would think grown adults would roll their eyes at gold stickers. They never did. They loved them. It told them that someone noticed they had done something hard. The journey works because it is built around the patient's own words and goals — not ours. That small thing, writing down what *they* want in *their* handwriting, changes everything about how they approach the adaptation.

---

**The principle**

Before a patient can succeed with hearing devices, both they and the professional need to agree on exactly what success looks like — in specific, measurable, personal terms. Three listening goals, written down and signed, turn a vague hope into a shared contract. When you grade every follow-up against that contract, you are not guessing whether the fitting worked. You know.

---

**The AI-Layer**

The COSI process Mark describes is a lifecycle management system — intake, goal-setting, weekly assignments, multi-session scoring, satisfaction survey, referral ask. Every one of those steps is a touchpoint that can be sequenced, timed, and delivered consistently through today's AI-assisted practice tools. A CRM can flag when a patient is due for their week-two assignment check-in, prompt the front desk with the patient's three named COSI goals before the appointment, and surface the baseline speech scores at the three-year visit without anyone having to dig through a folder. The human still sits across the table and has the conversation. AI makes sure the conversation always happens, with the right information, at the right time.

---

**For the Professional:** The modified COSI is how you honor each patient's individual story. When you write down their three goals in their words — hearing their wife across the dinner table, hearing the baseball game on Sunday afternoon — you are saying: *I listened, and this journey is built around you, not a generic fitting protocol.* That is the foundation of every meaningful outcome.
