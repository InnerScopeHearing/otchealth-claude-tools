# Chapter 1: Two Chairs, One Family

*(Kim)*

---

> "Everyone puts their pants on the same way as everyone else. No matter what walk of life they come from, everyone deserves kindness and respect."
> — Kim Moore

---

I grew up in the hearing aid business before I ever knew that was what I was doing.

I was around ten years old when my dad, Marvin Posey, started bringing the mailers home. Big stacks of them — advertising pieces he'd send out to bring people into his hearing aid offices. My brothers and sisters and I would sit around the kitchen table folding them, stamping them, peeling address labels and pressing them down on the envelopes so they'd lay smooth. My brother would fight over who got to run the envelope through the postage machine. Looking back now, that table was probably my first classroom in this business. We didn't think of it as work. It was just what we did to help Dad.

What made it real was what happened at dinner. Dad would come home and tell us how many hearing aids he had sold that day — and he'd always connect it back to us. "Those mailers you all did," he'd say. "That's how these folks came in." You could feel the pride at that table. It wasn't just that he appreciated the help. He made sure we understood that what we did mattered in the outcome. That the work of a ten-year-old with a glue stick had something to do with a person walking out of his office able to hear again. That stayed with me.

A few years later, around thirteen, I got to go to work with him during the summer. My first real job. I was so excited I probably drove everyone crazy. Dad showed me how to treat customers with kindness — how to be friendly and genuinely helpful while they were in the office. The receptionist back then was a woman named Marilyn. She was amazing. Patient in a way that I wanted to be. The customers adored her. I found myself studying her, watching how she moved through the office, how she talked to people who came in nervous or uncertain. She made them feel like they'd come to the right place. Like they mattered. I didn't have a word for it then, but what she was doing was caring — real caring, not performed.

My mom was teaching me a different piece of the same picture. She handled the bookkeeping, and she was very clear about why. "Your father can do what he does," she told me, "because the numbers are covered." She showed me that the support side of a business isn't the background. It's the foundation the whole thing stands on. Without someone watching the accounting, Dad couldn't keep selling. Without someone like Marilyn holding the front desk, the patients didn't come back. None of it worked without all of it working.

I went back every summer through high school. I earned work-experience credits for it. I was the first of my friend group to have a real job, and the first of my sisters to work for my parents. My other two sisters followed after me. By the time I graduated, I had years of real experience — experience most people don't get until they're well into a career.

Two lessons stayed with me through all of it, and I carry them into the practice today. The first is the one my dad repeated so often it became part of how I see people: everyone puts their pants on the same way. It didn't matter if a patient came in driving a new car or in worn shoes, if they were a retired doctor or a postal worker. Every single person who walked through that door deserved the same kindness, the same attention, the same care. Dad modeled that. Marilyn modeled it. And I have tried to model it every day I've spent in this work.

The second lesson was simpler but just as important: always treat people how you want to be treated.

When I met Mark and we eventually opened our own practice in Woodland, California, I came in already knowing what role I wanted to play. I wasn't the salesy type — never have been, never wanted to be. What I wanted was to be the best caregiver in the building. The front desk person. The tester. The one who made patients feel like they'd come home when they walked through our door. And as we started expanding to different cities and needed more licensed dispensers to cover our locations, I did become licensed — because the work required it and because I believed in it enough to commit to it fully.

Mark and I figured out early that my strengths were in the delivery side, the follow-ups, and especially the testing. I loved testing. To me, it wasn't the boring part of the job. It was the most human part. You are sitting across from a person, learning who they are, watching their face while the world comes back into focus for them — or watching them realize for the first time how much has faded. That is not a minor moment. That is one of the most important moments in a person's day.

I also believed, and still believe, that the front desk is where the whole journey begins. Before the test, before the fitting, before any conversation about hearing aids — it starts with a phone call. And that phone call is answered by a person. Whether that person sounds like they're glad you called or like they'd rather be somewhere else will determine whether you make the appointment or quietly hang up and decide you'll deal with it later. I knew from my years with Marilyn that the front desk wasn't a stepping stone to something more important. It was the most important position in the building.

I tell people now: the front desk person controls the environment, the flow of the business, and the outcomes for every patient who walks through that door. I believed that when I was a teenager handing patients their coffee, and I believe it now.

Over the years, as we grew and opened more offices, I tried to teach every receptionist and front desk person we hired what I had learned from Marilyn. Some got it right away. Some never quite did. And I came to understand that you can't fully teach it in an onboarding session. You have to model it, the same way Marilyn modeled it for me. The patient who comes in nervous because she doesn't know what a hearing test feels like, the man who won't admit he has a hearing problem but showed up anyway because his wife asked him to — those people are reading the room the moment they walk through the door. They're deciding, in the first thirty seconds, whether they feel safe here or whether they should come back some other time or maybe just not bother. That first impression is set long before the professional enters. It's set by whoever answers the phone. It's set by whoever looks up and smiles when the door opens.

When I think about what my dad gave me — beyond the business itself — it was a way of looking at people. He never let any of us forget that the patients coming through the door were whole human beings with full lives, not cases or numbers. He never let us separate the helpfulness from the heart. That's something you can't train into someone. But you can model it, day after day, until the people around you start to carry it too.

That's what Marilyn did for me. That's what my mom did for me. That's what my dad did for all of us sitting around that kitchen table, folding mailers, and learning without knowing we were learning.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

When I was introduced to Kim's family and started learning this business, her father Marvin told me something I've never forgotten. He said, "The hearing aid business has been very, very good to us." He was sitting across from this kid who'd been dabbling in real estate and thinking about golf course management — and he was laying out the possibility of a different life. At the time, he had five hearing aid stores and had built them from nothing, starting out of his trunk, going to convalescent hospitals with his brother before licensing even existed in most states. He wasn't bragging. He was showing me what was possible if you committed to the work and committed to the people. Kim had grown up watching him live that out. She already knew it in her bones. I had to learn it, and I'm grateful I had the right teachers.

---

**The principle**

The hearing aid business — like most businesses built on trust — runs on relationships that start long before any transaction. A child folding mailers is learning that their effort connects to someone's outcome. A teenager watching a great receptionist is learning what genuine care looks like in practice. The support role is not secondary to the work. It is the work, at every level.

---

**The AI-Layer**

The kitchen-table mailer operation Kim describes was an early acquisition funnel — reach the right people, give them a reason to call, make the call worthwhile. Today's practices can build that same funnel at scale: AI-assisted outreach identifies and segments prospective patients, automated follow-up sequences keep the practice present, and scheduling tools reduce the friction of that first appointment. The technology is new. The principle — make it easy for someone to take the first step toward better hearing — is the same one Marvin Posey was running with a stack of paper and a postage machine.

---

**For the Professional**

The caring journey begins the moment a patient considers calling. Build your intake experience — phone greeting, scheduling, lobby — to reflect the same kindness you'd show a member of your own family, because to many patients, you may be the most consistent caring presence they have.
