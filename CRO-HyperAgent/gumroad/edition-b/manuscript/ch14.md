# Chapter 14 — Saving the Return

*(Kim)*

> "Once you have figured out the most important issue to them at that moment, then you are able to fix the problem so that they can stay on that journey."

---

I really don't like to sell hearing aids. I know that sounds strange coming from a licensed dispenser who spent decades in the hearing aid business, but it is the truth. What I love is helping people. What I love is seeing someone walk in tight and scared, and walking out a little lighter. Helping patients hear better, for me, always felt like the natural result of doing the caring work well. And nowhere did I feel that more clearly than when a patient came back with a white envelope in their hand.

We always put everything together for a patient at delivery — the hearing aids in their charging case, the paperwork, the warranty card, the Journey to Better Hearing booklet — all of it tucked into a white envelope. It was just how we did things. So when a patient showed up for a follow-up visit and they were holding that white envelope, we knew before they sat down. They were coming to return. That envelope told the whole story before anyone said a word.

I want to be honest: that moment used to make my stomach drop a little. Not because of anything transactional. Because I knew that envelope meant someone was about to give up on hearing better, and I believed — I still believe — in what hearing aids can do. I had seen it too many times. I knew what was on the other side of that struggle if they would just stay in the journey a little longer.

So that is when I geared up, as I used to say. Not to fight the patient. Not to defend the product. To listen.

The single most important thing I learned about saving a return is this: you cannot save it until you find the one thing. There are always a list of concerns — the hearing aids feel funny, the sounds are too sharp, the wife is complaining that they whistle. Patients will pour everything out when they are frustrated. But buried somewhere in all of that is the real thing, the one issue that is actually driving them toward the door. Your job is to understand the patient's real concerns fully, listening through all of it until you can identify what that one thing is. Then you fix that one thing.

Most of the time, I found that patients did not really even know exactly what they wanted to hear differently. One week they were not hearing the birds. The next week they came in and said they wanted the television clearer. The following week it was something else. The goal posts moved because the brain was still sorting out this new world of sound it had been handed. Part of my job was helping them understand that the shifting was normal — that the brain takes time to decide what it wants to prioritize — and that I was not going anywhere while they figured it out.

What I always started with was reminding them: we are here to help. Not help as a slogan. Help as in, let's sit down right now, and tell me everything, and let's work through this together. That often changed the whole atmosphere of the appointment. Someone who had walked in frustrated and maybe even a little angry would start to soften when they realized they were not being managed or brushed off. They mattered. Their concerns mattered. Each concern was valid, I always told them that — from hearing a loved one's voice to hearing the television, whatever it was, it was real and worth taking seriously.

The story that stays with me most is a young woman, forty-two years old. She had been in a car accident and lost much of her hearing very suddenly — not gradually the way it happens for most of our patients, but all at once, just taken from her. She was still working, still raising her life, and she had a lot of skepticism about whether she really needed hearing aids at all. She had bought our smallest pair, because discretion mattered to her at her age, and after a couple of visits she came back still unconvinced. She sat down and I could tell she was about to talk herself out of keeping them.

Before she could, she said: "I want to tell you something first. I decided I'm keeping them."

I said, "Tell me why."

She said, "I didn't know there were birds in my backyard." She paused, and then she started to smile. "And I didn't know that when you open a can of soda it makes a popping sound. My husband thought it was the funniest thing. I kept popping cans just to hear it."

And then she said the thing I have never forgotten: "I decided I need to start being among the living again."

Forty-two years old. Among the living.

That is the cost of giving up too soon. Not the price of the hearing aids — the cost of going back to a quiet world that you do not even realize is quiet anymore, because the brain forgets. Hearing robs you of your independence when it goes. It makes the world smaller without you even noticing, because the loss is so gradual. And when someone has a sudden loss at forty-two, the shrinking is not gradual at all — it is a cliff. She had been on one side of that cliff and this was her way back.

That young woman kept her hearing aids. She came back for cleanings and checkups for years. Eventually she bought a second pair as time went on and technology improved. She became one of the people I thought about when things felt hard, when a return didn't save and I was sitting with that feeling of having tried my best and not been enough. Her story reminded me that the ones who stay in the journey — the ones you hold on for one more visit, one more adjustment, one more patient conversation about what they really want to hear — those are the ones who write the good endings.

The practical truth about saves is simpler than people think. You have to listen more than you talk. You have to resist the urge to immediately defend the product or explain the fitting rationale or cite the audiogram. All of that can come later. First: what are they actually feeling? What is the one real concern that is driving them toward that white envelope?

Once you have that, you can work. Maybe it is the volume in a specific situation — you can adjust that. Maybe it is the way their own voice sounds — you can explain the occlusion effect and give them the talking-out-loud exercise again, and most of the time that is enough if they do it consistently. Maybe it is an expectation mismatch: they paid a significant amount and expected to hear like a twenty-year-old and they are discovering that amplification is not the same as restored youth. That is a harder conversation, but it is an honest one, and honest conversations earn the patient's trust even when they are uncomfortable.

I always did my best. Sometimes a return did not save. I would not pretend otherwise. But I knew — and I still know — that if you lead with caring, if you listen before you explain, if you treat each person as if you might be the only person they talk to all week, you give the journey its best possible chance.

And the journey is worth fighting for. Because on the other side of that white envelope is a woman hearing birds in her backyard for the first time in years, laughing because a soda can sounds like a small miracle.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

When a patient returns a hearing aid, they are not returning the hearing aid. They are returning you. That is the hardest thing for a new clinician to absorb, but it is the truth. They are saying: I no longer believe you can help me. That means the save is not about the product — it is about rebuilding the trust and reminding them why they came in the first place. Every return is a chance to prove you meant what you said when you told them you would do whatever it took to help them hear. Kim was extraordinary at this. She had a gift for making people feel heard before they ever described what they were not hearing. That is the whole game. If they feel heard by you, they trust you to help them hear again.

---

**The principle**

A patient who comes back with a hearing aid to return is not making a statement about the technology — they are making a statement about their belief in the journey. Your job is to listen until you find the single most important issue, address that issue specifically, and reconnect them to the reason they started in the first place. The patient who walks back out with their hearing aids is the patient who got one more person genuinely invested in their success.

---

**The AI-Layer**

The white envelope moment is not the first warning sign — it is the last. By that point, the patient has already had two or three visits where something was quietly not working and no one caught it. An AI-assisted patient lifecycle system can flag early-warning signals: a patient who misses a follow-up appointment, a COSI goal that has not improved after two sessions, a response to a check-in message that sounds discouraged. Getting ahead of the return — reaching out before the white envelope shows up — is where AI tools have the most leverage. The conversation Kim describes will still be human. But the trigger that makes sure it happens in time can be automated.

---

**For the Professional:** The save is an act of care, not a sales move. When you sit with a frustrated patient and hear them out fully before you say a word in defense of anything, you are demonstrating exactly the kind of commitment that changes a skeptic into a believer. The journey is hard. Being the person who stays with them through the hard part is the whole point.
