# Chapter 2: Folding Mailers & Building Hearing Aids

*(Mark)*

---

> "I learned right away that you cannot sell, manufacture, fit, and service all in one — because you're gonna run out of time, and you will then stop selling, and then you have to fill up the funnel again."
> — Mark Moore

---

The way I got into this business was not exactly a straight line.

When I met Kim and was introduced to her family, I learned that her father, Marvin, had built his whole life around hearing aids. He'd started with his brother — going out to convalescent hospitals, selling out of the trunk of a car, long before licensing existed in California or most other states. By the time I came along, he had five hearing aid stores. He asked me one day, straight out, whether I'd ever thought about getting into the hearing aid business. I told him I hadn't. His response has stayed with me a long time: "The hearing aid business has been very, very good to us."

I had a business degree and a background in golf course management. I'd tried real estate for a while, but interest rates were going through the roof and sales were slow. The hearing aid business started to seem a little more interesting. Still, I didn't want to work for my father-in-law — that felt like a complicated way to start. Kim was already working for her uncle, about 35 minutes away, doing the reception and the bookkeeping. So I went to talk to the uncle, and we made a deal: I'd come in, train underneath him, and work toward my dispensing license.

What I didn't expect was the back room.

Kim's uncle was a builder. Not a manufacturer in the big-factory sense — but in the literal sense of the word. He had a little shop set up in the back of the office where he built hearing aids himself. He was ordering Electone faceplates — prefab shells, essentially — and then selecting the right circuits for each patient's particular slope and degree of hearing loss. Three-transistor devices. He'd put a few trim pots on each one, little potentiometers you could adjust with a screwdriver, and that was your fitting.

He taught me the whole process. We'd take ear impressions from the patient — that's the male. Then we'd flip it into a female mold and pour acrylic plastic into the cavity. It would come out raw and rough, so we'd run it through something like a pressure cooker crossed with a rock tumbler to smooth the edges after we'd trimmed them down. Then you'd solder in the receivers, wire in the microphones, change out the wax guards, adjust the potentiometers. I learned to repair them too — swapping receivers, troubleshooting circuits, the whole thing.

The plan was that once I learned the building side, the uncle would put me on the road. I'd take a portable audiometer into people's homes, test them at the dining room table, sell them, and then come back to the shop and build what I'd sold.

For a while, it worked. I was going out on leads from the mailers — the same concept Kim had grown up folding at the kitchen table, except now it was a plastic model of a hearing aid that we'd send out in response to cards people returned or calls they made. I'd go deliver the model, test the person right there in their home, and more often than not walk out with a sale. I sold eight or nine hearing aids in a single week while the uncle was away on a hunting trip. I was feeling pretty good about myself.

Then the math caught up with me.

Here's what happened. The more I sold, the more I had to build. Every sale meant another trip back to the shop, another day or two at the bench before I could deliver. After delivery came a follow-up visit. And if something wasn't right, I'd have to go back, collect the aid, repair it, and make another delivery trip. I was also building a client base — people who'd bought from me and needed service. All of that took time. Time I wasn't spending selling.

I got to about 20 units sold in one month. That sounds good until you realize it took me another month and a half just to build, deliver, and follow up on those 20 units. During all that time, I wasn't selling anything new. The funnel ran dry. And then I had to start filling it up all over again.

I figured this out somewhere around months two or three, and it became one of the most useful lessons I've ever learned: you cannot sell and manufacture at the same time. Not if you want to do either one well. It's like hiring a car salesman and then, every time he sells a car, sending him under the hood to fix it. Pretty soon he's not selling anymore — he's just repairing. And then the pipeline is empty and you have to start all over.

The hearing aid is not a widget. The relationship is the product. But even the best relationship doesn't help anybody if you don't have time to go build it.

Once I got my license — about seven or eight months into the process — I went back to the uncle and told him I wanted to work in the office. I didn't want to keep doing home visits indefinitely. He pushed back on that, understandably enough: he'd wanted me to be an additional profit center for him, not competition for his own chair. We worked out an arrangement to see if I could add to the practice without cutting into his numbers. Within a couple of months, I'd roughly doubled production. He was doing 25 to 30 units a month. We were doing 50 to 60.

He wanted to open another location — 45 or 50 miles away — and put me there. I thought about it seriously and said no. A brand-new office with no established patient base and no clear marketing plan wasn't a gamble I wanted to take right out of the gate. I'd seen how much an established patient base was worth. I didn't want to start from scratch somewhere strange.

So that was the end of that chapter.

But the lesson I carried out of it shaped everything we built afterward. When Kim and I eventually opened our own practice, we didn't build hearing aids in the back room. We ordered from manufacturers. We sold, we fit, we serviced — and we hired people to do the parts of the job that needed dedicated attention, so that no single person was stretched across more roles than they could fill well. The dispensing stayed with the dispensers. The front desk stayed with professionals who understood what the front desk actually does. The books stayed with people who knew the books.

The trap I walked into working for the uncle was trying to do everything myself. It felt like efficiency. It was actually the opposite. Every hour I spent at the bench was an hour I wasn't spending with a patient. And the patient is where the work happens. That's where the connection is. That's where the reason for all of it lives.

Later in my career, when we had multiple offices running from California to Florida, I thought back to that shop in the back of the uncle's office a lot. Building hearing aids by hand, one at a time, circuit by circuit. It was a strange and specific education. I understood the device in a way that a lot of dispensers didn't, because I'd literally assembled it. But more than the technical knowledge, that time taught me what the limits of one person actually are — and why, if you want to grow, you have to be willing to let go of the parts of the work that aren't yours to carry.

The mailer starts the relationship. The front desk continues it. The dispenser earns the patient's trust. The technician maintains it. When everyone does their part, the whole thing moves. When one person tries to do all of it, the funnel runs dry.

I learned that the hard way, one acrylic mold at a time.

**From the other chair**

*(Kim)*

While Mark was learning to build hearing aids in the back room, I was learning the books on the front end of the same building. My mom had shown me that the bookkeeping side of the business made everything else possible — that someone had to watch the numbers so the dispenser could stay in the room with the patient. What I saw in those early years is that "support work" is not small work. Running the front desk, keeping the accounting clean, managing the schedule — all of that is what lets the hearing part happen. The selling depends on the caring, and the caring depends on someone keeping the whole operation running underneath it. I never thought of the back-office work as less important. I thought of it as the part no one sees until it breaks.

---

**The principle**

Doing more things does not produce more results. Every function added to an already full role takes time away from the function that was already there. In a practice where the relationship is the product, the dispenser's most valuable resource is undivided attention — and anything that competes for that attention costs more than it appears to save.

---

**The AI-Layer**

The back-office time that threatened to swallow Mark's selling hours — building, delivering, repairing, scheduling follow-ups — is exactly where today's AI tools are most useful. Automated recall sequences, AI-assisted scheduling, digital intake forms, and smart CRM reminders handle the administrative repetition that used to eat the dispenser's day. The human stays in the room with the patient. The system handles what happens in between. This is not about replacing the relationship. It's about protecting the time in which the relationship gets built.

---

**For the Professional**

Support roles exist to protect the patient-facing relationship, not to fill a seat. When the front desk, the bookkeeping, and the scheduling run smoothly, the dispenser can give every patient their full attention. That full attention is what turns a first appointment into a lifelong patient.
