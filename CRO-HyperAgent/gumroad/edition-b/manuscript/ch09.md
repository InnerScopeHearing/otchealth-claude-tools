# Chapter 9: The Testing Chair

*(Kim)*

---

> "To me, it was the highlight of my day — between working the front desk and testing, both were my highlights."

---

A lot of people in this business will tell you that testing is the boring part. You put someone in a booth, you run the tones, you do the speech testing, you print the audiogram, and then the real work begins. The testing is just the setup. It is procedural. It is administrative.

I never saw it that way. Not for one day in the years I was doing it.

For me, walking someone into the testing booth was the most important thing I did. It was the moment when everything that had happened before — the phone call, the paperwork, the coffee in the waiting room, the conversation at the front desk — it all came together. This person was sitting down and saying, in a way that was much braver than they probably realized: I am willing to find out the truth about my hearing.

That is not a small thing. For most of the patients I saw, this was the moment they had been putting off for years. Their spouse had been asking them to come in. Their children had been after them. They had been managing and compensating and pretending that everyone else just mumbled. And now they were here, and I was the person on the other side of that booth window.

I always wanted to make sure I handled that right.

---

The first thing I would say, every time, before I ever started the test, was: "Don't shoot the messenger." I said it with a smile, because I wanted them to relax, but I meant it sincerely. I told them I was always hoping — genuinely hoping — that they did not have a hearing loss. I wanted everybody to walk in and walk out with normal hearing. I knew most of them would not. I had already read the paperwork, I already had a sense of what the results were likely to show. But I wanted them to know that I was on their side, that I was not looking for a reason to sell them something, that I would be just as happy to say "everything looks fine, come back in a year" as I would be to say "we found something, and here is what we can do."

That distinction matters more than people understand. Patients come in already braced. They have heard stories about high-pressure sales environments. Some of them have been to other offices and left feeling like they were being pushed toward a purchase. I wanted them to feel that what was happening in that booth was pure and honest — that the result was going to be whatever it was, and I was going to tell them the truth about it, and we were going to figure out together what to do next.

I had tested over 10,000 people by the time we wound down our practice. And I still miss it. I genuinely do. There was something about that moment of learning who someone was, right at the beginning, that I never got tired of.

---

I started giving out Hershey bars.

I know that sounds like a small thing, and maybe it is. But I cannot tell you how many times that one gesture cracked open a conversation that would not have happened otherwise.

Here is how it started: I was always looking for ways to make people feel comfortable before the test, to take the edge off, to remind them that this was a human interaction and not a medical procedure. And I found that giving someone something — a small, tangible thing to hold — changed the energy in the room almost immediately. People would look down at the candy bar, and something in their face would shift. It had to be a Hershey bar specifically. I tried other things. It did not work the same way. There is something about that particular bar, the way it looks, the smell of it, that takes people somewhere else instantly. I think it is because Hershey has been around so long that almost everyone has a memory attached to it.

One gentleman stands out in my memory. I gave him the bar and he looked at it for a moment, and then he looked up at me and said, "I sure wish my brother was here."

I said, "Why is that?"

He said, "I had a twin brother. He passed away. We used to share our Hershey bar together. I really wish he was here so I could share this one with him."

I did not have words for that. I did not need words. That moment was not about hearing aids. It was not about the test. It was just two people in a room, and one of them had just trusted the other with something precious. He told me about his brother, about what they were like growing up, about what sharing that chocolate meant to them as boys.

That is what the Hershey bar did. It did not just welcome people — it opened them. Some patients shared theirs with the spouse who came along. Some saved it for a grandchild. Some told me they were going to eat it in the car on the way home. Every person who looked at that bar had a story, and often they told it to me, and then I knew them — not just their audiogram, but them.

Some of those people bought hearing aids. Some of them didn't need them yet. Some of them came back six months later when the time was right. It did not matter. I had given them something, they had given me something back, and the relationship was already real before the test even started.

---

Testing, to me, was where caring happened at the most fundamental level.

I learned things in that booth that I never could have learned at the desk. I could see how patients responded to what they were hearing — not just whether they pressed the button or not, but how their whole body responded. Some people would tense up when they realized the tones were getting quieter, like they were fighting to hear them. Some would have a look of concentration so intense it was almost painful. Some would relax when a tone was clearly audible, and you could watch the relief move across their face.

And then when I brought them back out and sat across from them with the audiogram — that was another kind of conversation. The graph on the paper is just lines and numbers. My job was to make it a story about their life. Not a frightening story, not a catastrophizing one, but an honest one: here is where your hearing is, here is what it means for the things you have told me matter to you, and here is what we can do.

I always told them that taking the test was the second step toward better hearing. The first step was walking in the door. That mattered. People do not give themselves enough credit for doing hard things, and coming into a hearing office — admitting, at least privately, that something might be wrong — is a hard thing for a lot of people. I wanted them to know I noticed, and that I appreciated the courage it took.

---

I could figure people out pretty quickly, just by listening to them for a few minutes before the test. I tried to teach that to other people over the years, and I am not sure I was ever able to fully explain how it worked. It was not a technique exactly. It was just — paying attention. Listening to how they talked about their life, what they worried about, what made them laugh. Reading the posture, the energy, the small things.

One of the things I always tried to do was understand what was most important to that particular person in their particular life. Not what mattered to hearing-aid patients in general. What mattered to this person, in this room, today. Because if I could help them reconnect with the specific thing they had lost — the grandchildren playing in the yard, the baseball game on television, the voice of the spouse across the dinner table — then the hearing aids were not a product. They were a gift. And I knew I had the gift of hearing to offer. Whether they chose to accept it was up to them.

---

There is something I want to say to anyone who is doing the testing in your office and thinking it is the lesser part of the job.

It is not. It is the foundation. Without an honest, careful, compassionate test, you cannot fit anyone well. And without a testing experience that makes the patient feel seen and safe and valued, you are going to lose people before you ever get to show them what you can do. The test is where trust is built or broken. It is where the relationship begins.

I have heard people say they find the testing tedious. I understand that. Running the same frequency tests over and over, adjusting, repeating, documenting — it can feel rote. But I would challenge anyone who feels that way to look at the person on the other side of the glass, not at the machine. Because that person is doing something brave. They are finding out the truth. And you are the one they are trusting to deliver it with honesty and care.

That is not tedious. That is an honor.

**From the other chair**

*Mark* — Kim is right that the testing is where trust begins, and I want to add one thing from the clinical side: the audiogram is not just a result. It is a teaching tool. When I sat down with patients after a test, I used that graph to explain *why* they were having the specific difficulties they described — why they could hear someone speaking but not understand them, why the television was clear but conversation in a restaurant was impossible. High-frequency loss means consonants blur together. You hear the vowels, you lose the S's and F's and TH's. The audiogram shows them that they are not imagining it, and that what they are experiencing has a specific, understandable explanation. That shift — from feeling confused and a little embarrassed to feeling informed — is one of the most important things that can happen in a hearing office.

---

**The principle**

The testing appointment is not a prelude to the real work — it is where the real relationship begins. A patient who feels welcomed, honestly informed, and genuinely cared for during the test is a patient who will trust your recommendation, stay the course when the adjustment period is hard, and send their friends and family to you when the time comes. The Hershey bar, the "don't shoot the messenger," the listening — none of it is small. It is all the work.

---

**The AI-Layer**

At-home hearing screening tools — simple online tests, app-based checks, screening questionnaires — can function as the no-pressure first step that gives people a way to begin acknowledging their hearing before they ever walk through your door. AI can help you deliver these tools, follow up on results with empathy rather than urgency, and create a warm pathway from "I wonder if I should get tested" to "I have an appointment." Done right, this extends the reach of the care Kim describes — not replacing the human moment in the booth, but creating more opportunities for that moment to happen.

---

**For the Professional**

You are not just running frequencies and printing a report. You are meeting someone at one of the harder moments of their adult life — the moment they stop pretending everything is fine. Show up for that moment with your whole self. A candy bar, a kind word, an unhurried presence — these are the things patients remember long after they forget the decibels on the chart.
