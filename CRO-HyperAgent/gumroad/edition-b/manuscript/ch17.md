# Chapter 17: Winning Without Selling

*(Kim)*

---

> "If you truly care about each patient, you will never come across as being pushy."
> — Kim Moore

---

People ask me sometimes how I managed to help patients through the whole hearing journey — from the first nervous phone call to the day they walked out with hearing aids and actually wore them — without ever really thinking of myself as a salesperson. My honest answer is that I was never trying to sell anything. I was trying to take care of people. And when you genuinely take care of people, the outcome usually takes care of itself.

The advice I always gave to any front desk person or dispenser who came to me and said they hated the idea of selling was simple: treat each patient the way you would treat your parents or your grandparents. If you had an older parent who was struggling to hear — who was getting left out of family conversations, who was turning up the television until everyone else in the house was frustrated, who was nodding along in doctor's appointments and pretending to understand when they had missed half of what was said — you would not walk away from that person and say, "Well, she's not ready to commit to anything today." You would sit down with her. You would explain things slowly. You would call her back to check in. You would hold her hand through the scary part.

That is the posture. If you bring that posture into every single patient interaction, you will never be pushy. Pushy comes from pressure — from wanting the outcome for yourself more than you want it for the patient. But if your focus is entirely on their well-being, there is no pressure. There is just care. Patients feel the difference immediately.

---

The first moment that matters is the phone call. No one who calls a hearing aid practice is calling because they feel great about the situation. They are calling because something is wrong and they are just now, finally, doing something about it. That is often an act of courage for them. Our job at the front desk — my job — was to make sure that when they hung up the phone, they felt glad they called.

That means you answer with energy. You are warm. You thank them for reaching out. You make an appointment as easy as possible. You give them a small piece of encouragement — you validate their decision to come in — before you even let them hang up. The first step of the caring process begins the moment they call. Not when they walk in the door, not when they sit down in the testing chair. The moment they call.

When they came through the door, I made sure they had what they needed to feel comfortable. Coffee, cookies, something to drink. A warm room, not a cold waiting area that felt like a waiting room. I would help them fill out paperwork if they needed it. I listened when they wanted to talk. And most of the time, they wanted to talk. This age group — the 65-and-up patients who were most of our practice — many of them were lonely. They might have been the only person they spoke to all week. Or close to it. When they walked into our office and felt like someone was genuinely happy to see them, that meant something real to them.

People only want to know that they matter to you in this world. I learned that not from a textbook but from years of sitting across from patients and watching their faces change when they realized I was actually listening. Not waiting for my next talking point. Listening. That shift — the moment a patient stops being defensive and starts being present — is the moment the relationship becomes something real.

---

There is no shortcut to that. You cannot fake it consistently enough for it to work. Patients who have been around for seven or eight decades have very good instincts about when someone is genuinely interested in them versus when someone is performing interest in order to get somewhere. If you are going through the motions — asking the questions on the checklist but not actually caring about the answers — they will know. And if they know, you have lost them.

But here is the wonderful thing about genuinely caring: you do not have to manage it. It does not take energy to sustain it. When you actually like people, when you are actually curious about where they came from and what their life has been, when you actually want them to hear their grandchildren's voices — caring is effortless. It does not feel like work. It feels like the reason you came in that day.

I used to hear stories all day long at the front desk. I would sit and listen to patients tell me about their careers, their travels, their families, their histories. I had a colonel who came in regularly, and I always addressed him by his rank because he had earned it and it mattered to him. I had veterans who would show me their tattoos and tell me the stories behind them. I had doctors and postal workers and retired teachers. Each one of them brought their whole life through our door, and I made it a point to know their stories, so that the next time they came in, we could pick up exactly where we had left off. I was proud of that. I felt like I had traveled the world from behind that front desk.

That investment — that genuine curiosity and care — is what brought people back. Not a promotional mailer. Not a discount offer. The fact that they felt known in our office, and that they knew someone there would be happy to see them.

---

The approach to actually helping a patient stay on their hearing journey — especially when it got hard — followed the same principle. When a patient came back struggling, or came back with the white envelope in their hand wanting to return the hearing aids, the right response was never to argue with them or push back or quote statistics at them. It was to listen. To find the one thing that was bothering them most at that moment and focus there. Because most of the time, they were not truly returning the hearing aids. They were returning to us because they needed help, and they did not know how to ask for it.

I knew a secret about hearing aids: I knew they worked. The patients did not know that yet. They were in the middle of the adaptation period — that first difficult month when everything sounds too loud or too strange and the brain is still trying to catch up. I had seen enough patients come through it to know what was waiting on the other side. My job was to hold them through the hard part with enough honesty and enough warmth that they stayed on the road long enough to find out.

Sometimes I would lose that. I would be honest with a patient — tell them plainly that yes, they did need the hearing aids, that they needed to stay the course — and they would get upset and want to return. That hurt, because I knew what I was giving up. Not a transaction. Their hearing. I was giving up someone's chance to hear their loved ones clearly, to feel less alone, to stay engaged in the world around them. That is a hard thing to let go.

But I always tried. I gave my best to every patient, every time. And more often than not, if I had built enough trust — if they felt genuinely cared for rather than managed — they stayed.

---

The most practical advice I can give to anyone who dreads the selling part of this work is this: stop thinking about it as selling. Change the word entirely. You are caring. You are helping. You are walking alongside someone through one of the scarier transitions of their older life — the admission that their hearing has changed, and the willingness to do something about it. That is a big moment for them. Be present for it the way you would want someone to be present for someone you love.

From that posture, being pushy becomes physically impossible. Because pushy means you want something from the patient. Caring means you want something for them.

When you want it for them more than they want it for themselves — when you understand, as I did, that you hold a gift that can give them back their life — the only challenge is having enough patience to wait while they find their way to accepting it. That patience is how you help them say yes to better hearing — not through pressure, but through the kind of steady, honest presence that makes the decision feel safe.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

Kim's instinct — care first, let the rest follow — is something I always understood but articulated differently. My version is this: people don't want to be sold. But they very much want to buy. The difference is who the decision belongs to. When you walk in with a canned presentation designed to steer someone toward a conclusion you already decided on, they feel it. The walls go up. But when you walk in genuinely curious about their situation — when your questions are real questions, when their answers actually change what you do next — the patient feels like the decision is coming from them. They're buying, not being sold. That distinction, which Kim lives instinctively, is the whole game. You win not by pressing harder, but by making yourself someone worth trusting.

---

**The principle**

Genuine care and effective patient communication are not in tension — they are the same thing. When your orientation is entirely toward the patient's well-being, the transactional pressure disappears and the relationship becomes real. Treating every patient the way you would treat a beloved parent or grandparent is not a sentiment; it is a method. It works because it is honest, and patients can feel the honesty.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Every automated touchpoint in your practice — appointment reminders, follow-up texts, satisfaction surveys — passes or fails the same test Kim describes: does this feel like someone who cares, or does it feel like someone trying to close something? AI-driven communications can be written and sequenced to read like a caring check-in rather than a marketing message. The standard is simple: before any automated message goes out, ask whether Kim would be comfortable saying it in person. If the answer is no, rewrite it. The technology can scale caring communication — but the human who designed it has to mean it first.

---

**For the Professional**

You already know how to care. Trust that. When you treat each patient like someone you love, you are already doing the most important part of this work. Everything else — the paperwork, the follow-ups, the journey — flows from that.
