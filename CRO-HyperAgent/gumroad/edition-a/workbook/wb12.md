## Chapter 12 Workbook — The Honest Truth

**The takeaway:** Honesty with patients is not a risk to the relationship — it is the foundation of it. Telling someone the truth about their hearing, about what the adaptation process actually feels like, and about what to expect and when, builds the trust that keeps them in the journey through the hardest weeks. A patient who trusts you enough to hear the honest answer will stay. A patient who is told only what they want to hear will return the devices and walk away — permanently.

### 3 — The Drill: Build Your Honest Adaptation Script

This is the speech you give at the fitting appointment — before the patient walks out the door — that sets honest expectations for what the first month will feel like. It is not a disclaimer. It is a form of care: giving someone the truth in advance so they are not blindsided, and telling them clearly that you will be there.

Write your version using the blanks below.

**My honest adaptation setup — said at the fitting, before they leave:**

"I want to tell you something important before you go home today, because I want you to be ready for it. The first few weeks are going to feel __________. That is normal. Your own voice may sound __________, and sounds you have not heard in a long time — like __________ — are suddenly going to be very present. That is not a problem. That is your brain __________

__________. There will be a moment — and I have seen this hundreds of times — when it all starts to come together. I need you to be patient with yourself until we get there. And if you hit a hard day, I want you to __________

__________ before you decide anything. I've got you."

**Model line from the manuscript (Kim, Ch. 12):**

> "I know the hearing aids work — they don't, yet."

Say your version aloud. It should feel like something you would actually say to a patient you genuinely care about — plain, warm, and honest.
