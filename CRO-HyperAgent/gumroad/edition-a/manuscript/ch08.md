# Chapter 8: Thinkers, Knowers, Feelers

*(Mark)*

---

> "You have to become a master communicator — and the trick is developing your own awareness first."

---

There is a question I used to ask myself at the very start of every appointment, before I said a word about hearing, before I pulled out the audiogram, before I mentioned a single product. I would look at the person across from me and think: *how does this person make decisions?*

Not: what do they want to hear? Not: what objection are they going to raise? Not even: do they need a hearing aid?

*How do they process information?*

Because here is what I learned over 23 years and more than 10,000 fittings: you can have the right device, the right price, the right hearing assessment — and still walk them right out the door by presenting everything the wrong way. It does not matter how good your solution is if it is delivered in a language that person cannot receive.

---

I want to walk you through something I came to think of as the most important communication framework I ever learned. I did not invent these categories — they come from learning modalities research — but I applied them in the exam room and they changed the way I worked with people permanently. There are three types, and every person sitting across from you falls into one of them as their dominant mode.

**Thinkers.** You know a Thinker when you see one. They walk in with a folder. Not a brochure, not a single printout — a folder. Inside that folder is information they downloaded from the internet, quotes from other providers, maybe notes from two or three other hearing tests they have already had. They have a list of questions, and it is not a short list. Thinkers are people who make decisions using their brains, and their brain requires data. They arm themselves with facts before they will commit to anything. You cannot give them too much information — or so it seems. The truth is, a Thinker can never accumulate quite enough data, because no dataset ever feels fully complete.

Early in my career, before I really understood this, I had an appointment with an engineer. He was asking me about the components of the hearing aids — what was inside them, how they were built, how they processed sound. I started explaining, which seemed like the natural thing to do. And then he looked at me and said something I have never forgotten: "You know, these components sound like things I could order myself. Little capacitors, some circuitry. I could probably build one of these in my backyard for about fifty dollars."

I had no good answer for that. I did not know what to say. I probably stumbled through something, and I am fairly certain that man did not buy that day.

If I had understood Thinker modality back then, I would have gone about it very differently. I would not have gotten into a technical comparison I could not win. I would have asked him: "If you built one yourself, would it actually help you hear?" Redirect to the outcome, not the engineering. Meet him in his logical frame, but steer the logic toward what matters.

Thinkers are honestly one of the hardest to close, because there is no end to the information they can request. They will keep you for three hours when a normal fitting conversation takes ninety minutes. They want factory specs. They want the research behind the noise reduction algorithms. And if you are not careful, you walk out of that conversation having answered every question brilliantly and still having made no sale, because data alone does not close — trust does, and with Thinkers, trust is built by demonstrating that you know as much as they do, not more.

**Knowers.** These are my gut-decision people. They take in some information — they do not need to be data-dumped on — and then they just know. They are intuitive. They might not even be able to explain how they reached their decision, but they are sure it is right. Knowers use bullet points, not spreadsheets. Give them the highlights. Give them the three most important things you need them to understand, and stop. If you keep going, if you pile on additional information because you are proud of what you know, you will lose them.

Here is the key thing about Knowers: they are proving to themselves whether the solution will work for them, and they do it through a quick demonstration or a short, punchy fact. Show it. Demonstrate it. Let them experience something rather than read about it. Then stop and let the gut do its work.

Roughly 30% of the people you will sit across from are Knowers. They are actually the easiest close if you read the buyer correctly, because they decide fast once they have what they need. The trap is treating them like Thinkers and burying them in detail. That is the fastest way to make a Knower's gut say no.

**Feelers.** Here is the big number: roughly 55 to 60% of the people in your chair are Feelers. More than half. Feelers make decisions with their hearts. Thinkers use their heads, Knowers use their gut — Feelers feel their way to a decision. Some of them will tell you they physically experience a sensation in their body when they make a good choice. They need to feel comfortable, not just intellectually convinced.

Feelers do not care about the technical specifications. Channels, bands, processing speed — that information bores them and they will disengage if you lead with it. What they want is a story. Not a brochure, not a clinical explanation — a story. They want to know what it is going to feel like, what it is going to do for their life, how they are going to experience the world differently.

I used to explain it this way: if you were selling a convertible to a Feeler, you would not hand them a spec sheet. You would say, "Get in. Let's put the top down. Can you picture yourself on a highway, the wind in your hair, your favorite song on the radio? Can you feel that?" That is the Feeler experience. They want the movie of their own life, rewritten with better hearing.

For a Feeler in a hearing office, you might say: "Picture your grandson in the backyard, calling to you. 'Grandma, look at me, look at me.' Right now, you're missing that. He runs over and tugs your sleeve because you didn't hear. With these, he can call from across the yard and you hear it. You don't miss that moment." Tell the story. Make it specific to them. Make it emotional.

---

Now here is the part that most people miss, and it is crucial: **we all have elements of all three modalities. You have a dominant one and a secondary one.**

The dominant one drives the decision. But the secondary one is your backup — and it is your way in when you cannot get through the first.

Remember that engineer? If I had recognized he was a dominant Thinker, and I could not reach him through data, I would have tried to find his secondary modality. Maybe it was a Knower — give him three clean, logical bullet points and stop. Maybe it was a Feeler — find the emotional content of his hearing loss and speak to that. I did not know how to do that then. I know now.

The other thing most people do not think about is their own modality. If you are a natural Feeler, and most people are, you will tend to default to stories and emotional language. That works beautifully with 55% of the people you meet. But when an engineer walks in with a folder full of printouts, your Feeler instinct will get you in trouble. You have to be able to switch. You have to put down your story and pick up the spec sheet, even if it is not how you naturally communicate.

I call this being a chameleon. A master communicator is not someone who has one great way of explaining things. A master communicator is someone who can read the buyer and immediately adapt. You cannot have a canned presentation. The principles of what you need to convey are the same every time — you genuinely care, you are going to help them hear, you will stay with them through the process. But the *way* you deliver those principles has to flex to match the person.

---

So how do you figure out which type you are dealing with? You listen. You ask open questions and you pay attention to how they answer. A Thinker will start telling you what they researched and what they found. A Knower will get to the point quickly and tell you what they think they need. A Feeler will tell you a story about how not hearing is affecting their family, their relationships, their sense of self.

You also watch what they bring in, how they sit, how they talk. And you do this from the very first moment — during the intake, before the test, while you are walking them to the booth. By the time you sit down with the audiogram, you should already have a working hypothesis about their dominant modality.

I used to map this out in my head while I was in the testing booth. The spouse might be in the room watching the speech test, which is a useful technique in itself — hearing someone struggle to repeat words back makes the case without you having to say a thing. But while I was running the test, I was also thinking: who is this person, and how am I going to speak to them when we sit back down?

Read the buyer, close the buyer. Know the principles, know what you need to accomplish. But deliver it in the language that person can receive.

---

One last thing: this framework does not only apply to patients. It applies to spouses, to adult children in the room, to your own staff, to your multi-office team. When I was running offices across California and Florida, the same modality awareness that helped me communicate with patients helped me communicate with the people I was managing. A Thinker on your staff needs data and clear reasoning for why you are asking them to do something. A Feeler on your staff needs to feel that the culture of the office is warm and that what they do matters to people's lives. A Knower on your staff just needs you to cut to what you need and let them figure out how to get there.

Good communication enables strong relationships. It is that simple, and it is that hard.

**From the other chair**

*Kim* — I never thought about it in those technical terms while I was working, but I realize now that what I was doing was reading people the moment they walked in. I would listen to how they described themselves, what they were worried about, what stories they told. Some patients would come in and want every piece of information I could give them. Others just needed me to hold their hand and tell them it was going to be okay. I think I gravitated naturally toward the Feeler approach, because for me it was never about the device — it was always about the person. But I did learn over the years to give some patients more information and less comfort, and some patients more comfort and less information. You just have to pay attention to who is actually in front of you.

---

**The principle**

People do not all receive information the same way, and knowing your patient's dominant decision-making modality — Thinker, Knower, or Feeler — is as important as knowing their audiogram. Roughly 15% are Thinkers who need data, 30% are Knowers who need highlights, and 55% are Feelers who need stories. A master communicator does not have one great pitch; they have the flexibility to switch their approach in the moment based on who they are serving.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Modality-matched communication is exactly what AI-assisted tools can help you scale. Email sequences, pre-visit content, and follow-up messages can be drafted in three versions — data-rich for Thinkers, bullet-pointed for Knowers, story-forward for Feelers — and the right version can be delivered based on what you learned in intake or what behavioral signals the patient shows online. This does not replace the human judgment of reading a room; it extends your ability to speak the right language at every touchpoint before and after the appointment, not just during it.

---

**For the Closer**

When you identify the modality, you identify the path to yes. Stop data-dumping on Feelers. Stop storytelling to Thinkers. Read the room, match the mode, and close in the language they actually use to say yes.
