# Chapter 15 — Why They Came Back

*(Kim)*

> "I've traveled around the world hundreds of times in their stories, and I never left the office — and I was proud of that."

---

People ask me what kept patients coming back year after year, sometimes for decades. What made someone, after they had bought their hearing aids and finished the follow-up program and were doing well, still show up every few months just to have their devices cleaned and say hello? What made them bring their sister in, or their neighbor down the street, or that gentleman from their church who kept saying his wife kept asking him to get his hearing checked?

I think it is simpler than most people expect. They came back because someone listened to them.

I have the gift of gab. I always have. It is not something I learned from a training manual or practiced in front of a mirror — it is just how I am wired. I can talk to anyone, anywhere. I talk to people in the grocery store line, in the parking lot, in the waiting room at the dentist. I am genuinely curious about people — where they came from, what they have done, what they care about, what they have seen. And that curiosity, when you carry it into a practice, becomes something patients can feel the moment they walk through the door.

When a patient would come in, I would listen. Really listen. Not the kind of listening where you are waiting for your turn to speak or running through the next item on a checklist. The kind where you lean in a little and ask a follow-up question because you actually want to know the answer. Patients would tell me stories about their lives, and I would remember those stories. So the next time they came in — six months later, a year later — I could pick up exactly where we had left off. That is not a trick. That is just caring enough to hold onto what someone tells you.

I want to tell you about the Colonel.

He had been a patient for years, a retired military officer, and he was just a delight — dignified, warm, with a wry sense of humor and the kind of bearing that you notice immediately when someone walks into a room. I always called him Colonel. Whatever name might be on his chart, to me and to everyone in our office, he was the Colonel. He would come in and we would talk, and he had stories. Stories about his service, about the places he had been, about the things he had seen. I always honored that. If I found out someone had served, they became that to me — their rank, their role, the part of their life they had given to something larger than themselves. We had so many veterans come through our doors over the years, and I loved every one of them. They had so much to say if you would sit still long enough to hear it.

The Colonel was not unusual in that way. He was just one of the most vivid examples of something I noticed over and over across twenty-plus years of doing this work: the patients in this age group — most of our patients, the 65 and older crowd, and really down to 55 and younger too — they are lonely. Not always dramatically, not in a way they would necessarily say out loud, but quietly. Their kids are grown and gone. Their friends from work have scattered. Their spouses, some of them, have died. And when you are that person and you cannot hear well, the loneliness doubles, because the world becomes smaller and softer and harder to navigate.

So when they come into your office and someone actually sits down with them and wants to know about the tattoo on their arm or the trip they took last spring or the grandchild who just started walking — that matters enormously. Their stories mattered. They wanted someone to know that their stories mattered.

I have thought about it this way: I traveled around the world hundreds of times in those stories. I heard about places I will never go, lives I could not imagine, moments of grief and joy and ordinary Tuesday mornings that somehow meant everything. And I never left the office. I was proud of that. Proud that someone could walk into our lobby on a Wednesday morning and feel, for the length of that appointment, like the most interesting person in the room. Because they were. Every single one of them was.

The coffee and donuts helped too. I know that sounds small, but hear me out.

In our first office in Woodland, California, we always had coffee and donuts out in the lobby. Always. It was not a special promotion or a seasonal thing — it was just there, every day, warm and ready. And people would come in just to sit. Not for an appointment, not for a cleaning. Just to sit in a nice, clean, welcoming lobby and have a cup of coffee and talk to whoever happened to be there. The locals knew us. They would stop in. Some of them would run into someone they recognized from the neighborhood. Two older gentlemen who had not seen each other in years would end up talking for forty-five minutes because they both happened to come in that Tuesday. We let that happen. We wanted that to happen.

Because here is what I understood, maybe better than anything else I learned in all my years in this work: you might be the only person that patient talks to for the whole week. Not an exaggeration — it could literally be true. And if the person who represents better hearing and better connection to the world around them is also the kind of person who genuinely asks how the grandkids are, and actually listens to the answer — that person becomes indispensable to the patient's life. They do not just come back for their hearing aids. They come back for you.

The referrals followed naturally from all of that. Brothers came in because their sisters had talked about us. Neighbors came in because the couple down the street would not stop raving. Friends were sent in, sometimes almost with marching orders. "So-and-so told me to come see you because I need hearing aids, and apparently she will not let it rest until I do." I loved those appointments. Someone in that patient's life had loved them enough to push past their resistance, and someone in our office had given that patient enough confidence to pass along. That is not a marketing strategy. That is a community.

Mark always talks about the forty thousand patients, and yes, the number is extraordinary. But what I want people to understand is that forty thousand is not a statistic. It is forty thousand individual people with names and stories and specific reasons they walked through our door and specific moments when the world got a little bit louder and clearer and more present. Each one of them came back, again and again, because someone in our office treated their story as worth knowing.

I still think about the Colonel. I wonder where he is now, and I hope someone is listening to his stories.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

Building forty thousand patients over those years was not the result of any clever retention scheme, though we certainly had systems. It was the result of Kim. I have watched her work a lobby and I will tell you plainly: she could talk to anyone, and more importantly, she could make anyone feel like they were the only person in the room. That is not a small thing. That is the most powerful referral engine in the world — a patient who feels genuinely valued will do your marketing for you, and they will do it more credibly than anything you could put on a mailer. When people ask how we built a practice that size, the honest answer is: we hired for heart, and Kim set the example for what that meant every single day.

---

**The principle**

Patients return and refer not because of the technology they received but because of how they were made to feel. When you listen to someone's story — not as a professional technique but as a genuine human interest — you become part of their life. And people bring the people they love to the places that have become part of their lives.

---

**The AI-Layer**

Kim's gift was remembering: the Colonel's stories, the grandmother heading to the wedding, the neighbor who finally agreed to come in. Today, a well-configured CRM is Kim's memory at scale. It can store the detail from the last visit — the patient mentioned their son's graduation, their trip to Arizona, the baseball team they've followed for forty years — and surface it at the start of the next appointment so the clinician or front desk can pick up exactly where they left off. The warmth and the curiosity still have to be human. But the remembering can be systematized so that the hundred-and-fiftieth patient gets the same quality of personal recognition as the fifth. And the recall cadence — scheduling a touchpoint call six months after a successful fitting, prompting a patient to bring in a friend they mentioned — that can be automated without ever feeling cold, as long as the automation is grounded in real details about a real person.

---

**For the Closer:** Referrals are the highest-margin leads you will ever work, and they come from one source: a patient who felt like more than a customer. Invest in the relationship after the close to lock the win and drive referrals — not just before it. The patient who comes back for coffee and brings a friend is worth more over a lifetime in revenue than any advertising you will ever run.
