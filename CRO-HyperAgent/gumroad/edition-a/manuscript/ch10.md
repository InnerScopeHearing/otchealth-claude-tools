# Chapter 10: When the Family Is in the Room

*(Kim)*

> "When that patient answered back and kept on talking to their loved one, they had a smile on their face. I knew right then that I had that loved one understanding what a hearing aid can do."

---

There is a moment I have witnessed more times than I can count, and it never gets old. A spouse or a grown child comes into the office not entirely convinced. Maybe they dragged Mom in, or maybe they are quietly skeptical that any of this is worth the money or the fuss. And then something shifts. I have learned to watch for that shift, because when it comes, the whole room changes — and not just for that day. It changes for the rest of that person's life.

Families were always part of how we did things. When patients came in by themselves, we helped them. But when a loved one came along, I treated that as an opportunity, not a complication. The family member is not your obstacle. They are often your greatest ally — if you bring them in properly.

The technique I used most in testing was simple, and I developed it over years of watching what actually worked. We had a master audiometer — basically a way to give someone a preview of what amplification could do, right there in the office, in a quiet room. Not a perfect simulation, but enough to make a point. When a patient came in with their spouse or their adult child, I would set something up before we ever got to paperwork or pricing or any of the business end of the visit.

I would seat the patient, have them turn their back to the door, and close their eyes. Then I would take the loved one — the spouse, the daughter, the son — just outside the room, close enough that their voice could travel through, and ask them to start talking. Not reading from a script. Just talking. Where do you want to go to lunch? What movie do you want to see? What did you think about that show last night? Normal conversation. The kind that gets interrupted a thousand times a day in a family, or goes missed entirely when someone's hearing has slipped away without anyone quite naming it.

And the patient would answer. Not straining, not asking them to repeat, not turning their head and cupping their ear. Just answering, naturally, like the conversation cost them nothing.

I always watched the loved one's face in that moment. I could see the moment it landed. The daughter who came in certain her mother was being taken advantage of would go still. Then her eyes would fill. Then she would look at me, and I would just give her a small nod, because there was nothing I needed to say. She had just heard it herself.

---

I think of one daughter in particular. She came in from back east, where she'd been living while her mother — a retired schoolteacher, one of the sweetest women I ever worked with — stayed in California close to us. This woman had been coming into our office for a while. She had been through a couple of sets of hearing aids over the years, older ones she'd never really worn. We had just fitted her with a new set, and she was doing beautifully. She came in regularly for her follow-ups, and I will tell you honestly, a lot of times we were the only people she saw in a given week. Her family lived far away. We had the coffee and cookies going, the TV on if anyone wanted it, the warm room. She felt comfortable there, and she trusted us.

And then her daughter arrived.

She did not walk in. She stormed in. She had clearly made up her mind before she ever opened the door — that her mother did not need these hearing aids, that she had never worn the old ones, that this was an unnecessary expense, and that she was taking those devices back today. I understood her frustration. From her perspective, living at a distance, watching money go to something her mother had not used in the past — it made sense that she was worried. I did not take it personally. I took it as information.

I brought them both into a room and I listened. I let the daughter say what she needed to say. I did not argue. I did not show her any paperwork or any audiogram or any chart. I just said, "Let me show you something."

I asked the mother to close her eyes and I took the daughter just outside the room. I told her to ask her mother some questions — real ones, easy ones, the kind a daughter asks her mom. Where do you want to go for lunch? What have you been up to this week? The daughter was skeptical but she played along.

And her mother answered every single one.

When we came back into the room, the daughter was in tears. Not embarrassed tears, not polite tears. Real ones. She had spent however long — months, maybe years — compensating for her mother's hearing. Repeating herself. Raising her voice on phone calls. Worrying about what her mother was missing during those long stretches between visits. And in about sixty seconds, standing in a hallway, she understood in her whole body that her mother was not missing those things anymore.

She looked at me and she said, "Okay, Mom, you really do need these hearing aids, because you're hearing amazing."

That is the loved-one demo. That is the whole point of it. Not to prove anything, not to win an argument. Just to make visible what was already there. To let the family see what we could see.

Because you know what? Hearing really helps. When you cannot hear, it is a pretty lonely and quiet world. Families often do not understand how much they have quietly been compensating — repeating, adjusting, filling the silences — until they see what changes when that compensation is no longer necessary.

---

Not every family situation is that clear-cut, of course. Some days you walk in and read the room and realize the dynamics are more complicated. Who is in charge here? Who made the decision to come in today, and who is along for the ride? You have to figure that out quickly, because the approach that works for one dynamic completely fails in another.

I always tried to make the loved one feel included from the very beginning of the visit. Not treated as a bystander or a chaperone. I made them coffee. I talked to them. I asked them questions about what they had been noticing at home. A lot of times the family member has been watching the hearing loss up close for years — they know exactly what situations are hardest, exactly which conversations keep getting lost. They are a source of information, and they feel that when you treat them that way. You are not going to get the daughter onside by ignoring her while you talk to the patient. She is going to feel like an afterthought, and a suspicious afterthought at that.

The goal is to walk out of that appointment with everyone in agreement — the patient, the family member, and you — about what better hearing is going to mean for this particular family. When that happens, you have not just closed a sale. You have given a family something back — and that family will drive your referrals for years.

**From the other chair**

*(Mark)*

When a man walks in with his wife and he is the one you are trying to help, you have to read that room fast. You need to figure out who wears the pants, and I mean that practically, not unkindly. If she dragged him in, she is tired of yelling across the house and tired of being the hearing aid for someone who is in denial. Get her on your side and it becomes two against one — and that is the only way you turn a hard no into a yes in that room. Empathize with the spouse first. Let her know you understand exactly what she has been living with. Once she feels heard by you, she becomes your best advocate. The third-party close is the most powerful tool in the room when the patient himself is not ready to say yes.

---

**The principle**

When a family member is present, they are not an obstacle to the appointment — they are often the deciding voice. Bring them into the demonstration, not just the conversation. What a loved one witnesses firsthand will do more than any clinical explanation to confirm the decision. Make the family feel included, not managed, and you will leave that room with everyone aligned.

**The AI-Layer**

The loved-one demo is a moment that must happen in person, with a human reading the room. But what comes after that moment can be supported at scale. Automated follow-up sequences can include family members explicitly — a post-appointment message to the patient's emergency contact, a brief check-in asking the spouse how the first week has gone, a reminder that speaks to the household, not just the patient. AI-assisted lifecycle tools can keep the whole family enrolled in the journey, not just the person wearing the devices.

---

**For the Closer:** The family member is often the decision-maker you have not been talking to. Get the spouse or adult child into the demo, work their modality, and use the third-party close when the patient is stalling. Two voices agreeing that it is time beats your best presentation every time.
