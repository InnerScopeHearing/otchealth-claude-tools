--- CARD: Modality Wheel ---

# MODALITY WHEEL
*How does this person make decisions?*

---

## THINKER — ~15% of patients

**Cue:** Walks in with a folder, list of questions, printed research, may mention competitor prices or device specs. Direct, analytical, may push back on your explanations.

**How to communicate:**
- Match their data level — know specs, but redirect to outcome
- Use bullet-pointed facts, not stories
- Ask: "If you built it yourself, would it actually help you hear?" (outcome over engineering)
- Engage the secondary modality when data loops endlessly; try a direct demo (Knower) or an emotional story (Feeler)
- Never get into a technical comparison you can't win — it stalls the decision

---

## KNOWER — ~30% of patients

**Cue:** Gets to the point quickly. States what they think they need. Doesn't want to be buried in detail. Decides by gut after a short demo or a clean highlight.

**How to communicate:**
- Give the three most important points — then stop
- Show, don't tell: brief demonstration lands faster than explanation
- Let silence do the work; the gut needs space to land
- If you keep piling on information after they've decided, you'll talk them out of it

---

## FEELER — ~55–60% of patients

**Cue:** Tells you a story about how not hearing is affecting their family, their marriage, their sense of self. Disengages from specs and channels talk; lights up for connection and narrative.

**How to communicate:**
- Lead with the story of their *own* life with better hearing
- Make it specific and personal: "Picture your grandson calling from across the yard — you hear him the first time. You don't miss that moment."
- Skip channels and processing-speed specs entirely
- Warm tone, unhurried pace, emotional language of connection and independence
- The decision is felt, not calculated — give them the feeling first

---

**REMEMBER:** Everyone has a dominant + a secondary modality.
When you can't reach the dominant, find the secondary.
Your own dominant modality will pull you toward one style — be a chameleon.

--- CARD: Objection Iceberg ---

# OBJECTION ICEBERG
*What they say vs. what's actually stopping them*

---

```
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
        │              COST                   │  ← What they say out loud
        │   "It's too expensive."             │     (the polite exit)
        └─────────────────────────────────────┘
                        │
     ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ waterline ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─
                        │
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
        │             VANITY                  │  ← Beneath the surface
        │   "I don't want to look old."       │
        ├─────────────────────────────────────┤
        │             STIGMA                  │
        │   "Hearing aids = old, damaged,     │
        │    diminished."                     │
        ├─────────────────────────────────────┤
        │             DENIAL                  │  ← The root
        │   "I don't really have a problem."  │
        └─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

## One-Line Reframe for Each Layer

**COST** — *"Not acting is also a cost — in relationships, in connection, in what happens to the auditory system if we wait. Today is the best day we will ever have to address this."*

**VANITY** — *"Asking 'What?' at every dinner table is more visible than any hearing aid you'd wear. Discreet devices exist precisely so the device is never the story."*

**STIGMA** — *"That image is thirty years out of date. Today's devices fit in the canal, stream from your phone, and the only thing people notice is that you're engaged in the conversation."*

**DENIAL** — *Don't argue — show the evidence. Let the audiogram and speech test do the talking. Your job is to let reality land gently, not to win a debate.*

---

## The Flip Rule
Patients state the list backwards.
Real order: **Denial → Stigma → Vanity → Cost.**
Solve them in the right sequence. Financing does not fix denial.

## Surface early, not at the close
Objection surfaced during intake = a conversation you can guide.
Objection surfaced at the moment of decision = a wall to dismantle.

--- CARD: Patient Journey ---

# PATIENT JOURNEY TO BETTER HEARING
*Seven steps — one cue per step*

---

**STEP 1 — FIRST CALL**
*"The smile you can hear."*
The sale begins the moment the phone rings. Warm, unhurried voice. No hesitation you can hear. The caller is anxious — your first job is to ease that, not to confirm it.

---

**STEP 2 — FIRST VISIT / WAITING ROOM**
*Settle them before they sit down.*
Coffee, water, a real conversation — not about hearing aids, about them. Pass key intel to the dispenser before the appointment begins. Trust starts here.

---

**STEP 3 — THE OPENER + HISTORY**
*"What brings you in today?"*
Open question. Listen without assumption. Repeat back exactly what they say. Let them fill the silence. Surface objections now — not at the close.

---

**STEP 4 — TESTING CHAIR**
*Let the evidence do the talking.*
Run the audiogram and speech test. If the spouse is in the room, the speech test shows them — you don't have to argue. By the time you sit back down, know their modality and which objection layer is live.

---

**STEP 5 — THE DECISION**
*Sell yourself, not the device.*
One price includes you — your expertise, your follow-up, your commitment to making it work. Cost of inaction is real. Today is the best day. Help them say yes to the outcome, not to the hardware.

---

**STEP 6 — FITTING + MONTH OF ADAPTATION**
*Name three COSI goals. Write them down. Sign them.*
First week: read aloud 20 min/day (trains the brain to the new voice). Second week: real-world noise exposure. Check in at week one and week two. The brain needs time; warn them before they discover the struggle alone.

---

**STEP 7 — COMING BACK + REFERRALS**
*Grade every follow-up against the three signed goals.*
At completion: "You know, if you have friends with hearing aids who aren't doing as well as you are right now, they may not have been through a program like this. You could help them." The satisfied patient becomes the referral engine.

---

*Track: Recency · Frequency · Transaction count.
Five or six visits = ~75% loyalty. That patient upgrades. That patient refers.*

--- CARD: First-Contact ---

# FIRST-CONTACT CARD
*Phone · Door · Waiting Room*

---

## The Smile You Can Hear

> Before you pick up the phone: **smile first.**
> The caller cannot see you — they can only hear your energy.
> Every person calling from a place of hesitation or embarrassment will decide in the first thirty seconds whether this is a place they can trust.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Voice warm and unhurried — no impatience, no rush
- [ ] Genuinely glad they called — it should come through without saying it
- [ ] Name used early and naturally
- [ ] No clinical or transactional opener — lead with welcome, not process

---

## The Opener (In the Room)

**"What brings you in today?"**

- Open question — no assumptions attached
- Gives the patient permission to answer honestly
- Tells you instantly where they are in their own process
- Sets up the repeat-back

*What to listen for:* The real reason behind the presenting reason.
"My wife made me come" = probable denial situation.
"I've been putting this off for years" = stigma or vanity working.
"I just want to get tested, I'm not buying anything" = repeat it back without defense.

---

## The Repeat-Back

Restate what they just said — their words, not yours — then stop.

**Example:**
- Patient: "My wife thinks I have a hearing problem."
- You: "Your wife thinks you have a hearing problem." *[pause — let them continue]*

**What this does:**
- Signals you heard them and take it seriously
- They fill the silence with more — the real story
- You map the objection layer before the audiogram even starts
- Builds rapport faster than any scripted opener

---

## Before the Appointment Ends

*Ask early, low pressure:*
"If we find something today, and we can help you — would you be willing to consider doing something about it?"

Most people say yes. That single early agreement changes the conversation at the close.

--- CARD: Close ---

# THE CLOSE CARD
*Cost of Inaction · Today is the Best Day · One Price Includes Me*

---

## Cost of Inaction

Not acting is not free. The real price of waiting:
- Interpersonal friction at home (repeating, frustration, withdrawal)
- Pulling back from social situations that become exhausting
- Isolation at family dinners and gatherings
- The auditory system continues to change — we can do more with what they have now than with what they will have left if they wait

*Speak this as your own observed understanding — not a clinical claim.*

---

## Today Is the Best Day

> "Today is a better day to address this than next year. The hearing you have right now is more intact than it will be twelve months from now. We can do more for you today than we can do for you later."

This is not a pressure tactic. It is true. Say it plainly.

---

## One Price Includes Me

**Closer:** "If you can get it cheaper somewhere else — you are not getting me. My 23 years. My commitment to staying in it with you until we get this right. You are not buying a device. You are buying the outcome."

**Professional:** "The price you pay includes everything I bring — my expertise, my follow-up, my commitment to your journey. This isn't a device on a shelf. This is a relationship, and I don't leave until it's working."

---

## When Financing Comes Up

- Don't lead with financing — it shifts focus to price, away from value
- Mention it plainly when cost is a genuine barrier: "If the number is an issue today, we have a way to bridge it."
- Then return to value: "But what we're really talking about is whether you're ready to start hearing better — and I'm ready to help you do that."

---

## The Device Is Not the Product

**Closer:** "Stop defending the device when the price objection hits. Sell yourself before you quote the number. What only you can offer is what they're actually buying."

**Professional:** "A return is not a device failure — it's a trust question. The patient is asking: 'Can this person actually help me?' Everything before the close is building the answer to that question."

--- CARD: Return-Save ---

# RETURN-SAVE CARD
*(Kim Moore)*

---

## The White Envelope Signal

When a patient arrives holding the white delivery envelope: **they are here to return.**
The envelope tells the whole story before anyone says a word.

*Your first response is not defense — it is presence.*

> "Gear up — not to fight, but to listen."

---

## Step 1 — Create Space

- Welcome them warmly, no change in tone
- Sit down with them fully — no counter, no distance
- Open: "Tell me everything. I want to hear all of it."
- Validate each concern as it comes: "That's real, and it matters."
- Do not defend the device. Do not cite the audiogram. Not yet.

---

## Step 2 — Find the ONE Issue

Patients will pour out a list when frustrated. Listen through the list.

**Common presenting concerns** (these are usually not the root):
- "Sounds are too sharp / tinny"
- "My own voice sounds strange / hollow"
- "They whistle"
- "Background noise is worse"
- "I'm still not hearing in [specific situation]"

**The root question:** *What is the single thing driving them toward the door right now?*

Keep asking, gently, until you find it:
- "Of everything you've told me — which one thing, if we fixed it today, would make the biggest difference?"

---

## Step 3 — Fix That One Thing

| Root Issue | Response |
|---|---|
| Own voice sounds hollow/loud | Occlusion effect — normal. Re-do the read-aloud exercise 20 min/day. Most patients work through it if they're warned and coached. |
| Specific situation not working | Return to the COSI goals. Adjust fitting for that exact situation. Was that goal on the list? If not, add it now. |
| Expectation mismatch | Honest conversation: amplification is not restored youth. Name what it *can* do — and reconnect to the reasons they came. |
| Lost trust / feel unsupported | Rebuild the relationship first. Ask what you could have done differently. Commit explicitly to staying in it with them. |

---

## Step 4 — Reconnect to the Reason

Bring them back to why they came in the first place.

**Closer:** "A return is a re-close on the promise you made — not on the product. Reconnect them to the outcome. Re-close on you."

**Professional:** "The save is an act of care, not a sales move. When you sit with a frustrated patient and hear them fully before you say one word in defense of anything, you show them exactly the commitment that changes a skeptic into a believer."

---

## What to Remember

- *They are not returning the hearing aid. They are returning you.* They no longer believe you can help. The save rebuilds that belief.
- Not every return saves. Lead with caring regardless.
- The patient who stays through the hard first month becomes the one who hears birds in their backyard for the first time in years — and tells everyone.
