# From the Chair — The Team Rollout / Training Kit

*For owners and managers rolling the method out to a whole practice.*
*Built from the real careers of Mark & Kim Moore, Licensed Hearing Aid Dispensers.*

---

> "If you can treat people with kindness, they will come back. They will tell their friends. They will bring their spouse in six months later, and their neighbor the following year."
> — Kim Moore

---

## How to Use This Kit

This kit gives you everything you need to take a new team member — or a full practice — from first read to confident, consistent execution of the From the Chair method. It does not assume clinical experience. It assumes a person who has genuine heart and wants to learn to use it well.

The four-week onboarding plan is structured: each week has a reading assignment, a workbook module, and a team drill. Work through it in order. The role-play scenarios can be run any week, starting in Week 2. The weekly drill calendar repeats indefinitely — these are habits, not a one-time curriculum.

The manager's guide at the end explains how to use a team member's filled workbook as a coaching tool, not a performance review.

A note on language: throughout this kit, lines that require edition-sensitive framing are labeled:

**Closer:** *[sales-framed]* and **Professional:** *[care-framed]*

## PART ONE: THE 4-WEEK ONBOARDING PLAN

---

### WEEK 1 — Foundation: Why This Work Matters and How It Starts

**Goal:** The team member understands the Moores' philosophy of care, the patient mindset on arrival, and the physical environment they are responsible for.

---

**What to Read**

- *From the Chair*, Chapters 1–6 (Parts I, II, and III)
- Chapter 1: Two Chairs, One Family — the family origin; why this work runs in generations
- Chapter 2: Folding Mailers & Building Hearing Aids — learning what the job actually is
- Chapter 3: Rain on the Roof — the first sale; why the gift of hearing changes everything
- Chapter 4: You Hear With Your Brain, Not Your Ears — what patients are actually going through in adaptation
- Chapter 5: You Are the Business — the phone call, the warm room, first impressions
- Chapter 6: What Brings You In Today? — the opening question; the repeat-back

**Workbook Modules to Complete This Week**

- Module: *Define your "why" + your role* (Ch. 1 workbook)
- Module: *Write your own "rain on the roof" moment* (Ch. 3 workbook — the patient or person in their own life whose experience of this work changed how they see it)
- Module: *The Mirror drill* (Ch. 5 workbook — practice smiling before picking up the phone)
- Module: *The repeat-back drill* (Ch. 6 workbook — practice the opening question + repeat-back sequence)

**Week 1 Team Drill: The Mirror Drill**

*Purpose:* Establish that the physical and vocal state you bring to the phone or the door is the patient's first experience of the practice.

*How to run it:* Each team member stands in front of a mirror (or pairs up). Before "answering the phone," they place a genuine smile on their face — not a performed smile, a real one. Then they answer with the practice greeting. The team listens and gives one word of feedback: did it sound like a smile you could hear?

*Debrief question:* "What did you have to do or think about to make the smile real, not just polite?"

*Kim's standard:* "Selling starts when the phone rings. The voice you answer with tells the patient everything about what they are about to walk into."

---

**Manager notes for Week 1**

This week is about mindset, not technique. Watch for team members who find the "warm room" philosophy natural versus those who find it effortful. The ones who find it natural are your Feelers — they already live there. The ones who find it effortful may be Thinkers or Knowers who need the *reason* behind warmth to adopt it. Give them the reason: one dismissive front-desk moment can undo months of marketing spend and lose a patient they may never get back.

---

### WEEK 2 — The Patient: Objections, Modalities, and the Testing Chair

**Goal:** The team member can identify where a patient is in the denial → stigma → vanity → cost sequence and can adapt their communication style to the patient's modality.

---

**What to Read**

- *From the Chair*, Chapters 7–9 (Part IV)
- Chapter 7: Denial, Stigma, Vanity, Cost — the inverted objection sequence
- Chapter 8: Thinkers, Knowers, Feelers — the three modalities; being a chameleon
- Chapter 9: The Testing Chair — the Hershey-bar welcome; testing as the highlight

**Workbook Modules to Complete This Week**

- Module: *Invert your last "too expensive"* (Ch. 7 workbook — take a recent patient who cited cost; work backward through the four-layer sequence to identify the real barrier)
- Module: *Modality self-test + tag 5 patients* (Ch. 8 workbook — identify your own dominant modality; tag five recent or role-played patients with their likely dominant and secondary modality)
- Module: *Design your "Hershey bar"* (Ch. 9 workbook — what is your practice's version of a small, thoughtful welcome that makes testing feel like a highlight?)
- Module: *Script your week-2 check-in* (Ch. 4 workbook — write the words you use when a patient calls in week two, frustrated that their voice sounds strange)

**Week 2 Team Drill: Modality Tagging**

*Purpose:* Build the habit of identifying a patient's modality before presenting any information, so the team naturally adjusts their approach in the moment.

*How to run it:* Manager reads a short patient vignette aloud (use the scenarios in Part Two of this kit — the engineer, the nervous first-timer, the denial patient). Team members write down, independently: (1) dominant modality, (2) secondary modality, (3) the first thing they would say differently because of that modality. Compare and discuss.

*Debrief question:* "What did you notice about your own default communication style? When do you fall back on it even when it doesn't fit the patient?"

*Mark's standard:* "You have to become a master communicator — and the trick is developing your own awareness first."

---

**Dual-edition note for Week 2**

When discussing the objection sequence with a new team member:

**Closer:** The inverted order tells you which conversation to have first. If you start at cost before you've addressed denial, you're negotiating with the wrong person. Handle the real objection and the price conversation usually handles itself.

**Professional:** When a patient says "it's too expensive," hear what they're not saying: "I'm not sure I'm ready, and I'm not sure this will work." Your response is not a price negotiation — it's an invitation to keep talking.

---

**Manager notes for Week 2**

In the modality self-test, most team members will identify as Feelers — this is consistent with the book's approximate distribution of ~55–60%. Validate this; it is genuinely useful. The coaching challenge is helping Feelers recognize when a Thinker walks in and put down the story in favor of the spec sheet. Pay particular attention to how each team member handles the engineer scenario in role-play.

---

### WEEK 3 — The Appointment: Family, Demos, the Close, and Honesty

**Goal:** The team member can run the loved-one demo, handle the family dynamic, and deliver the one-price conversation with authenticity rather than script.

---

**What to Read**

- *From the Chair*, Chapters 10–12 (Parts V and VI)
- Chapter 10: When the Family Is in the Room — the loved-one demo; the refund daughter; the third-party close
- Chapter 11: You're Not Selling a Hearing Aid, You're Selling Yourself — price = device + me; cost of inaction; today is the best day; financing as a bridge
- Chapter 12: The Honest Truth — honesty even when it costs the sale; setting honest adaptation expectations

**Workbook Modules to Complete This Week**

- Module: *Your loved-one demo script* (Ch. 10 workbook — write out the exact instructions you give the family member before the demo; what do you say to set it up, what do you watch for, what do you do when they respond?)
- Module: *Write your one-price-includes-me line* (Ch. 11 workbook — in your own voice, not a script; must be genuine enough to say without reading it)
- Module: *A time honesty cost/saved you* (Ch. 12 workbook — a personal or professional reflection on when telling the harder truth produced a better outcome)

**Week 3 Team Drill: The Loved-One Demo**

*Purpose:* Practice the mechanics and the emotional awareness of the demo so it becomes natural, not staged.

*How to run it:* One team member plays the patient, one plays the family member (start skeptical — a slightly doubtful adult child is a good default). The third runs the demo. After the demo "moment," pause and debrief: What did the person playing the family member actually feel? What shifted? What did the person running the demo do that helped — or nearly broke — the moment?

*Variation:* Run the scenario where the family member starts as a hostile returner (see Scenario A in Part Two of this kit). The team member running the demo must settle the room before the demo can even begin.

*Kim's standard:* "I always watched the loved one's face in that moment. I could see the moment it landed."

---

**Dual-edition note for Week 3**

On the one-price conversation:

**Closer:** "The price you pay includes me — my years, my fittings, my willingness to keep working with you until we get this right. You are not buying a device. You are buying the outcome."

**Professional:** "One price means I am not going to stop working with you when the budget runs out. I am with you through every follow-up, every adjustment, every question. That is what the price covers."

---

**Manager notes for Week 3**

The hardest piece for most new team members is the honest adaptation conversation — telling a patient that hearing aids will not make them hear like a twenty-year-old, and that the first month may feel hard. This honesty is counter-instinctive for people who want to reassure. Help team members understand that preparing a patient for difficulty is not a sales risk; it is the reason patients stay. The ones who return are almost always the ones nobody warned.

---

### WEEK 4 — The Journey: Follow-Through, Returns, Recalls, and Culture

**Goal:** The team member understands the full patient lifecycle — from COSI goal-setting through return-save to referral — and understands how the practice's culture is maintained and hired for.

---

**What to Read**

- *From the Chair*, Chapters 13–18 (Parts VII, VIII, and IX)
- Chapter 13: The Patient Journey — the modified COSI; the duplicatable process; the British-PBS-accent return
- Chapter 14: Saving the Return — the white-envelope signal; find the one issue; the 42-year-old patient
- Chapter 15: Why They Came Back — loyalty; referrals from satisfaction; the coffee-and-donuts lobby
- Chapter 16: Hiring for Heart — the relationship-with-grandparents question; clean bathrooms; never ask staff to do what you won't
- Chapter 17: Winning Without Selling — treat every patient like your parents or grandparents
- Chapter 18: The Gift of Hearing — the closing commitment; the gold stickers; what this work means

**Workbook Modules to Complete This Week**

- Module: *Capture a patient's 3 COSI goals* (Ch. 13 workbook — practice the COSI conversation with a colleague role-playing a patient; write the three goals in the patient's words)
- Module: *Your return-save protocol* (Ch. 14 workbook — write the exact sequence you follow when a patient arrives with the white envelope)
- Module: *Your recall/referral cadence* (Ch. 15 workbook — map out when you ask, how you ask, and what you say)
- Module: *Your hiring question + culture checklist* (Ch. 16 workbook — draft the Kim-style interview question for your context; list the three physical standards you will never compromise)
- Module: *Your commitment + 30/60/90 re-score* (Ch. 18 workbook — write a one-paragraph personal commitment; re-score the workbook's practice scorecard to establish a baseline)

**Week 4 Team Drill: The Return-Save**

*Purpose:* Make the return-save feel like a practiced response, not a panicked improvisation.

*How to run it:* One team member plays a patient arriving with the white envelope — frustrated, possibly a little hostile, listing multiple complaints. The team member running the save must: (1) resist the urge to defend immediately; (2) listen fully through the list; (3) ask the one question that surfaces the real issue; (4) fix that one thing and reanchor the patient to the reason they started. Rotate roles. Debrief each round on where the team member fell into defending or explaining too early.

*Debrief question:* "What did you have to stop yourself from saying first?"

*Kim's standard:* "The single most important thing I learned about saving a return is this: you cannot save it until you find the one thing."

---

**Manager notes for Week 4**

By the end of Week 4, a new team member should have a filled workbook with real self-scores, drafted scripts, and at least one personal reflection per module. The workbook is not an exercise to be filed — it is a live document that the 1:1 coaching process (see Part Four of this kit) will reference for months. Help team members understand that the workbook is their own tool first, your coaching tool second. Their honesty in it is what makes it useful to them.

## PART TWO: ROLE-PLAY SCENARIOS

*Six scenarios drawn from the real situations in From the Chair. Each has a setup — who the patient is and what you know before they sit down — and a "what good looks like" guide for debriefing. Use starting from Week 2.*

*Casting note: these work best with one person running the patient and one running the team role. A third person as silent observer improves debrief quality. Rotate roles so everyone experiences the patient side.*

---

### SCENARIO A — The Refund Daughter

**Source:** Chapter 10, Kim's account of the daughter who arrived from back east.

**Setup for the person playing the patient's family member:**
You are the adult daughter of a retired schoolteacher. You flew in from the east coast. Your mother has a history of buying hearing aids and not wearing them. You are certain this is an expensive mistake, and you walked in this morning prepared to return the devices today. You are not angry with the staff personally — you are worried about your mother's money and tired of being far away while she makes decisions you think she should run by you. You are skeptical but not cruel.

**Setup for the person running the appointment:**
The mother has been a patient for some time and is doing genuinely well with her new devices. She is wearing them consistently. You know her situation and believe the devices are working. The daughter has arrived unannounced with the intention of returning. You do not have the daughter's history.

**What good looks like:**
- Does not argue with the daughter's concerns or show paperwork immediately.
- Listens fully — lets the daughter say everything she came in to say.
- Says some version of: "Let me show you something" before offering any defense.
- Sets up the loved-one demo: seats the mother, takes the daughter just outside, asks her to start a real conversation.
- Watches the daughter's face; does not break the moment with talking.
- After the moment lands, gives the daughter space to respond before saying anything.
- Does not claim victory — simply says: "You can see what we've been seeing."
- The goal is the daughter leaving as an ally, not a defeated objection.

**Debrief questions:**
1. What would have happened if you had shown her the audiogram first?
2. At what moment did the room change? What created that change?
3. What did you have to resist saying?

### SCENARIO B — The British-PBS Return

**Source:** Chapter 13, Mark's account of the patient who returned because she could not understand her British television program.

**Setup for the person playing the patient:**
You are a patient returning after several weeks. The hearing devices are technically working well — your family says you seem sharper, you can follow general conversation. But you bought them specifically because of a British television program you watch every night, and you still cannot follow the accents clearly. This is the thing that matters to you. You are prepared to return. You are not dramatically upset — you are just disappointed, and quietly certain that the devices did not deliver what you expected.

**Setup for the person running the appointment:**
You did not take COSI goals at delivery — this patient was fitted before the three-goal system was fully in place (or you simply did not ask the right question). You are now realizing, for the first time in this conversation, exactly what she bought them for.

**What good looks like:**
- Does not become defensive or technical immediately.
- Asks the open question: "Tell me specifically — what were you most hoping these would help you with?"
- Listens without interrupting.
- Acknowledges the specific situation genuinely: "I understand. British accents — even people with normal hearing sometimes struggle with certain productions."
- Does not over-promise. Does not say the devices will fix it if they cannot.
- Offers to adjust the programming for her television environment; asks what program and what channel so the adjustment is specific, not general.
- Resets expectations honestly: amplification is not the same as perfect clarity for all speakers; some listening goals need a real conversation up front.
- Uses this moment to introduce (or re-introduce) the three-goal system: "Let's write down the three situations that matter most to you, so every visit from here we're measuring against what you actually came in for."
- Does not let her leave without a plan and a follow-up appointment.

**Debrief questions:**
1. When did you realize you didn't know what she'd actually bought them for?
2. How do you have this conversation honestly without losing the patient's trust?
3. What would you do differently at the next delivery appointment?

### SCENARIO C — The Engineer Who'd Build His Own

**Source:** Chapter 8, Mark's account of the engineer who said he could build a hearing aid for fifty dollars.

**Setup for the person playing the patient:**
You are an engineer. You have done your research. You have printed out component specifications for hearing aid circuitry and have a working understanding of signal processing. You are genuinely curious, not hostile — but your questions will keep going deeper, and at some point you will say, in a matter-of-fact way: "You know, these components sound like things I could order. Little capacitors, some circuitry. I could probably build something like this in my backyard for about fifty dollars."

**Setup for the person running the appointment:**
You recognized the folder when he walked in. You are working with a dominant Thinker. Your normal comfort zone is storytelling and emotional connection — you are a natural Feeler. You need to adjust.

**What good looks like:**
- Does not try to out-engineer the engineer on component specs — this is unwinnable.
- Acknowledges the technical knowledge genuinely: "You clearly understand how these are built."
- Redirects to the outcome: "If you built one yourself, would it actually help you hear?"
- Stays in the logical frame the patient is using — this is not a moment for the grandson-in-the-backyard story.
- Does not oversell or over-explain; gives the three most important technical points and stops.
- Finds the secondary modality: if logic alone isn't landing, tries a brief, targeted experience (a demo) rather than more data.
- Leaves the patient with a sense that they know more after this conversation, not less.

**Debrief questions:**
1. At what moment did you feel the urge to go back to your comfort zone?
2. What is the difference between matching a Thinker's frame and trying to beat them in their frame?
3. What three things — only three — does this patient actually need to decide?

### SCENARIO D — The Denial Patient

**Source:** Chapter 7, Mark's account of the well-dressed gentleman who cited cost but was clearly in denial; and Chapter 6, the repeat-back technique with the patient who said "my wife just mumbles."

**Setup for the person playing the patient:**
You are a man in your late sixties. You came in because your wife insisted. You genuinely do not believe you have a hearing problem — you might concede that you "ask people to repeat sometimes," but in your mind that is normal. When the team member asks what brings you in today, your honest answer is: "My wife thinks I have a hearing problem. I think she mumbles." You are pleasant and agreeable in tone. You are not ready to buy. You are here to be told you're fine.

**Setup for the person running the appointment:**
You have been warned by the front desk that the wife is tired of repeating herself and has been trying to get him in for two years. The wife is not in the room today — he came alone.

**What good looks like:**
- Opens with "What brings you in today?" — not "Are you having trouble hearing?"
- Receives the answer without correcting or challenging it: "So your wife thinks you have a hearing problem, but you're not so sure."
- Uses the repeat-back to draw out more: he fills the silence with more of his real position.
- Identifies the denial layer without naming it as denial: "It sounds like you're not sure there's actually a problem worth addressing."
- Does not immediately rush to the audiogram as a verdict. Uses it as a teaching tool, not a judgment.
- At some point before testing, asks: "If we did find something today, and we could help — would you be willing to consider doing something about it?" — and receives the implicit agreement.
- After testing, lets the results speak; does not argue, does not lecture.
- Empathizes with the male vanity dimension without condescending: "Most guys I sit with aren't sure they need anything when they come in. And most of them are glad they came."

**Debrief questions:**
1. When he said "my wife mumbles" — what was the right response?
2. What would have happened if you'd challenged his self-assessment directly?
3. At what moment did the implicit agreement ("would you be willing to consider it?") shift the dynamic?

### SCENARIO E — The White-Envelope Return

**Source:** Chapter 14, Kim's full account of the white-envelope protocol and the 42-year-old patient.

**Setup for the person playing the patient:**
You are a patient three weeks into wearing hearing devices for the first time. You are frustrated. Your list of complaints is real and long: your own voice sounds strange, everything in a restaurant is overwhelming, you are not sure the investment was worth it. You have brought in the white envelope. You are not entirely certain you want to return — part of you wants someone to give you a reason to stay — but you are prepared to leave with that envelope if this visit doesn't go differently than you expect.

**Setup for the person running the appointment:**
You recognize the white envelope the moment the patient walks in. You know what it means. You have practiced this.

**What good looks like:**
- Does not flinch at the envelope; stays warm and unhurried.
- Does not ask "what seems to be the problem" — opens with warmth: "I'm glad you came in. Tell me everything."
- Does not interrupt or defend during the list of complaints.
- Listens until the list winds down, then asks the one question: "Of everything you just told me — what's the one thing that, if we fixed it, would make the biggest difference?"
- Identifies the single real issue.
- Addresses that one thing specifically — not everything on the list, not a general tune-up.
- Explains the barrel effect (if voice is the issue) in plain language, not clinical language.
- Connects the patient to the reason they came in: "Can I ask — why did you decide to do this? What were you hoping would change?"
- Re-anchors the visit: "Let's fix this one thing today and book a follow-up for next week. If it's still not right, we keep working."
- If the save fails, does not make the patient feel like a failure.

**Debrief questions:**
1. Where was the temptation to defend the product or explain the fitting rationale?
2. What made the difference between "listening through" the list and "fixing" the list?
3. How did reconnecting the patient to their original reason change the conversation?

### SCENARIO F — The Nervous First-Timer

**Source:** Chapter 6, Kim's sidebar on the nervous patient at the front desk; Chapter 5, the warm room; Chapter 4, the honest adaptation conversation.

**Setup for the person playing the patient:**
You are coming in for the first time. You have been putting this off for at least two years. You are not sure what to expect. You are more embarrassed about being here than you have admitted to anyone, including yourself. You are also a little worried about what the test will show. You will be polite and cooperative, but your body language is guarded — you are sitting slightly forward in the chair, your answers are shorter than they need to be. You are a Feeler: you are reading the room constantly, deciding whether these people actually care or whether this is a transaction.

**Setup for the person playing the front-desk role:**
The patient arrived two minutes early. Your job before the appointment starts is to settle them down enough that when they go into the testing room, they are present and honest rather than defended and rushed.

**What good looks like (front desk phase):**
- Offers coffee or water without waiting to be asked.
- Makes brief, genuine small talk — not about hearing, about them.
- Does not hand a clipboard and point at the sign-in sheet without looking up.
- Passes something real and useful to the dispenser before the appointment: "She seemed a little nervous — mentioned she's been putting this off a while."

**What good looks like (appointment phase):**
- Opens with "What brings you in today?" — genuinely curious, not procedural.
- When the patient gives a guarded short answer, uses the repeat-back to make space for more.
- Reads the Feeler modality — uses story and feeling language, not clinical language.
- Does not rush toward the test. Earns the right to test.
- Before the test: "I want to be honest with you — there's no verdict here today. Whatever we find is just information. You're in charge of what happens next."
- After the test: does not immediately pivot to products. Sits with what the patient is feeling about what they just heard.
- If adaptation is discussed, uses the brain-not-the-ears framework in plain language; gives the honest expectation without catastrophizing.

**Debrief questions:**
1. At what moment did the patient relax? What created that?
2. How did the front-desk handoff affect the appointment?
3. What is the difference between rushing to the test and earning the right to test?

### SCENARIO G — The Adaptation Crisis (Week 2 Patient)

**Source:** Chapter 4, Kim's account of the month of rewiring; the patients who called in week two ready to quit; the gold stickers.

**Setup for the person playing the patient:**
You are two weeks into your first hearing aids. You are calling in — not coming in — and you are frustrated. Your own voice sounds wrong. You are noticing sounds you did not ask for (refrigerator hum, road noise in the car). You went to a restaurant last weekend and it was overwhelming. You are not angry; you are discouraged. Part of your message is: "I think these just aren't for me."

**Setup for the person taking the call:**
This is a follow-up call, but the patient called in rather than waiting for your scheduled check-in. You need to handle this on the phone, not in person. You cannot rely on visual cues.

**What good looks like:**
- Does not immediately reassure — listens first.
- Asks one question before anything else: "Tell me what's been happening."
- Listens through the list without interrupting.
- Then, only then: "Everything you're describing — the barrel feeling with your voice, the noise being louder than you expected — that is the right thing happening. That is your brain waking up."
- Uses the cast-and-sling analogy (or equivalent plain-language version): "Think about what happens when you take off a cast. The muscles didn't disappear — they just need time to come back."
- Does not promise that it will definitely get better in a specific number of days; does promise: "I am not going anywhere while you work through this."
- Brings them back in before the scheduled follow-up if possible — even just to check in and adjust one thing.
- Ends the call with a specific next step, not "let me know how it goes."

**Debrief questions:**
1. What was the risk in reassuring too fast?
2. What did "I've got you" actually mean in practical terms on this call?
3. How do you close a call like this without making an empty promise?

### SCENARIO H — The Hiring Interview (for managers and owners)

**Source:** Chapter 16, Kim's account of the grandparents question; Chapter 8, Mark's note on modalities in managing staff.

**Setup:**
This is a mock interview. The interviewer is running the Kim-style interview process. The candidate (played by a team member) has a full resume: previous healthcare admin experience, good references, comfortable with scheduling software. The question is whether they have heart.

**What good looks like (interviewer):**
- Does not lead with the resume or credentials — sets the candidate at ease first.
- Asks the relationship question naturally, not as a gotcha: "Can you tell me about your relationship with your parents or grandparents — or maybe an older friend or mentor who played that kind of role in your life?"
- Listens fully without steering the answer.
- Pays attention to energy, not content: does the candidate light up?
- Follows up with one specific: "What did you learn from that relationship that you still carry with you?"
- Does not hire against warmth, regardless of credentials.

**What good looks like (candidate — played by a team member who passes):**
- Has a real story. Lights up telling it. Is specific, not generic.
- The follow-up answer reveals something genuine about how they think about older people.

**Debrief questions:**
1. What did you hear in the answer that told you this person has heart?
2. How would you handle a candidate who gave a cold or dismissive answer?
3. What is the cost to the practice of hiring someone competent but without warmth?

## PART THREE: WEEKLY TEAM-DRILL CALENDAR

*Run these in your team huddles or brief staff meetings. Most take 10–15 minutes. Rotate who leads each drill. The goal is not to perfect every answer — it is to keep the skills fresh, make adjustment normal, and create a shared language across the whole team.*

*Note: This calendar runs on an indefinite loop. After Week 4 of onboarding, the team continues the calendar from Week 1.*

---

### WEEK A — The Mirror Drill

**Time needed:** 10 minutes

**Purpose:** Maintains the standard that the voice and energy you bring to the first contact is the patient's first experience of your practice.

**How to run it:**
Each person takes a turn "answering the phone." Before picking up, they pause, place a real smile, and then answer with the practice's standard greeting. The team gives one piece of feedback per person: *heard the smile / almost / not yet*.

**Variation for in-person greeters:** Same drill, but for the moment a patient walks in the door. The greeter looks up from whatever they are doing, makes eye contact, and delivers the welcome. The team rates whether the patient would feel welcomed or processed.

**Debrief prompt:** "What did you have to do to make it real this week? What was on your mind that tried to get in the way?"

**Kim's line:** "I am the business. Every interaction I have — on the phone, at the desk, in the hallway — is the business."

---

### WEEK B — The Repeat-Back Drill

**Time needed:** 10–15 minutes

**Purpose:** Keeps the opening-question + repeat-back habit sharp and prevents the team from slipping into assumption-mode on intake.

**How to run it:**
One person is the patient; one runs the intake. The patient delivers one of the following opening lines:

- "My wife made me come."
- "I want to get tested but I'm not here to buy anything."
- "I tried hearing aids years ago and threw them in a drawer."
- "My doctor said I should probably do this."

The team member must: (1) not react to the statement; (2) repeat it back without editorializing; (3) ask one follow-up question that opens the real story. The team watches for two failure modes: jumping to defense/reassurance too fast, or asking a leading question that steers the patient's answer.

**Debrief prompt:** "When did you feel the urge to reassure them before you fully understood their concern?"

**Mark's line:** "If a person tells you something, they believe it is important. The least you can do is repeat it back so they know you heard it."

---

### WEEK C — Modality Tagging

**Time needed:** 10–15 minutes

**Purpose:** Keeps the team actively observing and categorizing patient communication style, not just reacting.

**How to run it:**
Manager reads a two-minute patient vignette or uses a real (anonymized) intake note from the past week. Team members write independently: (1) dominant modality; (2) secondary; (3) what they would change about their presentation for this patient. Then compare and discuss.

**Variation:** Use one of the role-play scenarios from Part Two of this kit as the source material. Run the scenario, then pause and tag.

**Debrief prompt:** "Were there any moments this past week where you caught yourself presenting in your own modality rather than the patient's?"

**Mark's line:** "Don't have a canned presentation for everyone. Know the principles. Know what you need to accomplish. But deliver it in the language that person can receive."

---

### WEEK D — The Return-Save Drill

**Time needed:** 10–15 minutes

**Purpose:** Keeps the return-save response immediate and muscle-memory rather than improvised under pressure.

**How to run it:**
One team member plays the patient coming in with the white envelope. They present a complaint list — at least three items, only one of which is the real issue. The team member running the save must listen through the full list before asking the one question that surfaces the real issue. No defending, no explaining, no referencing the audiogram — only after the issue is found.

**Variations:**
- Week 1 of the loop: the real issue is the patient's own voice (the barrel effect). Solution is the vocal exercise.
- Week 2 of the loop: the real issue is an expectation mismatch (they expected to hear as well as they did before any loss). This is the harder, honest conversation.
- Week 3 of the loop: the real issue is a specific environment — a particular room, a specific television, a certain voice.

**Debrief prompt:** "Where did you catch yourself wanting to defend the device before you'd found the real issue?"

**Kim's line:** "Once you have figured out the most important issue to them at that moment, then you are able to fix the problem so that they can stay on that journey."

## PART FOUR: THE MANAGER'S GUIDE TO USING THE FILLED WORKBOOK IN 1:1s

---

### The Principle

The workbook a team member fills out during and after their four-week onboarding is not a test. It is not a performance evaluation. It is not evidence in a discipline conversation.

It is a mirror — a document the team member made of their own thinking and their own current capacity. Your job as a manager is to help them use that mirror to grow, not to audit them against a standard and tell them where they fell short.

Kim Moore ran the warmest rooms in the practice because she hired for heart and then trusted the people she hired. She modeled the standard herself — she vacuumed the carpet, she made the coffee, she cleaned the bathroom — and because her team saw her do it, they owned it too. The workbook conversation works the same way: you go in curious, not evaluating.

---

### What the Workbook Contains (and What It Tells You)

By the end of Week 4, a filled workbook typically contains:

- **Self-scores** from the Practice Scorecard modules — the team member's own assessment of how they are performing at each stage of the patient journey.
- **Drafted scripts** — their "one-price-includes-me" line, their repeat-back examples, their week-2 check-in language, their loved-one demo setup language.
- **Patient logs** — the modality tags, the COSI goal exercises, the "invert your last too expensive" reflection.
- **Personal reflections** — the "rain on the roof" moment, the "why," the honesty reflection.
- **The return-save protocol** — their own written sequence.

Each of these is data, but not the kind that goes in a performance file. It is coaching data.

---

### How to Run a 1:1 Using the Workbook

**Before the meeting:**
Review the workbook ahead of time. Look for: (1) areas where self-scores are notably low — this may be honest self-awareness or it may be a misunderstanding of what good looks like; (2) areas where self-scores are notably high but you have observed a gap between the score and the actual behavior; (3) drafted scripts that are technically correct but would not be said with genuine feeling; (4) reflections that reveal something important about how this person is wired.

Do not come in with a correction list. Come in with two or three questions.

**Opening the conversation:**
Do not start with the scores. Start with the personal reflection: "Tell me about your 'rain on the roof' moment — what did you write?"

This tells you a great deal. A team member who wrote something real and specific is more present in this work than a team member who wrote something generic. Neither is wrong — but they require different coaching.

**Working through self-scores:**
When you arrive at a low self-score, do not correct it. Ask about it: "You gave yourself a two on the repeat-back. Tell me about what happened in your head when you tried that this week." The answer tells you whether the issue is technique (coachable in a practice) or mindset (requires a different conversation).

When you arrive at a self-score that does not match what you observed: "You gave yourself a four on the return-save drill, and I want to understand your thinking — can you walk me through how that went for you?" This opens the conversation without accusation.

**The drafted scripts:**
Ask the team member to read their "one-price-includes-me" line aloud. Not to you as a recitation — to an imaginary patient. Watch whether it lands or whether it sounds like they are reading something they wrote because they had to. If it sounds scripted, say: "That's a good structure. Now say it the way you would actually say it if you were just talking." Give them a moment. What comes out naturally is closer to what will work in the room.

Do not rewrite their scripts. Help them find their own voice in the structure. Kim's line and Mark's line are reference models — but a team member who delivers a caring, honest, genuine version of their own line is more effective than one who memorizes theirs.

**The patient logs and modality tags:**
These are the most operationally useful part of the workbook for ongoing coaching. Look for patterns: Is this team member consistently misidentifying Knowers as Feelers? Are they only comfortable tagging Feelers because that's their own modality? Are their COSI goal exercises capturing the patient's actual words or paraphrases that lose the specific detail?

One good coaching question here: "In this log, you tagged her as a Feeler. What was the cue that told you that?" This develops the habit of observation, not just the label.

**Ending the conversation:**
End with one specific commitment, not a list. "Between now and our next check-in, I want you to notice one moment in each appointment where you adjusted your communication because of the patient's modality — and write it down. That's all." Small, specific, observable.

---

### What Surveillance Looks Like (and Why It Fails)

If you use the workbook to build a case — to document low scores as evidence, to compare team members against each other, to justify a disciplinary conversation — you will get workbooks that are filled out to impress rather than to reflect. The honest low scores will disappear. The drafted scripts will all sound the same. The reflections will be safe and vague.

Mark Moore ran multiple offices across California and Florida. He built a team culture by understanding each person's modality — giving Thinkers the reasoning behind a policy, giving Knowers the headline, giving Feelers the why-it-matters-for-patients story. He did not lead everyone the same way, and he did not measure everyone against the same yardstick.

The workbook is a coaching tool. Use it that way, and it compounds in value over time. The team member who fills it out honestly in Week 4 and reviews it honestly in a 1:1 will fill it out differently — more specifically, more honestly — at the 30/60/90 re-score. That progression is the evidence of growth. A self-score that moves from 2 to 4 over ninety days, explained in a team member's own words, is more meaningful than a 5 that was never true.

---

### The 30/60/90 Re-Score

At thirty days, sixty days, and ninety days after onboarding completion, return to the workbook together. The re-score process is simple:

1. Team member re-scores the Practice Scorecard.
2. They write one sentence next to any score that changed: *what changed, and why.*
3. In the 1:1, you spend most of the time on the *why* — not whether the number went up, but what the team member actually learned.

By the ninety-day mark, a team member who has engaged genuinely will have a workbook that reads like a journal of their own development. That document is theirs. They keep it. It is the record of their own growth in this work — not your file on them.

---

### A Note on the Dual Editions

If your practice uses both the Closer and Professional editions of *From the Chair*, your team members may have read and been coached from different editions. This is fine — the method is the same; only the framing differs. In 1:1s, use the language that matches the edition the team member was onboarded with. The coaching conversations in this guide are edition-neutral by design; only the specific script lines need the edition label when they come up.
