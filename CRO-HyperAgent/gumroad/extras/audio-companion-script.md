## PART I — HOW WE GOT HERE

*Chapters 1 & 2 — Kim leads, with Mark*

[NARRATOR: Kim]

When people ask me how I got into the hearing aid business, I always start with the kitchen table.

I was around ten years old when my dad, Marvin Posey, would bring home stacks of mailers — advertising pieces for his hearing aid offices. My brothers and sisters and I would sit around that table folding them, stamping them, peeling address labels. We didn't think of it as work. It was just what we did to help Dad.

But here is the part that stayed with me: at dinner, my father would come home and connect the dots for us. He'd say, "Those mailers you did — that's how these people came in today." A ten-year-old with a glue stick had something to do with a person walking out of his office able to hear again.

That lesson — that every small piece of effort connects to someone's outcome — is still the way I look at this work. The support role, the front desk, the testing, the follow-up call that gets made at eight in the evening because a patient left a voicemail — none of it is small. All of it matters.

My mother handled the bookkeeping, and she was very clear about why. "Your father can do what he does," she told me, "because the numbers are covered." The support side of a business is not the background. It's the foundation the whole thing stands on.

[NARRATOR: Mark]

I learned a different version of that lesson when I came in through the back door of this business — literally. Kim's uncle had a little shop set up in the back of his office where he built hearing aids by hand. He taught me the whole process: taking ear impressions, pouring acrylic molds, selecting circuits, soldering receivers, adjusting the tiny potentiometers with a screwdriver. It was a specific and strange education. And I loved it.

Then the math caught up with me.

The more I sold, the more I had to build. Every sale meant another trip to the bench, another delivery, another follow-up. I got to twenty units in one month — and then spent the next month and a half building, delivering, and servicing them. During all that time, I wasn't selling. The funnel ran dry.

That taught me one of the most important things I've ever learned: you cannot sell and manufacture at the same time. You cannot do the relationship work while you're buried in the back-room work. The dispenser's most valuable resource is undivided time with the patient. Everything that competes for that time costs more than it appears to save.

The takeaway from Part One is this: the relationship is the product. The mailer starts it, the front desk continues it, the dispenser deepens it, and the follow-up sustains it. When everyone does their part, the whole thing moves. When one person tries to do all of it, the funnel runs dry — every time.

## PART II — WHY THIS WORK MATTERS

*Chapters 3 & 4 — Mark leads, with Kim*

[NARRATOR: Mark]

I want to tell you about the moment this stopped being a business for me.

I was two and a half months into my training. Kim's uncle had gone on a hunting trip and left me in charge of the office for a week. I had done the book learning. I had built devices in the back room. But I had never run a full appointment solo — from the history all the way through to a decision. Now the schedule was mine.

She was about seventy-five years old.

She told me her friends felt like she was always asking them to repeat themselves, and they were getting frustrated. She was embarrassed by it — you could hear that in the way she talked about it, not dramatic, just worn down. I tested her. I explained what I was seeing as best I could for someone three months into the work. She said, "Great." Just like that. She was ready. She'd been ready for a while. She just needed someone to listen.

I sold her a hearing aid. She came back a few days after delivery. I saw her come through the front door and she started calling my name down the hallway. She reached out with both arms and started crying. She pulled me into a hug and held on.

I didn't know what was happening. I asked her what was the matter.

She said, "You have changed my life."

She said, "I didn't even know what I was missing. The rain — it rained yesterday, and I just sat and bawled, because I heard the rain on the roof. And I didn't even know I missed that."

Then she told me about the leaves blowing down the street. The birds when the sun came out. Things she hadn't heard in years without ever knowing they were gone.

From that day forward, I felt it was not a business anymore. The hearing aid was no longer a product with a circuit and a trim pot. It was the conduit to something that mattered — really mattered — in a way that almost nothing in commerce actually does.

I've fitted over ten thousand people with hearing devices in a twenty-three year career. The reason I stayed was that first patient. The rain on the roof. The understanding that what we do is not profit and loss — it's restoring something to people that they didn't even know they had lost.

[NARRATOR: Kim]

And then comes the part most people are never told.

Nobody walks out after their first fitting and says everything sounds perfect. That has never happened once in my career, and I want you to understand why.

You do not hear with your ears. You hear with your brain. The ears collect sound, but the interpreting — the making-sense of all of it — happens in the brain. And when you've lived with a hearing loss for years, sometimes decades, your brain has quietly adapted. It rewired itself around the silence. It forgot. Not dramatically, just gradually, the way a path through a field disappears when nobody walks it anymore.

When amplification arrives, the brain pushes back. Everything is loud. Everything is overwhelming. And that voice — the patient's own voice — sounds like they're talking from inside a barrel.

That is where I came in. My job, in those first weeks, was to hold their hand — sometimes literally — and say: "I've got you." Those three words matter more than any technical explanation I could offer. People only want to know that they matter to you in this world.

I gave out gold stickers. I know that sounds a little silly for grown adults, but it worked. When patients came back after a hard week and they had stuck it out, I handed them a gold sticker. They would chuckle. Then they would beam. Something about that small, tangible acknowledgment told them their effort mattered. A lot of them told me years later they still had their stickers. I believe them.

The takeaway from Part Two: the hearing aid is a tool. The relationship is the product. And the adaptation journey — that first hard month — is not extra work. It is the work.

## PART III — THE FIRST CONTACT

*Chapters 5 & 6 — Kim leads, with Mark*

[NARRATOR: Kim]

Before I ever worked in a hearing aid office, I worked at a chiropractor's practice. The detail that stayed with me from that job was a mirror mounted above the phone. You were supposed to look at yourself before you answered a call — not to fix your hair, but to make sure you were smiling. Because when you smile, the person on the other end of the line can hear it.

I never forgot that mirror.

When I moved into hearing care, there was no mirror above the desk. But I put one there in my mind, and I have never answered a phone without it.

Here's what I believe with everything in me: the selling process begins the moment the front desk person answers the phone. Not when the dispenser starts the audiogram. Not when the patient sits down in the testing booth. The moment the phone rings and someone picks it up — that is when it starts.

No one who calls a hearing aid office is calling because they feel great about the situation. They are calling from a place of hesitation, embarrassment, maybe a little fear. The voice they hear on the other end is either going to ease that fear or confirm it. I chose, every single time, to ease it.

I grew up watching my father's receptionist Marilyn do this in her bones. She remembered not just the patient's name but the names of their spouse, their dog, their grandchildren. She made everyone feel, from the moment they stepped inside, that this was a place they were welcome — not just processed. I studied her at thirteen years old, and I carried what she showed me into every office I ever ran.

The front desk is not where you wait for patients to arrive. It is where the practice wins or loses them, one phone call and one hello at a time.

[NARRATOR: Mark]

And once they're in the room, four words change everything: "What brings you in today?"

Not "Are you interested in hearing aids?" Just those four words, which tell the person across from me one thing: I am here to listen before I do anything else.

Nobody comes in to buy a hearing aid. I mean that literally. In twenty-three years of fitting over ten thousand people, I cannot think of a single patient who said "I've decided I want a hearing aid and I'm ready to purchase today." Every person who comes in is there for a more complicated reason, and my job is to find out what that reason actually is.

After they answer, I repeat it back. "Your wife thinks you have a hearing problem." And then I stop. They fill the silence. They tell me more. And everything they tell me I can use — not to manipulate them, but to understand where they are.

If they say "I don't really think I have a problem," then I already know — before we've gotten to the test booth — that we have a denial situation on our hands. I'm already mapping how to approach the rest of the appointment, and I haven't even picked up an audiogram form yet.

The takeaway: surface objections early, when they're conversations. If you save them for the close, they become walls.

## PART IV — UNDERSTANDING THE PERSON

*Chapters 7, 8 & 9 — Mark leads, with Kim*

[NARRATOR: Mark]

Every time a person walked out of my office without a hearing aid, they gave me one of four reasons: cost, vanity, stigma, or denial. They almost always led with cost.

For a long time, I believed them. I spent energy working the price angle. But more often than not I could feel that something else was going on — something they weren't saying out loud.

Then one day it clicked. I was in an appointment with a man who came in wearing a good watch, drove a nice car. He told me cost was the issue. And I sat there thinking: this man is not worried about four thousand dollars. So what is he really telling me?

He was in denial.

Here's what I learned, and I want you to write it down in your mind: flip the list. The order patients give you is backward. They say cost is number one. But the real order — what's actually stopping them — runs in reverse. Denial is number one. Stigma is number two. Vanity is number three. Cost is last.

Cost is the polite exit. The socially acceptable reason to get up and walk out. Nobody wants to sit across from you and say, "I'm terrified of looking old." So they say it costs too much — and if you haven't understood what's actually happening, you let them leave.

Duke University did a survey on the most embarrassing products people associate with aging. Hearing aids came in at number one — above adult diapers, above canes, above walkers. The gap between first and second place was significant. Think about what we're up against. And think about the irony: asking "What?" every five minutes at a restaurant is far more visible than a discreet hearing aid. But logic doesn't move people who are in denial and stigma. Trust does.

[NARRATOR: Mark]

Here's the framework that changed how I worked with everyone: Thinkers, Knowers, and Feelers.

Roughly fifteen percent of the people in your chair are Thinkers — they walk in with a folder. Information downloaded from the internet, quotes from other providers, a list of questions. They make decisions with data.

Roughly thirty percent are Knowers — they take in some information and just know. Gut decision. Give them the three most important things and stop. If you keep piling on, you lose them.

And roughly fifty-five to sixty percent are Feelers. More than half. Feelers make decisions with their hearts. They don't want technical specifications — they want a story. Tell them what better hearing is going to feel like, what it's going to do for their life. "Picture your grandson calling to you from across the backyard. Right now, you're missing that moment. With these, you hear it."

I had an engineer once who started telling me he could probably build a hearing aid in his backyard for fifty dollars. I had no good answer. I hadn't recognized his modality yet, hadn't learned to redirect to the outcome rather than the engineering. I am fairly certain that man did not buy that day.

A master communicator is not someone with one great pitch. A master communicator reads the person across from them and adapts — every time, to every patient, in the language they can actually receive.

[NARRATOR: Kim]

And then there is the testing booth — which was, for me, the highlight of my day.

A lot of people in this business will tell you testing is the boring part. I never saw it that way. Not once.

I always started with the same phrase before I began: "Don't shoot the messenger." I said it with a smile. I told every patient I was hoping — genuinely hoping — that they did not have a hearing loss. I wanted to be honest from the very first moment that I was on their side, not looking for a reason to sell them something.

And I started giving out Hershey bars. I know that sounds like a small thing. But I cannot tell you how many times that one gesture cracked open a conversation that would not have happened otherwise.

One gentleman looked at the bar and said, "I sure wish my brother was here."

I said, "Why is that?"

He said, "I had a twin brother. He passed away. We used to share our Hershey bar together."

I did not have words. I did not need words. He told me about his brother, about what they were like growing up, about what sharing that chocolate meant to them as boys. Some patients shared their bar with the spouse who came along. Some saved it for a grandchild. Every person who looked at that bar had a story.

The takeaway from Part Four: understand the real objection before you try to solve it. Read the person's modality before you open your mouth. And treat the testing booth like the beginning of a relationship — because it is.

## PART V — THE FAMILY IN THE ROOM

*Chapter 10 — Kim leads, with Mark*

[NARRATOR: Kim]

There is a moment I've witnessed more times than I can count, and it never gets old.

A family member comes in not entirely convinced. Maybe they dragged Mom in, or they're quietly skeptical that any of this is worth the money or the fuss. And then something shifts.

The technique I used most was simple. When a patient came in with a spouse or adult child, I'd have the patient turn their back to the door and close their eyes. Then I'd take the loved one just outside the room and ask them to start talking. Just normal conversation — where do you want to go for lunch? What did you think about that show last night? And the patient would answer. Naturally. Without straining or asking for a repeat.

And I always watched the loved one's face.

I think of one daughter in particular. She came in from out of town, and she did not walk in — she stormed in. She had made up her mind before she ever opened the door: her mother didn't need hearing aids, this was an unnecessary expense, and she was taking those devices back today.

I listened. I let her say everything she needed to say. And then I said, "Let me show you something." I brought them into a room, had the mother close her eyes, took the daughter just outside, and asked her to ask her mother some questions. Real ones. The kind a daughter asks her mom.

Her mother answered every single one.

When we came back into the room, the daughter was in tears. Not polite tears — real ones. She looked at her mother and said, "Okay, Mom, you really do need these hearing aids, because you're hearing amazing."

That is the loved-one demo. Not to prove anything, not to win an argument. Just to make visible what was already there. To let the family see what we could see.

The takeaway: the family member is not an obstacle. They are often your greatest ally — if you bring them in properly. What a loved one witnesses firsthand will do more than any clinical explanation to confirm the decision.

## PART VI — THE HONEST CLOSE

*Chapters 11 & 12 — Mark leads, with Kim*

[NARRATOR: Mark]

I want to be direct about something that took me years to really internalize, even though I was living it from the beginning: the hearing aid is not the product. I am the product. You are the product. The device is just the tool we use.

When someone walks through the door, they are not there to buy a hearing aid. They want to hear better. They do not want to own a device. And those are two completely different things.

What they are actually buying is you. Your expertise. Your willingness to stay with them through the month it takes for the brain to rewire. Your commitment to keep working with them until we get this right, with whatever device is necessary.

Here is what I used to tell people about cost: not doing anything is also a cost. The price of untreated hearing loss over years — the quality of relationships lost, the quiet withdrawal from social situations, the cognitive load of constant strain — that is real. Today is a better day to address a hearing loss than next year. The auditory system you have right now is more intact than it will be twelve months from now. That is not a pressure tactic. It is just true.

One price — for the device plus my expertise. That is the bundle. It is not separable. If you can get it cheaper somewhere else, you are not getting me.

When a hearing aid came back, I looked at it as a message. They were not returning the device. They were telling me that something in our relationship had not held. A return is a chance to prove you meant what you said.

[NARRATOR: Kim]

And honesty — that is the foundation of everything.

I knew a secret. I knew hearing aids work. The patients sitting across from me didn't know that yet. They were skeptical, sometimes afraid, sometimes ready to give up after two hard weeks. My job was to hold the line — not by pushing them, but by telling them the truth clearly enough that they trusted me through the hard part.

I'll tell you about a woman who came in after being in a car accident. She was forty-two years old — young for our typical patient — and her hearing had been taken from her suddenly. After a couple of visits she sat down and I could tell she was about to talk herself into returning.

But before she could, she said: "I want to tell you something first. I decided I'm keeping them."

I said, "Tell me why."

She said, "I didn't know there were birds in my backyard." She smiled. "And I didn't know that when you open a can of soda it makes a popping sound. My husband thought it was the funniest thing. I kept popping cans just to hear it."

And then she said the thing I have never forgotten: "I decided I need to start being among the living again."

She was forty-two years old. Among the living.

That is what is on the other side of the hard first month. That is what I held onto every time I sat with someone who was ready to quit. I knew. My job was to be honest enough — and warm enough — to hold the line until they found it too.

The takeaway from Part Six: you are not selling a hearing aid. You are selling your commitment to making it work. And honesty — even when it costs you — is the only thing that builds the kind of trust that keeps people in the journey.

## PART VII — THE JOURNEY AND COMING BACK

*Chapters 13, 14 & 15 — Mark leads, with Kim*

[NARRATOR: Mark]

The sale is just the beginning. The moment a patient walks out with hearing devices in hand, the real work starts. And across multiple offices, that work had to happen the same way every time — whether I was in the room or not.

At delivery, I'd pull out what we called the modified COSI — three specific listening goals the patient named themselves. Not ten goals, not "hearing in general." Three. Written down by the patient, signed, in order of priority.

I had a patient who came back after a few weeks and said the hearing aids weren't working. By every objective measure she was doing well. I asked what she'd bought them for.

She said, "I bought them to hear my PBS program. It's a British show I watch every night."

I sat with that for a moment. A British accent. If I had asked that question at delivery — what specifically do you most want to hear? — we would have had a real conversation about what she could reasonably expect. We could have adjusted for her specific goal. Maybe she would have stayed in the journey rather than walking out disappointed.

That one patient changed how we did every delivery from that point forward.

Three goals. The patient's own words, in their own handwriting. When they came back for follow-up, I wasn't asking a vague "How are the hearing aids?" I was pulling out those three signed goals and asking: "Your wife across the dinner table — better, much better, slightly better, no different, or worse?" We were measuring outcomes against a contract we had made together.

[NARRATOR: Kim]

And when someone came back with a white envelope — we always put everything together for a patient at delivery in a white envelope, so when they arrived for a follow-up holding it, we knew before they sat down that they were coming to return — that was my moment.

The most important thing I learned about saving a return: find the one thing. Patients will pour everything out when they're frustrated. But buried somewhere in all of it is the real issue driving them toward the door. Your job is to listen through all of it until you identify what that one thing is. Then you fix that one thing.

The forty-two-year-old woman I mentioned — she came back at one point still unconvinced about staying. Before I even had a chance to ask, she told me about the birds. About the soda can. About deciding she needed to be among the living again.

Some returns didn't save. I would not pretend otherwise. But if you lead with caring, if you listen before you explain, if you treat each person as if you might be the only person they talk to all week — you give the journey its best possible chance.

[NARRATOR: Kim]

People ask me what kept patients coming back year after year. My honest answer: someone listened to them.

I have the gift of gab. Always have. And that curiosity — genuine curiosity about where people came from, what they had done, what they cared about — was something patients could feel the moment they walked through the door.

We had a retired military officer I always called the Colonel. He came in for years, with stories about his service and the places he'd been. I honored that. If I found out someone had served, they became their rank to me. We had so many veterans come through our doors, and I loved every one of them.

We always had coffee and donuts in the lobby. Not a promotion — just there, every day. People would come in just to sit. Not for an appointment. Just to be somewhere warm, with someone who was glad to see them.

You might be the only person that patient talks to all week. When the person who represents better hearing and better connection is also the kind of person who genuinely asks how the grandkids are — and actually listens to the answer — that person becomes indispensable to a patient's life. They don't just come back for their hearing aids. They come back for you.

The takeaway from Part Seven: the journey is a process, not a switch. Three goals, written down, measured at every visit. Find the one real issue when someone wants to return. And listen — really listen — to every story, because patients come back to where their stories are known.

## PART VIII — RUNNING A CARING PRACTICE

*Chapters 16 & 17 — Kim leads, with Mark*

[NARRATOR: Kim]

Running a practice across multiple offices means you are almost never the person at the front desk when a patient walks in. Whoever you hired is.

So over the years, I started asking a question in interviews that I know surprised a lot of people. It had nothing to do with whether they'd worked in healthcare before or knew how to operate a scheduling system. Instead, I'd ask: "What was your relationship with your parents and grandparents?"

And then I'd sit and listen.

If they lit up — if they told me how they used to drive their grandmother to appointments, how Sunday dinners at their grandparents' house were the best part of the week — I knew something important. I knew they had been shaped around older people. They'd be comfortable sitting with a 78-year-old man who wanted to tell them about his time in Korea before he let you put him in the testing booth. They would not rush him.

If the answer was cold — estrangement, indifference, no real memories of time spent with older family members — I knew they were not suited for this work. Not because they were bad people. But because kindness toward the elderly is something you have to have absorbed somewhere along the way. You cannot train it from scratch in a three-day orientation.

That one question told me more than a full page of references ever could.

The other thing I cared about — and I will not apologize for how particular I was — was clean bathrooms. When a patient comes in for the first time, they are already nervous. They have spent months quietly struggling. The last thing they should encounter is a waiting room that feels like nobody cares. Everything communicates. Everything.

I would never ask any staff member to do something I was not willing to do myself. That attitude sets the standard. People rise to what you model.

[NARRATOR: Kim]

And here is the simplest piece of advice I ever gave about the work itself:

Treat each patient the way you would treat your parents or your grandparents.

If you had an older parent who was struggling to hear — getting left out of family conversations, turning up the television, nodding along in doctor's appointments and pretending to understand when they'd missed half of what was said — you would not walk away. You would sit down. You would explain things slowly. You would call to check in. You would hold their hand through the scary part.

That is the posture. If you bring that posture into every patient interaction, you will never be pushy. Pushy comes from wanting the outcome for yourself more than you want it for the patient. When your focus is entirely on their well-being, there is no pressure. There is just care. Patients feel the difference immediately.

People only want to know that they matter to you in this world. When they realize that, they relax enough to keep going. They trust the process. They stay.

[NARRATOR: Mark]

And from my side of the chair: people don't want to be sold. But they very much want to buy. The difference is who the decision belongs to. When you walk in genuinely curious about someone's situation — when your questions are real questions, when their answers actually change what you do next — the patient feels like the decision is coming from them.

That is the whole game. You win not by pressing harder, but by making yourself someone worth trusting.

The takeaway from Part Eight: hire for heart — everything else can be taught. And never think of what you're doing as selling. You are caring. The outcome follows from that.

## PART IX — WHAT WE HOPE YOU REMEMBER

*Chapter 18 — Kim leads, with Mark*

[NARRATOR: Kim]

There is a woman I think about often.

She came into our office about a month before her granddaughter's wedding. She had just started wearing hearing aids for the first time, and she was terrified — not of the hearing aids, but of missing the wedding. The vows, the music, the laughter, the toasts. She had watched her hearing slip away over the years, and now one of the most important days in her family's life was coming, and she was afraid she'd be sitting in the front row hearing nothing but muffled sound and silence.

We started working together right away. Wear them six hours a day, then eight, then build up to a full day. Give your brain the time it needs. It takes about a month for the brain to begin rewiring itself to process all that new sound. We had about a month before the wedding. It was going to be close.

She came in for every appointment. She practiced. She stuck with it even when everything sounded too loud at first, even when her own voice sounded strange to her. I gave her gold stickers when she made progress. She would chuckle. Then she would beam.

She earned a lot of gold stickers that month.

Two weeks after the wedding, she came back in. And the moment she walked through the door, I could see it on her face before she said a single word.

She sat down and said: "Kim, I heard the whole wedding. I heard the reception. I heard everything." Then she looked at me and said, "I just want to tell you thank you very much for giving me that gift — that I could hear my granddaughter and her wedding."

I have been in this work for a long time. I have helped thousands of people. And still, in that moment, I had to take a breath.

That is what this is. That is the whole thing. Everything we do — the testing, the follow-ups, the patient journey, the honest conversations, the gold stickers, the clean waiting rooms and the warm coffee and the Hershey bars and the careful fitting and the long phone calls with nervous first-time wearers — all of it exists in service of moments like that one.

[NARRATOR: Mark]

I want to tell you why I stayed.

Twenty-three years. Over ten thousand people fitted with hearing aids. More than thirty thousand tested. One of the largest privately owned hearing aid practices in the country, built across California and Florida.

People look at those numbers and assume the answer to "why did you stay" must be something about business, about building, about the satisfaction of growing something.

Those things were real. But none of that is why I stayed.

I stayed because of a woman who walked back through my office door three days after I fitted her with her first hearing aid and started calling my name down the hallway. I was new. I was twenty-something and consciously aware of everything I did not yet know. And this woman — probably seventy-five years old, and so gentle about it — found me and threw her arms around me and started crying.

"You have changed my life," she said. "The rain — it rained yesterday, and I just sat and bawled. I heard the rain on the roof. And I didn't even know I missed that."

Then she told me about the leaves blowing through the trees. The birds when the sun came out. The sounds she had not heard in years without knowing they were gone.

From that day forward, what I was doing did not feel like a business anymore. It felt like the same feeling you get when you give a gift — a real gift, one you chose because you knew it was exactly what that person needed — and you watch their face when they open it.

That joy of giving is, to me, one of the highest things. Hearing is one of the most important senses we have. And hearing aids — traditional hearing aids, fitted carefully, followed up on honestly, supported through the hard first month — are the mechanism through which you can give that gift, over and over again, to people who had no idea what they were missing until it came back to them.

That is why I stayed for twenty-three years. Not the revenue. Not the growth. The gifts. One at a time, every single day.
