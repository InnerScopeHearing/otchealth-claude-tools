# The Scripts Library
## *From the Chair* — Complete Patient-Journey Script Bank
### Mark & Kim Moore, Licensed Hearing Aid Dispensers

---

> **How to use this bank:** Scripts are organized in patient-journey order — from the first phone call through recall and referral. Each entry gives you the situation, the line as Mark or Kim actually used it, and a blank line to adapt it in your own voice. Where wording differs by audience, both versions appear:
> **Closer:** sales-framed phrasing · **Professional:** care-framed phrasing.
> Most lines are shared — only the money- and decision-sensitive moments carry the dual label.

## PART ONE: FIRST CONTACT

---

### Script 1 — The Smile You Can Hear (Answering the Phone)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** Every inbound call, every time. The person calling has been hesitating, possibly for months. What they hear in the first three seconds determines whether they stay on the line.

> "When you smile, the person on the other end of the line can hear it."

**The line:**
"[Practice name], this is [your name] — we're so glad you called. How can I help you today?"

*Delivery note from Kim: Put a mirror above the phone. Look at yourself before you pick up. You are answering from a smile, not from a task.*

### Script 2 — Validating the First Step (Closing the Appointment)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** At the end of the first phone call, just before hanging up. The caller has agreed to an appointment. Send them off feeling good about their decision.

> "The first step toward better hearing was walking in the door."

**The line:**
"You made a great decision calling us today. We are going to take good care of you. We will see you [date/time] — and if you have any questions before then, please do not hesitate to call back."

### Script 3 — The Warm-Room Welcome (Patient Arrives)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** The moment a patient walks through the door — especially a first-time visitor.

> "Everyone puts their pants on the same way as everyone else. No matter what walk of life they come from, everyone deserves kindness and respect."

**The line:**
"Good morning — welcome in. I'm so glad you found us. Can I get you a coffee or some water while you get comfortable? Take your time with the paperwork — I'm right here if you have any questions."

*Delivery note from Kim: Use their name on every return visit. If the patient mentioned a family event, a pet, or a trip on the last visit, pick it back up. "How did the graduation go?" That is not small talk. That is the relationship.*

### Script 4 — Pre-Appointment Intel Pass (Front Desk to Dispenser)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** Just before the dispenser enters the room. The front desk has already learned something useful — who came with the patient, what the patient mentioned in the waiting room, whether the mood is nervous, skeptical, or ready.

**The line:**
"He came in with his wife. She did most of the talking getting him here. He seems a little resistant — I'd start with her."

## PART TWO: THE OPENING CONVERSATION

---

### Script 5 — The Opener
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** The very first thing out of your mouth after introductions, before any testing, before any clinical explanation.

> "I don't wanna assume that they're here to buy a hearing aid — because that's where you're gonna get into problems. No one wants to buy. They don't wanna be sold, and they don't wanna buy. However, they wanna be helped."

**The line:**
"What brings you in today?"

*Then stop. Let them answer. Do not fill the silence.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 6 — The Repeat-Back
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** Immediately after the patient answers the opener. Repeat back exactly what they said — not a paraphrase, their actual words.

> "If a person tells you something, they believe it is important. And if they believe it is important, the least you can do is repeat it back so they know you heard it."

**The patient says:** "I'm here because my wife thinks I have a hearing problem."

**You say:** "Your wife thinks you have a hearing problem."

*Then stop again. They will continue. Everything they add tells you where the appointment needs to go.*

**Pattern for any answer:**
Repeat the key phrase. Pause. They fill the silence. Repeat back the new layer.

"So — you're not sure you have a problem, but she's concerned."
*(Now you know: this is a denial situation.)*

My version: __________

---

### Script 7 — The Implicit Agreement (Early in the History)
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** During the intake conversation, before testing. Not a closing line — a quiet, low-pressure agreement that shifts the whole room.

> "Would you be willing to consider doing something about it, if we find we can help you?"

**The line:**
"If we find something today, and we can help you, would you be willing to consider doing something about it?"

*Delivery note from Mark: Most people say yes. That is a reasonable thing to agree to. You now have a shared understanding — not a commitment to purchase, just an agreement that they came to get help and they are open to it. This single question, asked early and without pressure, changes more conversations than any closing technique.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 8 — Surfacing Past Experience (History-Taking)
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** During the history, before testing. Pulls out objections while pressure is low.

**The lines:**
"Have you ever tried hearing aids before? — And how did that go? What did you like, what did you not like?"

"Do you know any friends or family members who have hearing aids? What have you heard from them?"

*Delivery note from Mark: The patient who says his buddy threw hearing aids in a drawer is telling you his specific fear — wasted money and wasted effort. The patient who says she has wanted to do this for years but wasn't sure anyone could really help needs to believe in you before she will believe in any device. Bring the real concern to the surface now, while it is a conversation, not at the close when it becomes a wall.*

## PART THREE: THE TESTING CHAIR

---

### Script 9 — "Don't Shoot the Messenger" (Pre-Test)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** Before starting any hearing test, every time.

> "To me, it was the highlight of my day — between working the front desk and testing, both were my highlights."

**The line:**
"Before we start, I want you to know — I am always hoping that you come in here and have perfect hearing. I would love to tell every single person: you are fine, come back in a year. Whatever the test shows, I will tell you the truth about it, and we will figure out together what to do next. So — don't shoot the messenger."

*(Said with a warm smile.)*

My version: __________

---

### Script 10 — Honoring the Courage (After the Test)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** When sitting back down with the patient to review results — especially if the results show significant loss.

> "Taking the test was the second step toward better hearing. The first step was walking in the door."

**The line:**
"I just want to say — coming in today took courage. A lot of people put this off for years. The fact that you're here matters, and I want you to know that."

*Then move into the audiogram explanation.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 11 — Reading the Audiogram (Explaining Results)
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** After testing, when showing the patient what the graph means.

**The line:**
"Here is what we found, and here is why it explains exactly what you have been experiencing. High-frequency loss means you are hearing the vowels but losing the consonants — the S's, the F's, the TH sounds. You can hear someone is talking, but the words blur together. You are not imagining it. The audiogram shows us exactly what is going on."

## PART FOUR: THE FOUR OBJECTIONS

---

### Script 12 — Responding to Denial
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** When the patient insists there is no real problem. Do not argue. Let the evidence do the work.

> "They throw up cost as being the biggest thing — because they're in denial."

**The line:**
"You're not sure you have a problem. Okay. Let's do this: let's run the test together and let the results speak. If there is nothing to address, I will be the first to tell you to come back in a year. And if we do find something, I will show you exactly what it is, in plain terms, and we will talk about what it means."

*(After the test, let the audiogram speak. You do not have to argue denial — reality argues it for you.)*

My version: __________

---

### Script 13 — Responding to Stigma
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** When the patient expresses reluctance tied to the image of hearing aids — "that's for old people," "I don't want something hanging on my ear."

> "Asking 'What?' every five minutes at a restaurant is far more visible than a discreet hearing aid."

**The line:**
"I understand that. The image most people have — the banana-shaped hearing aid from the 1960s, squealing in the ear — it is thirty years out of date. Today's devices are small, discreet, and many of them sit entirely in the canal. But here is the honest thing: asking 'What?' at every dinner, missing the punchline of every joke, turning the TV up until everyone else in the room is uncomfortable — that draws more attention than any hearing aid ever would. You are actually more visible with untreated hearing loss than you would be with one of these."

My version: __________

---

### Script 14 — Responding to Vanity (Especially with Men)
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** When a male patient is resistant and the issue is not cost but self-image.

> "Men do not want to feel old. They do not want the visible signal of a limitation."

**The line:**
"I hear you — and I want to be straight with you about something. In my experience, men who come in on their own are often looking for confirmation that they don't need anything. I respect that. What I'd ask is this: let's let the test settle it. If the numbers say you're fine, I'll be happy to send you home. If they say something different, I'll show you what that means for the specific situations you've told me about — and you can decide what to do with that information."

My version: __________

---

### Script 15 — Responding to the "It's Too Expensive" Objection
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** When cost is raised as the primary objection. First check: is this really about cost, or is cost standing in for denial, stigma, or vanity? If cost is the genuine final layer, use both of the following.

> "I also tell people this: the price you pay includes me — my 23 years, my 10,000-plus fittings, my willingness to keep working with you until we get this right."

**Closer:**
"I understand the price is a real number. But let me be direct: not acting is also a cost. It is costing you every dinner-table conversation that goes by, every grandkid's story you miss, every situation you've been quietly withdrawing from because it is too hard to follow along. The auditory system you have right now is more intact than it will be next year. Today is the best day to start. And I want you to know — what you are paying for is not a device. It is me. My commitment to working with you until we get this right. That is not separable from the price."

**Professional:**
"The price is a real consideration, and I want to honor that. What I'd ask you to weigh is this: the longer untreated hearing loss goes on, the harder the adaptation becomes. The brain stops processing signals it is not receiving, and over time, that gets harder to reverse. We can do more with what you have now than we will be able to do a year from now. When cost is genuinely a barrier, we have financing available that can bridge the gap — but I want to make sure we are talking about the right thing first."

My version: __________

### Script 16 — Financing as a Bridge
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** After the value has been established, when cost remains a practical barrier. Never lead with this — it puts the emphasis on price rather than value.

**The line:**
"If the number is a barrier today, we have a way to bridge it. We can break it into payments that work for most people's budgets. The goal is to make sure cost is not the thing standing between you and better hearing."

## PART FIVE: MODALITY-MATCHED COMMUNICATION

---

### Script 17 — Speaking to a Thinker
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** The patient brought a folder. They have questions, research, comparison quotes. They are a data-first decision-maker (roughly 15% of patients).

> "You cannot give them too much information — or so it seems. The truth is, a Thinker can never accumulate quite enough data, because no dataset ever feels fully complete."

**The redirect line (when data-comparison is going nowhere):**
"I appreciate how thoroughly you have researched this. Let me ask you the question that actually matters here: if you built one yourself — or ordered something else online — would it actually help you hear? What I can offer you is not just the device. It is 23 years of fitting, and my commitment to staying with you until we have this right for your specific hearing profile."

*Delivery note from Mark: Do not try to win the technical comparison — you will not. Redirect to the outcome. Meet the Thinker in their logical frame, then steer the logic toward what matters.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 18 — Speaking to a Knower
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** The patient is decisive, gets to the point quickly, does not need a full clinical explanation. Gut-decision maker (roughly 30% of patients).

> "Give them the highlights. Give them the three most important things you need them to understand, and stop."

**The frame:**
"Here are the three things that matter for your situation. One: [specific result]. Two: [specific option]. Three: [specific next step]. That's it. I don't need to bury you in everything I know — just the things that apply to you."

*Then stop. Let their gut do its work. Do not pile on more information.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 19 — Speaking to a Feeler
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** The patient is talking about relationships, loss, family moments — they are leading with emotion, not data. This is your majority: roughly 55–60% of patients.

> "Feelers do not care about the technical specifications. What they want is a story."

**The line:**
"Picture your grandson in the backyard, calling to you. 'Grandma, look at me, look at me.' Right now, you're missing that. He runs over and tugs your sleeve because you didn't hear. With these, he can call from across the yard and you hear it. You don't miss that moment."

*Or adapt to their specific situation — use what they told you in the history.*

*Delivery note from Mark: Find the movie of their life, rewritten with better hearing. Make it specific to their people and their situation — not a generic benefit, their story.*

## PART SIX: THE LOVED-ONE DEMO

---

### Script 20 — Setting Up the Loved-One Demo
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** When a family member — spouse, adult child — is in the room and is skeptical, resistant, or has come in to demand a return. Also useful when the patient's own denial is still active and a third-party witness would help.

> "When that patient answered back and kept on talking to their loved one, they had a smile on their face. I knew right then that I had that loved one understanding what a hearing aid can do."

**The setup line (to the family member):**
"I want to show you something before we talk about anything else. It will just take a minute. I'm going to ask [patient's name] to close their eyes and turn their back to the door. I'd like you to step just outside and ask them a few normal questions — where you want to go to lunch, what you did this week, anything that comes to mind. Just a real conversation. Don't say anything out of the ordinary — I want you to see what happens."

**To the patient:**
"I'd like you to close your eyes and turn your back to the door for just a minute. Your [spouse/daughter/son] is going to ask you a few questions. Just answer naturally."

*Then watch the family member's face when the patient answers clearly and easily.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 21 — After the Demo (The Third-Party Close)
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** After the loved-one demo lands — the family member is now on your side. Let them speak. Do not interrupt.

**The line (to the family member):**
"What did you notice?"

*Then stop. Let the family member answer. In many cases they will do more to close the room than anything you could say.*

**If the dispenser needs to add:**
"What you just heard — that is what better hearing looks like. Not perfect, not twenty-five years old again, but present. In the conversation. Not missing the question. That is what we are working toward."

**Closer:**
"Two voices in agreement that it's time beats any presentation I can give. If you're both ready to move forward, let's talk about what that looks like."

**Professional:**
"The whole family benefits from better hearing — not just the person wearing the devices. What you just saw is what it means to give that back."

## PART SEVEN: THE PRICE CONVERSATION AND DELIVERY

---

### Script 22 — One Price Includes Me
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** When presenting pricing — before any negotiation, as a statement of what the investment actually covers.

> "If a person returns a hearing aid, they're not returning a hearing aid, they're returning you."

**Closer:**
"Here is how I think about the price: you are not buying a device off a shelf. You are buying me. My 23 years, my 10,000-plus fittings, my commitment to staying with you through the whole process — the week your own voice sounds strange, the month when background noise is overwhelming, the follow-up visits where we dial things in. One price. That includes all of it. If you go somewhere else, you may find a lower number on the device. What you will not find is what I'm committing to deliver. I can only speak for myself."

**Professional:**
"The price you see is the full commitment: the devices, the fitting, the adaptation support, every follow-up visit through the journey. There is no separate charge for the relationship. When I fit you with hearing devices, I'm in this with you — through the hard first month, through every adjustment, until we get it right."

My version: __________

---

### Script 23 — Setting the COSI Goals at Delivery
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** At the moment of delivery, after fitting, before the patient walks out.

> "If you were going to grade these hearing aids, what three situations matter most to you? Not hearing in general. Specific. Where do you want to hear? What program? What voice? What room?"

**The line:**
"Before you leave today, I want us to write down three things together. Not everything — just three specific situations you most want to hear better. When your wife talks to you at dinner. The baseball game on Sunday. Your grandson's voice. Whatever they are for you. We are going to write them down, you are going to sign off on them, and every time you come back, that is how we will measure whether we are doing our job. We call it grading ourselves."

*(Pause for their three.)*

"And one more thing: put them in priority order. What matters most?"

## PART EIGHT: ADAPTATION — THE FIRST MONTH

---

### Script 24 — "You Hear With Your Brain" (Setting Expectations at Delivery)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** At every delivery, without exception. The single most important thing to say before a new hearing aid wearer leaves your office.

> "It takes about a month to create a habit for the brain because you hear with your brain and not your ears — and believe me, the brain loves a quiet world."

**The line:**
"I want to tell you something that most people are never told, and I want you to hear it now so that the next thirty days make sense. You do not hear with your ears. You hear with your brain. The ears collect the sound, but the understanding — making sense of what you are hearing — happens in your brain. And when you have had hearing loss for a while, your brain quietly adapted to the quiet. It stopped expecting signals that were not coming. So when we restore the sound, the brain has to relearn. That takes about a month. You are going to hear things that feel too loud, or strange, or different than you expect. Your own voice is going to sound like you are talking inside a barrel for a little while. That is normal. That is your brain waking up. That is exactly the right thing happening."

My version: __________

---

### Script 25 — The Week-One Assignment
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** Giving instructions at delivery, or following up at the week-one check-in.

> "The brain has not been receiving the full signal, the synapses have quieted down, and when amplification arrives, those pathways need to be rebuilt."

**The line:**
"This week, I want you to read out loud for about twenty minutes a day. To your spouse, to a pet, to an empty room — it does not matter. You are going to hate the sound of your own voice at first. Do it anyway. That exercise is specifically designed to help your brain adjust to what you sound like with hearing devices. It is the single most common reason people return hearing aids in the first two weeks — they never got used to their own voice. We are going to work right through that."

My version: __________

---

### Script 26 — The Week-Two Check-In
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** Week-two follow-up call or visit. This is the most important check-in in the entire process — more patients give up in weeks two and three than at any other point.

> "Your script for week two is not a service call — it is the most important conversation in the entire relationship."

**The line:**
"I know it feels hard right now. I know your voice sounds strange. I know everything sounds too loud. That is your brain waking up. That is the right thing happening. Stick with me. Think of it like taking a cast off an arm — the muscles have been still for a long time, and you cannot just pick something up. You need physical therapy. That is what we are doing right now. You are in the middle of the physical therapy. We are not done yet."

My version: __________

---

### Script 27 — The Turning-Point Reminder (Encouraging Patience)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** Any time a patient is frustrated and considering giving up — week two, three, or four.

> "When they come in and would like to tell me that they have heard their grandchildren, their loved ones, they've heard the birds for the first time — I know that they're on their way to success."

**The line:**
"I want to tell you something I know that you do not know yet: I know the hearing aids work. I have seen it hundreds and hundreds of times. You do not know that yet because you are in the middle of the hard part. But I have seen what is on the other side of this, and it is real — the moment your grandson's voice comes through clearly across the yard, the moment you hear a bird you had forgotten was there. That moment is coming. I am asking you to trust me and stay in the journey a little longer."

## PART NINE: SAVING THE RETURN

---

### Script 28 — The White-Envelope Moment (Opening the Return Conversation)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** A patient arrives for a follow-up visit holding the white delivery envelope. They are preparing to return the hearing aids.

> "Once you have figured out the most important issue to them at that moment, then you are able to fix the problem so that they can stay on that journey."

**The line:**
"I can see you brought everything back in. I want to hear everything. Tell me what's been going on — what has bothered you most? I'm not going to rush you, and I'm not going to argue with you. I just want to understand."

*Then listen. Do not defend. Do not explain the fitting rationale. Do not cite the audiogram. Listen until you find the one thing — the single issue that is actually driving them toward the door.*

My version: __________

---

### Script 29 — Finding the One Issue (Return Save)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** During the return-save conversation, once the patient has told you their list of concerns.

> "Buried somewhere in all of that is the real thing, the one issue that is actually driving them toward the door. Your job is to listen through all of it until you can identify what that one thing is. Then you fix that one thing."

**The line:**
"I hear everything you've said, and each concern is real and worth taking seriously. Let me ask you this: if we could fix just one thing today — the one that is bothering you most right now — what would that be?"

*(Fix that one thing. Schedule a follow-up within a week.)*

My version: __________

---

### Script 30 — The Honest Hold (When the Patient Wants to Quit)
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** When the patient is frustrated and you need to give them an honest reason to stay — not a sales reason, an honest one.

> "I know a secret. I know the hearing aids work. These people don't know that."

**The line:**
"I am going to be straight with you, because I think you deserve that: yes, you do need these hearing aids. I know that is not what you want to hear right now. I know you are in the hard part. What I want to ask of you — because I have seen the other side of this more times than I can count — is one more week. One more week of the exercises, one more visit, and then we will reassess together. If you still want to return at that point, I will honor that. But I am not willing to let you go yet without telling you honestly what I see."

## PART TEN: RECALL AND REFERRAL

---

### Script 31 — The Recall Ask (Scheduling the Next Visit)
*(Mark and Kim)*

**When to use it:** At the close of every follow-up visit — when the patient is doing well and the COSI goals have been met.

**The line:**
"I want to see you back here in [three months / six months / at the next scheduled interval] for a cleaning and a check-in. Your hearing profile can shift over time, and we want to stay ahead of any changes. [Receptionist's name] will get you scheduled before you head out."

*(On a successful-outcome visit, this is also the natural moment for the referral ask.)*

My version: __________

---

### Script 32 — The Natural Referral Ask (After COSI Completion)
*(Mark)*

**When to use it:** At the moment a patient signs their COSI completion form — when outcomes have been documented and the patient has acknowledged they are doing well.

> "You know, if you have friends wearing hearing aids who are not doing as well as you are right now, they may not have been through a program like this. You could help them."

**The line:**
"You just signed off on hitting all three of your goals — that is a big deal, and it means a lot to me. I want to ask you something. Do you have a friend or family member who is wearing hearing aids and not getting this kind of result? Or someone who probably needs hearing aids and keeps putting it off? You could help them. Just tell them about your experience — that is the best recommendation they are ever going to hear. And if they want to come in, we will take care of them the same way we took care of you."

My version: __________

---

### Script 33 — Receiving a Referred Patient
*(Kim)*

**When to use it:** When a referred patient calls or walks in. Acknowledge the referral immediately — it honors both the existing patient and the new one.

**The line:**
"We are so glad [referring patient's name] sent you to us. That is the highest compliment someone can give — and it tells me a lot about the kind of care you are looking for. You are in the right place. Let's start at the beginning and make sure we get this right for you."
